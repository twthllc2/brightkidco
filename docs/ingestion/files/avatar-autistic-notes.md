## 2. BEHAVIORAL PATTERNS (10 distinct patterns)

### Pattern 1: Exhaustive Research Behavior
Parents Google at 2am, search Reddit threads, join autism parent Facebook groups, screenshot tips but feel overwhelmed, watch sensory OT content, read academic studies, and ask their pediatrician repeatedly.

> "I can't describe how many times I've gone on Reddit trying to figure out how to potty train them."

### Pattern 2: Avoidance Behaviors
Parents avoid public bathrooms, long outings, sleepovers, and preschool programs with strict potty requirements. They limit their world to avoid embarrassment and accidents.

> "But how can we ever leave the house?"
> "We did stay in for a week and it was absolute hell."

### Pattern 3: Hyper-Vigilant Monitoring
Parents watch child for body cues constantly, remind every 30–60 minutes, count minutes between attempts, and explain autism to strangers defensively.

> "Parent hyper-vigilantly watching body cues."
> "Parent counting the minutes between attempts."

### Pattern 4: Purchasing Multiple Solutions
Parents buy multiple potty types, visual charts, underwear with favorite characters, reward systems, sensory supports, books about autism & toileting. They return products that "didn't work."

> "We bought multiple potty types... returns products that didn't work."

### Pattern 5: Method Cycling
Parents cycle through naked method, reward charts, candy bribes, sticker systems, screen time on toilet, books next to potty, songs (Elmo, Cocomelon), bare bottom, underwear under pull-ups, taking diapers away cold turkey, juice loading, salty snacks, bubbles, running water, potty in every room, OT support, early intervention, ABA, IEP goals, social stories, visual schedules.

> "We tried everything."
> "We've been on round...who knows what of potty training too, and it's brutal."

### Pattern 6: Regression Panic
When progress reverses, parents experience intense anxiety and feel the need to start over or intensify efforts.

> "he was actually starting to use the potty before he was 2, but then he just stopped."
> "We dont know what caused the regression."

### Pattern 7: Demand Avoidance Management (PDA)
Parents learn to avoid direct prompts because they trigger meltdowns. They use indirect language, schedules, and environmental cues instead.

> "If I try prompting her she just freaks out and refuses to sit on the toilet."
> "I don't want to deal with power struggles 7 times a day either."
> "He freaks out the second I suggest the potty."

### Pattern 8: Sensory Sensitivity Navigation
Parents learn their child's sensory profile and adjust — bathroom sounds (flushing, echo), toilet seat texture, clothing sensation, temperature.

> "It sounds like the sensory experience is really dysregulating for him."
> "She says she is scared of daycare potty."
> "He had constant accidents and would have massive meltdowns if we asked him to try and use the toilet at regular intervals."

### Pattern 9: Context-Specific Success (Home vs. Public)
Children may succeed at home but completely fail at school, daycare, or public bathrooms. This creates a false sense of progress.

> "My son is apparently doing great at ABA and is basically potty trained there but at home he screams and won't even go near the potty."
> "My daughter is doing great with potty training at home... but she has anxiety around daycare toileting."
> "He has anxiety of public places and would never go in them."

### Pattern 10: Comparison Grief
Parents compare their child to NT siblings, peers, and younger children who are already trained. This triggers shame and urgency.

> "Other kids younger already trained."
> "What can take a NT child weeks to learn, takes months or years for our kids."

## 11. CORE INSIGHT: BODY SIGNAL TRANSLATION PROBLEM

The most valuable insight from the research:

**The Autistic Parent Avatar does NOT have a potty training problem. They have a BODY SIGNAL TRANSLATION PROBLEM.**

The child doesn't understand:
- Body Signal → Action
- Feeling → Need to go → Go to potty

And the parent doesn't have a tool to bridge this gap. Traditional potty training assumes the child can feel the signal and just needs motivation to act. Autistic children often cannot feel the signal at all (interoception), cannot communicate it (speech delay), or cannot translate it to action (motor planning).

**This is where a product fits:** A Body-Signal Learning Layer that helps the child:
1. Recognize internal body signals
2. Connect signals to the action of going to the potty
3. Build this connection gradually without pressure or trauma
