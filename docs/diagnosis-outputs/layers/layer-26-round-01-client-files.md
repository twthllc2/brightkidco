## 5. WHAT NOT TO SAY TO MEDICAID FAMILIES

### Universal Forbidden (DOC1)
- Never preachy about financial decisions
- Never imply financial hardship is the parent's fault
- Never use charity language ("for families in need")
- Never imply judgment about Medicaid usage

### Level 3 Specific Forbidden (DOC4, Section 26)
- "Stop spending on pull-ups" — Medicaid pays, not her
- Math reframes based on pull-up costs
- Charity language ("for families in need")
- Implied judgment about Medicaid usage
- "Save him from years of diapers" — implies diapers are shameful
- "Free her from pull-ups" — diapers are safety
- "Get out of diapers!" — shouting language
- "Be diaper-free!" — marketing language
- Adult diaper language as sales pressure ("Before he needs adult diapers!")
- "He deserves better" — implies you're failing him
- "Don't let your child miss out" — pressure language
- "Time is precious — start now" — urgency manipulation
- Grief as sales tool ("Don't let it be like the past 9 years")

### Level 2 Specific Forbidden (DOC3, Section 19)
- "Easy" / "Quick" in any form — she knows it's not easy
- "We can train your child in X weeks" — immediate skepticism
- "Don't give up!" cheerleader language — feels intrusive
- "Just trust the process" — feels patronizing
- Comparison with Level 1 stories — frustrates her

### Level 1 Specific Forbidden (DOC2, Section 18)
- "Years of failed attempts" — she has 1-3 attempts, not 5+
- Heavy resignation language — she is not resigned
- Comparisons with Level 2/3 — distances herself

## 6. MEDICAID-SPECIFIC LANGUAGE PATTERNS

### Correct Language for Medicaid Families (DOC4, Section 26)
- "Your pull-ups continue. Add 1-2 hours of underwear daytime when you have capacity."
- "This is one addition, not a replacement."
- "60-day guarantee. If it doesn't fit your budget or your kid, refund — no questions."
- "Continue pull-ups via Medicaid, add daytime trial"

### Lever L3-B: "Add Alongside Specialty Diapers" (DOC4)
- "This isn't about abandoning pull-ups or specialty diapers. It's about adding 1-2 hours per day, at home, when it's manageable. Some families try during play, others during TV time."
- Reduces fear of "complete change"
- Makes it doable
- Respects Medicaid reality

### Pull-Up Lever Caution by Level (DOC1, Lever 2)
- Level 1: Medium effect, she uses pull-ups not as long
- Level 2: High effect, but pull-ups are safety net — "Add alongside" not "abandon"
- Level 3: Medium effect with CAUTION — pull-ups are existential safety, Medicaid often finances them
