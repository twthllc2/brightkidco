## PART 1: THE 6 CROSS-LEVEL CALIBRATION RULES (from Plan Layer 18, lines 359-374)

When writing for General Fallback or any cross-level flow, six rules ensure the copy reaches all levels simultaneously without alienating any single group.

### Rule 1: Symptom Description Over Level Labels
- NEVER: "if your child is Level 2 or Level 3"
- INSTEAD: "if your child does not feel when they need to go"
- Symptoms are cross-level universal. Level labels are backend logic, not content.

### Rule 2: 3-5 Recognition Anchors Per Mail
- Every email must contain a block with at least 3 specific situations so all levels recognize themselves
- Level 1 anchors: "sits 20 minutes on toilet and nothing happens," "sometimes succeeds, sometimes does not"
- Level 2 anchors: "pees immediately when pull-up comes off," "plays while peeing"
- Level 3 anchors: "does not react to wet diaper," "made no progress in 4 years"
- The mom reads the list and finds 1-2 that exactly match her situation

### Rule 3: Age Ranges Instead of Fixed Ages
- NEVER: "for 5-year-olds"
- INSTEAD: "for kids 3 to 10+"

### Rule 4: Verbal and Non-Verbal Inclusivity
- When giving verbalization examples, always include both
- Example: "Look for signals: pulling at their pants, looking down, saying 'wet,' or showing you with a gesture"
- First is verbal, second is non-verbal

### Rule 5: Timeline Flexibility
- NEVER: "in 30 days your child will"
- INSTEAD: "some kids show first signals after 2 weeks. Others need 8 weeks. Both are normal."

### Rule 6: Success Metrics at a Low Bar
- Define "success" as mini-wins, not "fully trained"
- Examples: "looked at the wet spot for the first time," "pulled at their pants in awareness," "walked toward the bathroom even after the accident," "stayed dry 2 hours instead of 30 minutes"

## PART 2: THE "WHETHER...OR...IF..." LANGUAGE PATTERN (Plan Layer 20, lines 391-398)

This is the cross-level copy formula for General Fallback emails. It lets one sentence feel personal to all levels simultaneously by using conditional structures that cover multiple scenarios.

### Pattern Structure
"Whether [Sub-Group A situation], or [Sub-Group C situation] — if [common symptom], here's what most parents miss."

### Examples from the Plan
- "Whether your kid is 3 or 10..." → covers all age ranges
- "Whether you are 3 weeks in or 3 years in..." → covers all experience levels
- "Whether they are verbal or not..." → covers Level 1 through Level 3
- "If they sit on the toilet but nothing happens..." → symptom common to all levels
- "If they pee and keep playing..." → Level 2-3 symptom
- "If they tell you AFTER they have already gone..." → Level 2 symptom

### DOC5 Expanded Examples (Section 23)
- "Whether you're 3 weeks into wondering, or 3 years into trying — if your kid isn't progressing like 'every method' promises they will — here's what most parents miss."
- "Whether your kid is verbal or not, 3 or 10, diagnosed or watching — if the signal isn't arriving, behavior-training alone won't bridge the gap."
- "Whether you've tried 2 things or 12 — if your kid has 'almost trained' more than once, there's a piece worth considering."

## PART 3: TONALITY CALIBRATION PER FLOW STAGE (Plan Layer 17, lines 341-355)

### Pre-Purchase Flows (Welcome, Cart, Browse, Checkout)
- Tone: Education and soft conversion
- Language: Specific, mechanism-led, peer-to-peer
- Energy: Calm and confident, "we are not in a hurry"
- Approach: Validate failures, explain mechanism, soft CTA
- FORBIDDEN: Hard-sell, urgency manipulation
- Sample: "You came for the code. Stay for the reason nothing else worked."

### Conversion Moment (Cart Recovery final, Checkout)
- Tone: Friendly reminder with permission not to buy
- Language: "if today is not right, that is a real answer"
- Energy: Decision-respect, "you decide"
- Approach: Reduce friction, offer guarantee, soft permission
- FORBIDDEN: Pressure tactics, fake scarcity, guilt-tripping
- Sample: "You left the 3+3 bundle in your cart. I am not chasing — but I want to answer the question you might be sitting with."

### Transactional Flows (Order Confirmation, Shipping)
- Tone: Warm and functional
- Language: Direct, informative, branded
- Energy: Professional but human
- Approach: Clear info, set expectations, soft upsell spot
- FORBIDDEN: Too much sales talk

### Post-Purchase Early (Arrived, Day 0-7)
- Tone: Warm with reassurance and education
- Language: "here is what to expect"
- Energy: "welcome to the family"
- Approach: Confirm purchase, set realistic Week 1 expectations, sensory prep tips
- FORBIDDEN: Hard re-selling, overselling ease of use

### Post-Purchase Mid (Day 14-60)
- Tone: Realistic, patient, "it is normal"
- Language: "Week 1 plateau is real. Hold the line."
- Energy: Coach and cheerleader
- Approach: Manage expectations, celebrate mini-wins, troubleshoot
- FORBIDDEN: Promising quick wins, abandoning the customer

### Lifecycle Flows (Replenishment, Winback, Sunset)
- Tone: Respectful re-engagement
- Language: "how are you doing?" and "whichever path you take"
- Energy: Genuinely curious, low-pressure
- Approach: Acknowledge time passed, no guilt, reopen softly
- FORBIDDEN: "You forgot us" guilt-tripping, aggressive re-engagement

## PART 4: WIKI-SOURCED FRAMEWORKS

### 4A. Breakthrough Advertising — Awareness Levels & Market Sophistication
**Source:** `/root/wiki/marketing-copy/copy-books/__enqueued__/breakthrough-advertising-breakthrough-advertising-chapter-2-your-prospects-state-of-awareness.md`

Eugene Schwartz's awareness framework is foundational to cross-level calibration. The General Fallback audience spans ALL five awareness levels simultaneously:

1. **Most Aware** — Knows the product, just needs the deal (Sub-Group D who already knows)
2. **Product Aware** — Knows what it is but not convinced (Sub-Group C sensory parents)
3. **Solution Aware** — Knows solutions exist but not yours (Sub-Group A researching)
4. **Problem Aware** — Feels the problem but doesn't know solutions exist (Sub-Group B rejecting labels)
5. **Unaware** — Doesn't even know they have a problem yet (edge case)

**Application to Cross-Level Emails:**
- The "Whether...Or...If..." pattern works because it addresses multiple awareness levels in one sentence
- "Whether you're just starting to wonder" = Problem Aware
- "Whether you've tried 2 things or 12" = Solution Aware
- "Here's what most parents miss" = bridges to Product Aware
- Every GF mail must touch the awareness spectrum without assuming any single level

**Market Sophistication Layer (Chapter 3):**
- The potty training market is SOPHISTICATED — parents have tried Oh Crap, sticker charts, bootcamp, ABA protocols
- Copy must acknowledge prior attempts ("we've tried everything") before introducing the mechanism
- The "Nothing Worked Loop" is essential: walk through conventional solutions, show the growing frustration, then introduce the real cause

### 4B. Background Story Framework — Emotional Engine
**Source:** `/root/wiki/about-me/identity/__enqueued__/__enqueued__/file_1nnxqk7i2cpguo6jp37q9c.md`

The background story framework from the BF Content library provides the emotional architecture for cross-level emails:

**Core Principle:** "Use real avatar quotes to shape the voice, pain points, and emotional beats of your story. Make it feel stolen from your avatar journal, not written by a brand."

**Structure for Cross-Level Application:**
1. **Establish the Narrator** — Who is telling the story and why their perspective matters
2. **Emotional Setup** — A grounded, human moment that pulls the reader in. "A small behavior. A thought. A detail that makes them say, 'That's familiar.'"
3. **Surface the Emotional Weight** — Not just what happened, but what it meant. The pressure. The shame. The fear.
4. **Walk Through the "Tried Everything" Pattern** — List conventional attempts. Build emotional toll of false hope.
5. **Introduce the Breaking Point** — The moment when the problem could no longer be ignored.
6. **Begin the Search for Answers** — Shift from passively suffering to actively understanding.
7. **Transition into Discovery** — Something clicked. A pattern, a question, a post.

**Cross-Level Adaptation:**
- The "Tried Everything" section must list methods from ALL sub-groups: Oh Crap (Level 1), ABA/OT protocols (Level 2), specialty diapers (Level 3)
- The emotional weight must be universal: exhaustion, self-doubt, hope-residue
- The narrator can be Lena (universal) or a three-family composite

**Key Quote:** "The goal isn't logic. It's resonance. To make the reader stop, lean in, and think: 'This person gets me.'"

### 4C. AI Prompt Framework — Tone & Language Calibration
**Source:** `/root/wiki/about-me/identity/__enqueued__/__enqueued__/ai-prompt-for-ad-concepts-and-ad-scripts.md`

The ad concepts framework provides a structured approach to tone calibration:

**Tone Determination Questions:**
- What tone earns trust?
- Clinical vs casual vs emotional?
- First-person or third-person storytelling?
- What psychological state are they in, and what voice will cut through?

**Psychological Frameworks Applicable to Cross-Level:**
- **System 1 & 2 Thinking** — GF emails must work at both levels: System 1 (emotional recognition hooks) and System 2 (mechanism explanation)
- **COM-B Model** — Capability (does she understand?), Opportunity (does she see a path?), Motivation (does she believe it could work?)
- **Narrative Transportation** — The story must transport her into recognition regardless of her sub-group
- **Mirror Neurons & Identification** — She must see herself in the copy. Cross-level means she must see herself in at least ONE of the recognition anchors
- **Zeigarnik Effect** — Open loops that keep her reading: "There's a piece nobody mentions..."
- **Loss Aversion & Anticipated Regret** — "What if you've been addressing the wrong problem all along?"

**Avatar Quote Integration:**
- DOC5 provides verbatim frustration quotes, hope-residue quotes, self-doubt quotes, and child descriptors
- These must be woven into cross-level copy to create identification across all sub-groups

### 4D. Segmentation Strategy — Broad vs. Targeted
**Source:** `/root/wiki/tenza-marketing/email-marketing/__enqueued__/8-segmentation_strategy_-_creating.md`

Adam Kitchen's segmentation philosophy directly informs the General Fallback approach:

**Core Insight:** "Customer behaviour changes over time. People's goals and interests are not static - they have overlapping reasons they buy from you."

**Application to Cross-Level:**
- General Fallback is a TEMPORARY state — the goal is migration to level-specific segments
- Go BROAD with campaign strategy for GF audience to maximize impressions and reach
- Use automation for first interactions to maximize conversions and guide through journey
- Don't over-segment the GF audience — it's already a segment

**Resource Allocation Principle:**
- Creating 3 lots of creatives and strategies without clearly defined cohorts leads to 3x production costs
- For GF: ONE mail set that works across all levels > multiple level-specific variants
- The "Whether...Or...If..." pattern IS the broad targeting solution

**Campaign vs. Flow Distinction:**
- Flows (Welcome, Cart, Browse): Targeted by behavior trigger, GF-specific copy
- Campaigns: Go broad to the entire GF segment, maximize reach
- "Right message at the right time" is easier to predict in flows than campaigns

## PART 5: DOC5 CROSS-LEVEL STRATEGY DEEP DIVE

### 5A. The 5 Sub-Groups of General Fallback (DOC5 Section 1)

| Sub-Group | Share of GF | Key Characteristic | Email Implication |
|---|---|---|---|
| A — Diagnostic Entrants | ~25% | No ASD diagnosis yet, confusion dominant | Education-first, NO diagnosis language |
| B — Diagnosis-Rejecters | ~15% | Has diagnosis but rejects labels | Language WITHOUT diagnosis terms |
| C — Sensory-Difference Parents | ~25% | SPD/ADHD, no ASD, mechanism-oriented | Science prominent, no ASD prerequisite |
| D — Overwhelmed Non-Selectors | ~25% | Has ASD child but skipped pop-up | Symptom hooks, migration opportunities |
| E — Cart/Browse Without Opt-In | ~10% | Via Klaviyo cookie, no level tag | Direct, symptom recognition + mechanism |

**Decisive Insight:** General Fallback is NOT homogeneous. It is 5 different mom types all needing to receive the same mail set. Universal Symptom Hooks > Level Specifics.

### 5B. The 3-Level Psychological Pyramid (DOC5 Section 6)

Every GF mail must touch all 3 levels:

1. **Level 1: Surface — Universal Recognition**
   "You have a child who isn't progressing in potty training like mainstream methods promise."
   → Reaches: ALL sub-groups, all levels.

2. **Level 2: Middle — Specific Symptoms**
   "Kids who don't feel when they need to / keep playing while peeing / don't notice they're wet."
   → Reaches: those with interoception issues, regardless of diagnosis.

3. **Level 3: Deep — Mechanism**
   "This isn't behavior. This is signal processing."
   → Reaches: those who want to know WHY.

**Structure Formula for Each GF Mail:**
1. Opener: Universal Recognition (Level 1)
2. Body: Specific Symptoms (Level 2)
3. Why-Section: Mechanism (Level 3)
4. CTA: Universal Permission ("if this fits, here's the option")

### 5C. 4-Phase Mix Dynamics (DOC5 Section 7)

The 4 phases must all be addressable simultaneously:

- **Phase 1 Elements:** "If you're just starting to wonder..." — Education, gentle mechanism intro
- **Phase 2 Elements:** "If you tried Oh Crap or similar and it didn't work..." — Validation of method failure
- **Phase 3 Elements:** "If you've cycled through methods for years..." — Science, 60-Day Guarantee
- **Phase 4 Elements:** "Whether your kid is 3 or 10..." — "Late is not never" hint, permission-not-to-buy

**Integration Tip:** Instead of 4 separate sections, integrate through bridging phrases:
- "Wherever you are on this journey..."
- "Whether you're 3 weeks in or 3 years in..."
- "Some parents have just started looking, others have tried everything..."

### 5D. Universal Symptom Hooks (DOC5 Section 8)

**Priority 1 (Use 3-4 per mail):**
- "Kids who sit on the toilet for 20 minutes and nothing happens" → Hits Level 1-3
- "Kids who pee right after the pull-up comes off" → Hits Level 1-3
- "Kids who play in a wet pull-up without reacting" → Especially Level 2-3
- "Kids who refuse to even try the toilet" → Universal
- "Kids whose pediatrician keeps saying 'wait until they're ready' — but the readiness never arrives" → Universal, powerful

**Priority 2:**
- "Kids who hold their bowel movements until pull-ups go on"
- "Kids who can talk about pottying but can't do it"
- "Kids who 'almost trained' multiple times"
- "Kids who pee or poop seconds after leaving the toilet"
- "Kids who don't seem to notice or care that they're wet"
- "Kids who scream when the toilet flushes"
- "Kids who need the same toilet, anywhere else triggers meltdowns"

### 5E. Medium-Balanced Tonality (DOC5 Section 18)

| Dimension | Level | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| Hope Level | MEDIUM (50/50) | Not too high (loses Level 3), not too low (loses Level 1) |
| Urgency | LOW-MEDIUM | Soft urgency OK, never manipulative |
| Science Density | MEDIUM | Kelly Mahler yes, but not 3 paragraphs |
| Empathy Intensity | HIGH | Across all sub-groups, mom is tired |
| Testimonial Length | MEDIUM | 4-6 sentences, multiple shorter > one long |
| CTA Aggressiveness | MEDIUM-LOW | "See if this fits your situation" |
| General Energy | Inclusive, broad | Like an experienced advisor who doesn't assume |

### 5F. Cross-Level Bridge Language (DOC5 Section 19)

**Universal Openers:**
- "Wherever you are on this journey..."
- "Whether you're just starting or 3 years in..."
- "If you've tried 2 things or 12 things..."
- "For kids of all ages and profiles..."
- "Whatever you call this..."

**Universal Child Descriptors:**
- "Kids who don't feel when they need to go"
- "Kids whose nervous systems process signals differently"
- "Kids who 'should be ready' but aren't"
- "Kids whose pediatrician said 'wait'"

**Universal Validation:**
- "You've already worked harder than most parents ever do."
- "Whatever you've tried, you tried because you care."
- "You've already done more research than most."
- "The fact that you're here means you've been doing the work."

**Universal Mechanism:**
- "There's a signal that needs to come from the body."
- "Most methods assume the signal arrives. What if it doesn't?"
- "The brain needs sustained sensation to make the connection."
- "Pull-ups remove the signal entirely."

**Universal Closers:**
- "If this lands, you know where to find us."
- "If it doesn't, no hard feelings."
- "Whichever path you take — thanks for reading."
- "Take your time. The product will be here when you're ready."

### 5G. Forbidden Phrases — Cross-Level Specific (DOC5 Section 24)

| Forbidden | Why |
|---|---|
| "For Level 1/2/3 kids..." | Only hits Sub-Group D |
| "Diagnosed autism only" | Misses Sub-Groups A and C |
| "Verbal kids will..." | Misses Level 3 |
| "Your toddler will..." | Some kids are 8-10 |
| School-specific urgency (Pre-K) | Some kids are in special education |
| "Just like other kids" | Misses all neurodivergent families |
| "Easy" / "Quick" / "Simple" | Universal block |
| "Cure" / "Fix" / "Solve" | Misses all neurodivergent families |
| "He'll be normal" | Misses Sub-Group B+C |
| "When she's potty trained..." | Implies success guaranteed, misses Level 3 |

### 5H. Testimonial Strategy (DOC5 Sections 15-17)

**Three-Family Pattern:**
Instead of ONE testimonial, offer THREE (short, compact) — one per level:
- Sarah, mom of 4yo verbal son: "He went from 'almost' to 'mostly' in 6 weeks."
- Lisa, mom of 6yo daughter (sensory dominant): "After 3 years of trying everything, she finally paused when she felt wetness. That was Week 4."
- Maria, mom of 9yo non-verbal son: "We're 5 months in. He's not trained. But he's feeling something for the first time."

**Why this works:** Everyone finds themselves in a testimonial. Different levels implicitly acknowledged. No miracle promises. Realistic outcomes per profile.

**Single Cross-Level Testimonial:**
- No diagnosis labels, no fixed age, multiple methods mentioned
- Realistic outcome ("not fully trained, but...")
- Symptom-First Language throughout

### 5I. Migration Strategy (DOC5 Sections 28-33)

General Fallback is TEMPORARY. Goal: migration to level-specific segments.

**4 Migration Paths:**
1. **Click-Based:** Level-specific story links in GF mails → automatic tag update
2. **Reply-Based:** Mom replies with details → level inferred from language
3. **Purchase-Based:** Underwear size → age hint → level hint
4. **Post-Purchase Level Detection (Day 14):** 4 buttons, 40-50% click rate, strongest migration method

4. **Processing Fluency** — The DOC system's "Symptom-First Language" is supported by processing fluency research: familiar-sounding descriptions (symptoms she recognizes) feel more true than abstract labels (Level 2).

## PART 7: PRACTICAL APPLICATION GUIDANCE FOR WRITING CROSS-LEVEL EMAILS

### Before Writing Checklist:
1. Which sub-groups should be primarily addressed?
2. Plan the "Whether...Or...If..." structure
3. Plan migration anchors (3-4 per mail)
4. Select 3+ symptoms from Universal list
5. Include Permission-Not-To-Buy closer
6. Identify flow stage + mail position

### While Writing Checklist:
1. Symptom-First language — NO level labels
2. Age range language (3-10, not fixed)
3. Verbal AND non-verbal hints included
4. Subtly address all 4 phases
5. Cross-level testimonials or Three-Family format
6. Balance tonality at 50/50 hope/realism
7. Build in migration opportunities
8. Lena sign-off compliant

### After Writing Checklist:
1. Forbidden phrases checked
2. No Pre-K hooks
3. No level labels in copy
4. No diagnosis prerequisites
5. No "easy/quick" language
6. Mobile-first layout
7. 3 subject line options created
8. 3 preheader options created
9. Cross-Level Bridge Language used

### The One-Line Summary:
"An audience of diagnostic entrants, label-rejecters, sensory-different parents and just-not-self-identified — mails must address symptoms, not diagnoses, and build in migration anchors."
