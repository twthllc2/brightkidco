## Copy Rules About Partner References

### Direct Partner Reference Rules
1. **Never blame the partner.** Don't say "your partner is wrong" or "they don't understand." Frame as "different perspectives" or "natural caution."
2. **Give the purchasing parent ammunition, not ammunition to fight.** Provide evidence, not arguments. "Here's the research" not "Here's why your partner should listen."
3. **Offer a sharing mechanism.** Every partner-conflict email should have a way to forward/share: "Save this email," "Forward this link," "Show them this."
4. **Respect the skeptical partner's concerns.** Don't dismiss price, timing, or trust objections — address them head-on with proof.
5. **Use "we" and "our" language.** "We know this is a big decision for your family" — not "you need to convince your partner."

### Forbidden Partner-Conflict Language
- "Convince your partner" (implies one is right, one is wrong)
- "Your partner will thank you" (presumptuous)
- "Don't let your partner hold you back" (divisive)
- "Just do it anyway" (undermines the relationship)
- "Fix your disagreement" (reduces real conflict to a simple problem)

### Recommended Partner-Conflict Language
- "When you're both ready"
- "Here's something to review together"
- "Many families start with one parent being skeptical — that's normal"
- "The research speaks for itself"
- "No pressure — take the time you need"
- "We're here when you've had a chance to talk it through"

## Emotional Dynamics Frameworks

### 1. The Partner Conflict Cycle (from PAS + Avatar Calibration)
```
Purchasing Parent (excited) → Shares with Skeptical Partner → Partner raises objections
       ↓                                                              ↓
  Frustration builds ← Conflict escalates ← Both dig in ← Objections not addressed
       ↓
  Decision paralysis → No purchase → Child misses window → Guilt → More conflict
```

### 2. The Resolution Path (from BAB + Objection Handling)
```
Before: Parents in conflict, decision paralysis
After: Both aligned, child receiving support
Bridge: Evidence-based framework that addresses both perspectives
```

### 3. Avatar-Specific Partner Conflict Dynamics

| Avatar Level | Purchasing Parent State | Skeptical Partner State | Copy Approach |
|---|---|---|---|
| Level 1 (mild) | Excited, hopeful | Cautious, "wait and see" | Gentle urgency, aspirational proof, easy sharing mechanism |
| Level 2 (moderate) | Experienced, tried methods | Skeptical, "we've tried everything" | Respect existing approaches, proof-heavy, "complements what you're doing" |
| Level 3 (severe) | Exhausted, desperate | In denial or overwhelmed | Dignity-first, never urgency, late-trainer stories, "it's never too late" |
| General | Unknown | Unknown | Universal hooks, migration-focused, "whether your partner agrees or not" |

### 4. The 3 Emotional Phases of Partner Conflict

**Phase 1: The Disagreement**
- One parent sees the problem clearly, the other minimizes or denies
- Copy approach: Validate both perspectives, provide objective evidence

**Phase 2: The Stalemate**
- Both parents entrenched, communication breaks down around the topic
- Copy approach: Third-party authority (research, testimonials, expert quotes), not brand opinion

**Phase 3: The Alignment (or Separation)**
- Parents either align (purchase) or one parent gives up trying to convince
- Copy approach: Low-pressure, "we'll be here when you're ready," no guilt
