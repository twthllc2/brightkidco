## 2. SCOPE-TO-SUB-GROUP MAPPING (A–E)

### 2.1 Sub-Group A: Diagnostic Entrants (~25% of GF, ~10% of total)

**Profile:** No formal ASD diagnosis. Child 2–4 years. Confusion dominant. Doesn't identify as "autism mom."

| Scope Rule | Applies? | Sub-Group A Calibration | Conflict? |
|------------|:--------:|------------------------|:---------:|
| Phase 1 = Autistic Parent Only | PARTIAL | She SUSPECTS autism but has no diagnosis | YES — scope assumes diagnosis |
| Symptom-First language | YES | "If you're starting to wonder..." | ALIGNED |
| Never assume diagnosis | CRITICAL | NEVER say "Your autistic child" | ALIGNED |
| Hope/Realism 50/50 | NO — she needs HIGHER hope | She's in Phase 1 (Slow Realization); 70%+ hope | CONFLICT |
| Migration anchors | YES | She'll likely self-select after diagnosis | ALIGNED |
| Education-first approach | YES | Gentle mechanism intro, no hard sales | ALIGNED |
| "Whether...Or...If..." pattern | YES | "Whether you're 3 weeks into wondering..." | ALIGNED |
| GF-D lever ("Not just for autism") | MEDIUM | She likely has ASD context; less critical | ALIGNED |
| RS Integration | CAUTION | She doesn't have failed methods to acknowledge | PARTIAL |
| Anti-Fabrication | YES | Non-negotiable | ALIGNED |

**Sub-Group A Scope Conflicts:**

1. **Hope level mismatch.** Scope rules assign General Fallback 50/50 hope/realism. But Sub-Group A is in Phase 1 (Slow Realization) with HIGH hope (70%+). A 50/50 mail feels too pessimistic for her. She needs education + hope, not realism.

2. **RS integration gap.** The scope rules require 2–4 authentic verbatim quotes per mail. But Sub-Group A doesn't have "failed method" stories. She hasn't tried anything yet. The standard RS quotes about "Oh Crap didn't work" or "BCBA protocol got us nowhere" don't resonate.

3. **Scope assumes ASD context.** The Phase 1 boundary ("Autistic Parent Avatar ONLY") implies the audience IS autistic parents. Sub-Group A is PRE-diagnostic. She's in the audience but doesn't yet belong to the scope's defined population.

**Sub-Group B Scope Conflicts:**

1. **Lena's identity conflicts with Sub-Group B's worldview.** The scope rule defines Lena as "Mom of two autistic sons." Sub-Group B rejects the "autism" label. Lena's standard sign-off ("Customer Support · Mom of two autistic sons") contains the exact word Sub-Group B finds alienating. Sub-Group B is IN the audience but REJECTS the identity framework. She's a scope-eligible subscriber who is alienated by the scope's language.

4. **The 28 Buckets framework doesn't apply.** Layer 35 R3 found that the wiki's avatar infrastructure assumes a single persona. Sub-Group B doesn't fit ANY persona — she has the experience of Level 2–3 but the identity resistance of someone outside the framework entirely.

**Sub-Group C Scope Conflicts:**

1. **Phase 1 scope EXCLUDES her by definition.** The scope rule states "Phase 1 focus is exclusively on the Autistic Parent Avatar." Sub-Group C's child is NOT autistic — SPD, ADHD, or other neurological differences. Yet she's in the General Fallback audience receiving Phase 1 emails. The scope boundary doesn't account for non-ASD subscribers who are captured by the same pop-up and email flows.

2. **RS files are autism-specific.** The in-scope RS files (Autistic_Parent.pdf, Autistic_Parent_G.pdf, Autistic_language_Bank.pdf, Autistic_MIRO_m.pdf) contain quotes from AUTISTIC parents. Sub-Group C's parents have SPD/ADHD children. The verbatim quotes about "autism" don't resonate. The scope rule requiring 30–50% authentic verbatim language from these files creates a mismatch.

3. **Lever 5 (49% SPARK Stat) is ASD-specific.** The SPARK study statistic is about autistic 4–5 year olds. Sub-Group C's child isn't autistic. The scope rules don't provide a SPD/ADHD equivalent statistic. Layer 21 suggests: "49% of autistic 4-5 year olds aren't trained. For kids with sensory differences of any kind, the percentage is similar."

4. **The "Not just for autism" lever is the highest-leverage GF lever but isn't in the original scope rules.** The 73 scope rules from client files don't explicitly define GF-D. It emerges from Layer 21's cross-reference. This is a scope gap — the most important lever for 40% of General Fallback (Sub-Groups B + C) isn't codified in the scope rules.

**Profile:** Has ASD child with clear level. Didn't select pop-up out of overwhelm. Migration goldmine.

**Sub-Group D Scope Conflicts:**

1. **Hope level is unknowable.** Scope rules assign General Fallback 50/50 hope/realism. But Sub-Group D has a HIDDEN level — she could be Level 1 (70% hope), Level 2 (40% hope), or Level 3 (20% hope). The 50/50 calibration is a compromise that may feel too hopeful for a hidden Level 3 or too pessimistic for a hidden Level 1.

2. **Migration strategy requires level detection, which contradicts "never label" rule.** The scope rule says "NEVER say 'Level 2' in copy." The mail itself must present level options without using level labels — symptom-based buttons only ("Talks about potty but won't actually do it" / "Sensory issues, tried many methods" / "Non-verbal or high support needs" / "Still figuring out").

But the scope rules don't address how to simultaneously serve Phase 1 (Slow Realization), Phase 2 (First Attempt), Phase 3 (Method Cycling), and Phase 4 (Resignation) in a single mail. This is a structural gap in the scope rules.

## 5. RECOMMENDATIONS FOR RESOLVING CONFLICTS

### 5.1 Critical Fixes (Pre-Launch)

**R1: Expand Phase 1 scope to acknowledge non-ASD Sub-Group C.**

Current scope: "Phase 1 focus is exclusively on the Autistic Parent Avatar."
Recommended: "Phase 1 focus is on parents of children with neurological differences affecting toileting. The primary audience is autistic parents (Levels 1–3 + General Fallback), but General Fallback also serves parents of children with SPD, ADHD, and other sensory differences (Sub-Group C). All Phase 1 emails use symptom-first language that works across diagnostic categories."

Rationale: Sub-Group C is ~10% of total audience, higher-income, mechanism-literate, and converts well on GF-D ("Not just for autism"). Excluding them from scope definition means their needs aren't systematically addressed.

**R2: Create Lena voice variants for Sub-Groups B and C.**

Current scope: "Lena S., Customer Support, Mom of two autistic sons"
Recommended variants:
- Sub-Group B: "Lena S., Customer Support, Mom of two boys who didn't follow the typical timeline"
- Sub-Group C: "Lena S., Customer Support, Mom of two boys who process the world differently"
- Sub-Group D + Standard: "Lena S., Customer Support, Mom of two autistic sons" (unchanged)

Rationale: The standard Lena identity contains the exact word Sub-Group B finds alienating. One word ("autistic") in the sign-off can cause 6% of the audience to disengage.

**R3: Codify GF-D lever ("Not just for autism") in scope rules.**

Current scope: 73 rules, none mention GF-D.
Recommended addition: "General Fallback mails for Sub-Groups B and C MUST include the 'Not just for autism' framing: 'For autistic kids. For ADHD kids. For SPD kids. For kids who just process signals differently.' This is the highest-leverage GF lever for non-ASD and label-rejecting subscribers."

Rationale: GF-D is the single most important lever for 40% of General Fallback (Sub-Groups B + C). Not codifying it means it's left to individual copywriter judgment.

**R4: Create Sub-Group C-specific RS supplement.**

Current scope: In-scope RS = Autistic_Parent.pdf, Autistic_Parent_G.pdf, Autistic_language_Bank.pdf, Autistic_MIRO_m.pdf.
Recommended: Add SPD/ADHD parent research to RS scope. If unavailable, adapt existing RS quotes by replacing "autism" with "sensory differences" where the quote is about sensory/interoception issues (not autism-specific). Document adaptations internally per anti-fabrication rule.

Rationale: The RS files are autism-specific. Sub-Group C's parents need SPD/ADHD-relevant verbatim quotes. Without supplement, the 30–50% RS quota forces autism language into non-ASD mails.

### 5.2 High-Priority Fixes (Pre-Launch)

**R5: Define hope-level ranges per sub-group within General Fallback.**

Current scope: General Fallback = 50/50 hope/realism.
Recommended:

| Sub-Group | Hope Level | Realism Level | Rationale |
|-----------|-----------|--------------|-----------|
| A — Diagnostic Entrants | 65–70% | 30–35% | Phase 1 hope-intact; education-first |
| B — Diagnosis-Rejecters | 45–50% | 50–55% | Medium hope; label-free framing |
| C — Sensory-Difference | 45–50% | 50–55% | Intellectual buyer; science-first |
| D — Overwhelmed | 40–60% | 40–60% | Variable by hidden level; 50/50 is safe default |
| E — Cart/Browse | 50–55% | 45–50% | Slightly hopeful; guarantee-first |

Rationale: A single 50/50 calibration serves Sub-Groups B–E adequately but is too pessimistic for Sub-Group A (Phase 1, high hope). The "Whether...Or...If..." pattern allows hope-level variation within a single mail by offering different sentence paths.

**R6: Create format exceptions for cart recovery emails.**

Current scope: 2–4 verbatim RS quotes per mail, 30–50% RS language.
Recommended exception: Cart/Checkout/Browse recovery emails (Sub-Group E) use abbreviated format: 1 RS quote maximum, mechanism in 2–3 sentences, guarantee prominent, Lena sign-off. Total length: 150–250 words maximum.

Rationale: Sub-Group E has active buying intent but no context. Long-form RS-heavy emails don't match her decision state. The scope rule should define format tiers by flow type.

**R7: Distinguish baseline vs trigger-spike urgency for Level 2.**

Current scope: Cross-level matrix assigns Level 2 urgency = LOW.
Layer 4 reality: Level 2 urgency spikes to HIGH on trigger events (withholding crisis, school transition, GI hospitalization).
Recommended: Add to scope rules — "Level 2 baseline urgency = LOW. Urgency may spike to HIGH on specific trigger events (withholding crisis, school transition, new diagnosis). Trigger-spike emails use event-specific subject lines and shortened decision windows."

Rationale: Treating Level 2 urgency as permanently LOW misses the highest-conversion moments. The scope rules should allow event-driven urgency without contradicting the baseline.

### 5.3 Medium-Priority Fixes (Post-Launch, 30 Days)

**R8: Design 4-phase integration pattern for General Fallback mails.**

Current scope: No guidance on serving all 4 emotional phases in one mail.
Recommended pattern (from Layer 21):
1. Opener: Universal Recognition (Pattern A or D) — hits all phases
2. Body: Specific Symptoms (Pattern E — 3–5 from Priority 1 list) — each symptom resonates with different phases
3. Why-Section: Mechanism — universal across phases
4. CTA: Universal Permission — "If this fits, here's the option"

Phase integration tips:
- "Wherever you are on this journey..." = Phase 1–4 inclusive
- "Whether you're 3 weeks in or 3 years in..." = Phase 1 vs Phase 3–4
- "Some parents have just started looking, others have tried everything..." = Phase 1–2 vs Phase 3–4

**R9: Extend flow timelines for Level 2 and Level 3.**

Current scope: Welcome Flow = 14–21 days.
Layer 4 reality: L2 decision = 7–21 days; L3 decision = 14–30+ days.
Recommended: Extend L2 Welcome to 21–28 days. Extend L3 Welcome to 28–35 days. Add post-flow nurture sequence (2–3 additional mails) for subscribers who haven't converted by flow end.

**R10: Create Sub-Group B engagement strategy.**

Current scope: No specific strategy for label-rejecting subscribers.
Recommended: Sub-Group B is a PERMANENT General Fallback resident. She will NEVER migrate to a level-specific segment. The GF flow must convert her IN the General segment. Key adaptations:
- Lena variant (no "autism" in sign-off)
- Symptom-only language (never label)

- No migration pressure

### Key Findings

1. **The scope rules are designed for a DIAGNOSED, IDENTITY-ACCEPTING audience.** They work perfectly for Level 1, Level 2, and Level 3 — subscribers who have an ASD diagnosis and accept the "autism parent" identity. They work adequately for Sub-Group D (has diagnosis, just didn't say so) and Sub-Group E (unknown but captured via cart).

2. **The scope rules BREAK for 26% of General Fallback (Sub-Groups A, B, C).** These subscribers are pre-diagnostic (A), label-rejecting (B), or non-ASD (C). The scope's foundational assumption — "Autistic Parent Avatar" — doesn't match their identity. Every scope rule built on this assumption (RS integration, Lena voice, lever selection, hope calibration) needs sub-group-specific variants.

3. **Sub-Group C is the most structurally excluded.** She's not autistic, not pre-diagnostic, not label-rejecting — she's a parent of a child with SPD/ADHD who was captured by the same email system. The Phase 1 scope explicitly excludes her by definition. Yet she's ~10% of total audience, higher-income, and mechanism-literate. She represents hidden revenue that the scope rules don't account for.

4. **Sub-Group D is the best-served by existing scope rules.** She has a real diagnosis, accepts the framework, and just didn't communicate her level. The scope rules work for her as-is. The Day 14 Level-Detection Mail is the bridge from General to level-specific flows.

5. **The GF-D lever ("Not just for autism") is the most important missing scope rule.** It's the highest-leverage lever for 40% of General Fallback (Sub-Groups B + C) and it doesn't appear in any of the 73 scope rules. It emerges only from Layer 21's cross-reference analysis.

6. **RS integration rules need format tiers.** A blanket "2–4 quotes, 30–50% RS language" rule works for Welcome Flows but breaks for cart recovery (too long), Sub-Group B (label language), and Sub-Group C (autism-specific). The scope should define RS quotas by flow type and sub-group.
