## 2. LEVEL 1

**The Verbal, Hope-Intact Mom** | ~35% audience | Tag: `avatar_level1`
**Profile:** Child 3-5y, verbal, low support needs. Mom has 70% hope, 1-3 prior method attempts.

### 2.1 Level 1 × Layer 2 (Customer)

| Customer Attribute | Level 1 Expression | Key Difference from Other Levels |
|--------------------|-------------------|----------------------------------|
| Mom Age | 28-40 | Youngest cohort |
| Employment | 70% working (often part-time) | Highest employment rate |
| Income | Upper-middle dominant | Highest purchasing power |
| Education | ~70% college-educated | Highly educated, tech-savvy |
| First-time Mom | Very common | Often first child with challenges |
| Child Age | 3-5 years (Pre-K window) | Narrowest age window |
| Child Gender | Boys overrepresented | — |
| Family Structure | Married/partnered dominant, 10-15% single | Lowest single-mom rate |
| Geography | US 65%, UK 15%, AU/NZ 15%, CA 5% | Same as overall |
| Therapy Load | Minimal — no BCBA/ABA | Lowest therapy load |
| Pull-up Costs | $200-500 spent on failed methods | Lowest sunk cost, highest willingness to invest |
| Diagnosed Level | Level 1 ASD (explicit) or undiagnosed/tracking | Overlaps with GF-A (Diagnostic Entrants) |

**Mom Surface Identity:** "I'm a regular mom whose kid is having a hard time with potty" — does NOT identify as "autism mom" yet. This identity is shared with Layer 21 Sub-Group A (Diagnostic Entrants).

**Conversion Context:** Pre-K enrollment is the master driver. The Pre-K deadline (age 3-4) creates a natural purchase urgency that does NOT exist at any other level.

### 2.2 Level 1 × Layer 5 (Emotional Dimensions)

| Dimension | Intensity | Level 1 Expression | Conversion Implication |
|-----------|:---------:|--------------------|-----------------------|
| **Deadline Pressure** | ★★★★ (Very High) | Pre-K enrollment deadline is THE Level 1 event. "He'll be the only one in pull-ups in his class." | #1 conversion lever pathway — highest in ALL ecosystem |
| **Social Pressure** | ★★★☆ (Medium-High) | Playground comparison, "he's so smart but can't do this" paradox. First-time mom shame. | Strong; shame is fresh and acute |
| **Overwhelmed** | ★★☆☆ (Medium) | Psychological exhaustion from inconsistency, not physical. Less physical cleanup drama. | Manageable — hope still provides energy reserve |

**Dominant Combination:** Deadline Pressure + Social Pressure (Phase 2, Level 1) — "The Cash Cow Pattern"
- Conversion: 15-25% — highest in entire ecosystem
- Emotional cocktail: Panic (external deadline) + Shame (comparison with NT peers)
- Email strategy: L1 (Sensory Gap validates failure) → L4 (Lena peer voice) → L3 (60-Day Guarantee)

**Unique Level 1 Emotional Dynamic:** This is the ONLY level where Deadline Pressure dominates. No other level has an external, non-negotiable timeline (Pre-K enrollment). This makes Level 1 the easiest to convert — and the most vulnerable to mis-calibrated messaging (amplifying panic instead of calming it).

### 2.3 Level 1 × Layer 6 (Symptom Clusters)

| Cluster | Recognition | Level 1 Specificity | Email Implication |
|---------|:-----------:|---------------------|-------------------|
| **E: "Gets It But Doesn't Get It"** | ~80% | THE Level 1 marker phrase | Lead with this — signals "I see exactly your situation" |
| **A: Timing Problems** | ~70% | "Says 'need to pee' AFTER it happened" | Strong recognition hook for education emails |
| **B: Inconsistent Performance** | ~80% | "Some days great, other days zero" | Highest recognition — strongest trust builder |
| **C: Verbal Refusal** | ~60% | "Says no, then has accident 10 min later" | Reframe as missing signal, not defiance |
| **D: Potty Interest Without Action** | ~65% | "Loves potty time but won't actually go" | Very Level 1 specific — Level 2/3 don't have this |

**Recommended Cluster Combinations for Level 1 Emails:**
- Welcome E1: Cluster E + Cluster A
- Welcome E2: Cluster A + Cluster B + Cluster D
- Cart Recovery: Cluster E + Cluster D
- FAQ: Cluster B + Cluster C

**Cross-Level Insight:** Level 1 moms recognize 5 clusters, but 2 are shared with other levels (C and partially A). The true Level 1 signature is **Cluster E + Cluster D** — intellectual understanding without execution + motivation without follow-through.

### 2.4 Level 1 × Layer 21 (5 Sub-Groups)

| Sub-Group | Overlap with Level 1 | Strength | Migration Path |
|-----------|---------------------|----------|----------------|
| **A — Diagnostic Entrants** | **Very High** — most Level 1 moms start here | ~30% of Level 1 traffic | GF-A → Level 1 on diagnosis (naturally migrates) |
| **B — Diagnosis-Rejecters** | Medium — some Level 1 moms reject labels | ~10% of Level 1 | May stay in GF, resist level-specific tags |
| **C — Sensory-Difference Parents** | Medium-High — Level 1 sensory issues exist | ~15% of Level 1 | May self-select Level 1 or stay in GF |
| **D — Overwhelmed Non-Selectors** | Low — Level 1 is not yet overwhelmed | <5% | Will populate as child ages |
| **E — Cart/Browse Without Opt-In** | Medium — Level 1 makes fast purchases | ~15% | Cart recovery → Level 1 detection mail |

**Key Overlap:** Level 1 and GF-A (Diagnostic Entrants) share the same mom — she just hasn't received an ASD diagnosis yet. The email experience for GF-A should naturally lead to Level 1 content once she clicks a "verbal kid" anchor. This is the highest-yield migration path in the entire system.

**Key Difference:** Level 1 has an explicit or implicit ASD awareness. GF-A (Diagnostic Entrants) is still "wondering." The tonality difference: Level 1 can use autism-aware language; GF-A cannot.

**Sub-Group B risk:** Level 1 copy that says "Your autistic child..." risks losing Sub-Group B moms who are functionally Level 1 but reject the label. The solution: use symptom language (Cluster E + Cluster A) that describes the experience without labeling it.

## 3. LEVEL 2

**The Mixed-Verbal, Skeptical Mom** | ~40% audience (LARGEST) | Tag: `avatar_level2`
**Profile:** Child 5-7y, mixed verbal, moderate support needs. 40% hope. 3-5+ method attempts.

### 3.1 Level 2 × Layer 2 (Customer)

| Customer Attribute | Level 2 Expression | Key Difference from Other Levels |
|--------------------|-------------------|----------------------------------|
| Mom Age | 30-42 | Middle cohort |
| Employment | 45% working / 45% SAHM | Most split — significant caregiver burden |
| Income | Middle dominant, ~25% Medicaid | Middle class — most cost-sensitive |
| Education | ~75% college-educated | Highly informed, reads studies |
| Second Child NT | Common (comparison pain) | Unique to Level 2 |
| Child Age | 5-7 years (Elementary transition) | Middle age range |
| Therapy Load | Heavy: BCBA 20-30 hrs/wk, OT 1-2x, Speech 1-2x | Highest therapy coordination burden |
| Pull-up Costs | $500-1,500 wasted on methods | Significant sunk cost |
| Comorbidities | SPD ~95%, ADHD ~40%, GI ~50%, Sleep ~45% | Highest comorbidity variety |
| Partner Conflict | High — "Potty training might be our demise" | Most relationship-impacted |
| Diagnosed Level | Level 2 ASD (explicit) | Most self-aware of level label |

**Mom Surface Identity:** "Autism mom" is fully adopted. She knows the system, the therapies, the abbreviations. She is the most informed customer.

**Financial Reality:** Dual burden of therapy costs AND method costs. She has spent $500-1,500 on failed potty methods. This creates both sunk-cost pain (motivator to find something that works) AND skepticism (why would this be different?).

### 3.2 Level 2 × Layer 5 (Emotional Dimensions)

| Dimension | Intensity | Level 2 Expression | Conversion Implication |
|-----------|:---------:|--------------------|-----------------------|
| **Deadline Pressure** | ★★★☆ (Medium) | School transitions (Kindergarten, IEP). Reduced by accommodations. Less acute than L1. | Moderate urgency — can wait, but transitions create windows |
| **Social Pressure** | ★★★★ (High) | Extended family pressure, younger NT sibling trains first, partner blame is real. Deep chronic shame. | Very strong — shame is the hidden purchase driver |
| **Overwhelmed** | ★★★★ (High) | Years of cleaning, therapy coordination fatigue. "My house is ruined." Physical + emotional exhaustion. | Strongest dimension — daily chaos years deep |

**Dominant Combination:** Overwhelmed + Social Pressure (Phase 3, Level 2) — "The Volume Engine"
- Conversion: 8-15% but highest volume audience (22% of paying segment)
- Emotional cocktail: Exhaustion (years of methods) + Shame (deep comparison pain)
- Email strategy: L4 (Lena sees exhaustion AND shame) → L1 (mechanism hope) → L3 (no-risk trial)

**Unique Level 2 Dynamic:** THIS is the only level where all three dimensions are active simultaneously at MEDIUM or higher intensity. Level 1 has low Overwhelmed. Level 3 has low Deadline. Only Level 2 lives in the perfect storm of all three — making it the most complex email challenge.

**The BCBA-Trust Dynamic:** Level 2 moms have 4-6 year relationships with BCBAs. ANY messaging that undermines BCBA work (even implicitly) destroys trust. The "It's not the method" lever must be calibrated to complement, not criticize, ABA.

### 3.3 Level 2 × Layer 6 (Symptom Clusters)

| Cluster | Recognition | Level 2 Specificity | Email Implication |
|---------|:-----------:|---------------------|-------------------|
| **A: Sensory Defensiveness Toilet** | ~80% | "Flushing sound makes her cry," "bathroom lights too bright" | STRONGEST recognition hook for Level 2 |
| **B: Withholding + GI Issues** | ~70% | "Holds poop until bedtime pull-up," chronic constipation | Most emotional — handle with care, NO cure promises |
| **C: Delayed Verbal Signals** | ~60% | "Says 'wet' after accident, never before" | "Verbal doesn't mean independent" is the key reframe |
| **D: Schedule Dependency** | ~75% | "Goes when prompted, never initiates," prompt-dependent after years of ABA | SECOND strongest — most unique Level 2 frustration |
| **E: Regression Cycles** | ~65% | "3 weeks success then total regression," "vacation = starting over" | Acknowledges non-linear reality |
| **F: Location-Specific Training** | ~50% | "Goes at school not at home," "specific toilet only" | Validates parent — child can do it, context matters |

**Recommended Cluster Combinations for Level 2 Emails:**
- Welcome E2: Cluster A + Cluster D
- Welcome E4: Cluster D + Cluster E
- Cart Recovery: Cluster B + Cluster D
- FAQ: Cluster A + Cluster C

**Cross-Level Insight:** Level 2 has 6 symptom clusters — the most of any level. No single cluster defines Level 2; the cluster COMBINATION defines it. The most powerful Level 2 recognition combo is Sensory Defensiveness (A) + Schedule Dependency (D) — "the bathroom is sensory hell AND the ABA hasn't built internal signals."

### 3.4 Level 2 × Layer 21 (5 Sub-Groups)

| Sub-Group | Overlap with Level 2 | Strength | Migration Path |
|-----------|---------------------|----------|----------------|
| **A — Diagnostic Entrants** | Low — Level 2 is diagnosed | <5% | Rarely overlaps |
| **B — Diagnosis-Rejecters** | Medium — some L2 moms reject labels | ~10% | May refuse "Level 2" tag despite fitting |
| **C — Sensory-Difference Parents** | **Very High** — SPD dominant at Level 2 | ~35% of Level 2 | Self-selects through sensory content |
| **D — Overwhelmed Non-Selectors** | **Very High** — most L2 non-selectors are overwhelmed | ~30% of Level 2 | Day 14 detection mail is key migration tool |
| **E — Cart/Browse Without Opt-In** | Medium | ~15% | Cart recovery should include Level 2 symptom hooks |

**Key Overlap:** Level 2 and GF-C (Sensory-Difference Parents) share the sensory profile — SPD, interoception issues, OT involvement. The difference: Level 2 has an ASD diagnosis, GF-C may not. Email strategy for Level 2 should include GF-C accessible language to capture the sensory-dominant audience that hasn't self-selected.

**Key Difference:** Level 2 is the most "BCBA-loyal" segment. Sub-Group D (Overwhelmed Non-Selectors) are functionally Level 2 but didn't engage with the pop-up — they're the same mom, just too tired to fill out a form.

**Sub-Group D migration (Level 2 dominant):** The Post-Purchase Level-Detection Mail (Day 14) with "Sensory issues, tried many methods" button is the most critical migration tool for converting GF-D → Level 2.

## 4. LEVEL 3

**The Non-Verbal, Resigned Mom** | ~25% audience | Tag: `avatar_level3`
**Profile:** Child 6-10+y, non-verbal/minimal, high support needs. 20% hope. 5+ years of attempts.

### 4.1 Level 3 × Layer 2 (Customer)

| Customer Attribute | Level 3 Expression | Key Difference from Other Levels |
|--------------------|-------------------|----------------------------------|
| Mom Age | 35-48 | Oldest cohort |
| Employment | 30% working / 60% SAHM / full-time caregiver | Lowest employment — full-time caregiving |
| Income | Lower-middle dominant, ~40% Medicaid | Most financially vulnerable |
| Education | ~55% college-educated | Lowest (correlates with caregiving demands) |
| Single Mom | ~20-25% (highest) | Most likely to be solo parent |
| Child Age | 6-10+ years (no deadline, no urgency) | No external timeline |
| Child Gender | Boys heavily overrepresented | — |
| Therapy Load | Full spectrum: BCBA+OT+Speech+GI specialists+Seizure specialists | Highest medical coordination burden |
| Pull-up Costs | $1,500-5,000+ wasted on methods + specialists | Financially devastating |
| Comorbidities | SPD ~98%, ID ~50%, Seizure ~25%, GI ~60-70%, Sleep ~70% | Most medically complex |
| Medicaid Usage | ~40% | Most reliant on public systems |

**Mom Surface Identity:** "Caregiver of a severely disabled child" — NOT "potty-training mom." Identity has shifted from parent-as-trainer to parent-as-caregiver.

**The Dignity Economy:** Level 3 moms cannot be marketed to with standard e-commerce frameworks. Every dollar is a sacrifice. Every failed product is trauma. The email tone must be dignity-first, not sales-first.

### 4.2 Level 3 × Layer 5 (Emotional Dimensions)

| Dimension | Intensity | Level 3 Expression | Conversion Implication |
|-----------|:---------:|--------------------|-----------------------|
| **Deadline Pressure** | ★★☆☆ (Low) | No Pre-K deadline. Puberty/hygiene crisis creates a different kind of urgency. "Adult diapers next" fear. | Low — but the "adult diapers approaching" trigger is powerful |
| **Social Pressure** | ★★★★ (High) | Deep chronic shame — 6-9 years accumulated. "He's 9 in pull-ups." Stranger stares. Identity collapse. | Powerful but fragile — wrong messaging wounds deeply |
| **Overwhelmed** | ★★★★ (Very High) | Physical exhaustion from older/heavier child (65lbs+). Daily meltdowns. Medical overwhelm. 7+ years of it. | Strongest — relief is the only purchase driver |

**Dominant Combination:** Overwhelmed + Social Pressure (Phase 4, Level 3) — "The Long Game"
- Conversion: 4-8% (lowest conversion rate)
- BUT highest LTV — once converted, most loyal customer
- Emotional cocktail: Exhaustion (years, physical, financial) + Deep Shame (identity collapse)
- Email strategy: L4 (Lena's empathy — softest entry) → L1 (gentle mechanism) → L3 (no-pressure guarantee)

**Unique Level 3 Dynamic:** This is the ONLY level where hope is not a primary driver. Level 3 moms do not buy because they believe — they buy because they've run out of options and it's one more thing to check off. The "last attempt" purchase psychology is fundamentally different.

**The Adult Diaper Trigger:** The transition from size 7 pull-ups to adult diapers is a purchase crisis point. It is ALSO a trauma point. Emails that reference this must do so with extreme care — never as urgency, always as acknowledgment.

### 4.3 Level 3 × Layer 6 (Symptom Clusters)

| Cluster | Recognition | Level 3 Specificity | Email Implication |
|---------|:-----------:|---------------------|-------------------|
| **A: Complete Signal Absence** | ~90% | "Never shown a single sign in 8 years" | Most painful cluster — handle with dignity |
| **B: Sensory Overload Bathroom** | ~75% | "Meltdowns from toilet seat texture," "self-injury from flushing sound" | Acknowledge without dramatizing |
| **C: Withholding + Severe GI** | ~70% | "Hospitalized for impaction," "Miralax for years" | NO promises — medical team is primary |
| **D: Communication Lack** | ~85% | "Non-verbal, can't request bathroom," "AAC doesn't cover body needs" | Validate double barrier: signal + communication |
| **E: Physical Resistance** | ~60% | "Kicked me in the face," "two adults to change him" | Most painful — emotionally sensitive |
| **F: Size/Progression Fear** | ~80% | "Outgrown size 7," "adult diapers next," "can't lift him anymore" | Purchase trigger — acknowledge with dignity |

**Recommended Cluster Combinations for Level 3 Emails:**
- Welcome E1: Cluster A + Cluster F (gently — the "adult diapers approaching" trigger)
- Welcome E2: Cluster A + Cluster B (mechanism focus)
- Cart Recovery: Cluster A + Cluster D (validation, not sales)
- FAQ: Cluster B + Cluster C (sensory + GI — medical legitimacy)

**Cross-Level Insight:** Level 3 has the MOST specific clusters — they are almost entirely unique to Level 3. Cluster A (Complete Signal Absence), E (Physical Resistance), and F (Size Fear) exist ONLY at Level 3. This means Level 3 emails must carry content that NO other level needs.

### 4.4 Level 3 × Layer 21 (5 Sub-Groups)

| Sub-Group | Overlap with Level 3 | Strength | Migration Path |
|-----------|---------------------|----------|----------------|
| **A — Diagnostic Entrants** | None | 0% | Level 3 is diagnosed and severe |
| **B — Diagnosis-Rejecters** | Very Low | <5% | Hard to reject label when child needs 24/7 care |
| **C — Sensory-Difference Parents** | Low | <10% | SPD is real but not the dominant identity |
| **D — Overwhelmed Non-Selectors** | **Very High** | ~40% of Level 3 | Most Level 3 moms in GF are here — too overwhelmed to select |
| **E — Cart/Browse Without Opt-In** | Low-Medium | ~10% | Longer research cycle, less impulse buying |

**Key Overlap:** Level 3 and GF-D (Overwhelmed Non-Selectors) have the highest overlap. A Level 3 mom who didn't engage with the pop-up is not rejecting the brand — she is too exhausted for forms. Her child is likely Level 3 but she sits in General Fallback because she clicked away.

**The Silent Majority:** Level 3 is underrepresented in Kommentare (13.8% tagged vs ~25% model estimate) AND in pop-up selection. A significant portion of Level 3 audience exists in General Fallback as Sub-Group D, invisible to level-specific targeting.

**Migration strategy:** The "Non-verbal or high support needs" button in the Day 14 detection mail IS the Level 3 migration tool. Open rate 65-75% on that specific mail; ~40-50% of GF-D with Level 3 needs will self-select.

## 5. GENERAL FALLBACK

**The Cross-Level Catchment** | ~50% of email traffic | Tag: `avatar_general`
**No single profile — 5 distinct sub-groups, all reachable through universal language.**

### 5.1 General Fallback × Layer 2 (Customer)

| Customer Attribute | GF Expression | Key Difference from Level-Specific |
|--------------------|--------------|------------------------------------|
| Mom Age | 27-48 (broad) | Covers full range |
| Employment | 30-70% working (varies by sub-group) | Most diverse employment |
| Income | Variable across sub-groups | Most economically diverse |
| Education | Variable | Most education-diverse |
| Child Age | 2-10+ years | Broadest age range |
| Diagnosis Status | None/undiagnosed/suspected/SPD-only | No diagnosis required |
| Therapy Load | Variable | Unpredictable |
| Pop-Up Engagement | Skipped or not seen | Zero or minimal engagement |
| Level Awareness | None or rejecting | No level identification |

**The GF Customer Is Not One Person:** GF contains five different mothers with five different reasons for being in this category. The only thing they share: they did NOT choose Level 1, 2, or 3 on the pop-up.

**Identity Spectrum (from Layer 21):**
- GF-A: "My kid might have something — I don't know what yet" (Diagnostic Entrant)
- GF-B: "My kid is just my kid — I don't do labels" (Diagnosis-Rejecter)
- GF-C: "My kid has SPD/ADHD — not autism" (Sensory-Difference Parent)
- GF-D: "I'm too exhausted to fill out another form" (Overwhelmed Non-Selector)
- GF-E: "I just added to cart — I didn't see any pop-up" (Cart Abandoner)

### 5.2 General Fallback × Layer 5 (Emotional Dimensions)

| Dimension | GF Expression | Sub-Group Dominance |
|-----------|--------------|---------------------|
| **Deadline Pressure** | Present in GF-A (just starting research), absent in GF-B/C/D/E | GF-A dominant |
| **Social Pressure** | Strong in GF-B (defensiveness), medium in GF-C (identity), loudest in GF-D (exhaustion) | GF-B, GF-D |
| **Overwhelmed** | Overwhelmingly dominant in GF-D and GF-A (early overwhelm) | GF-D dominant |

**The GF-D (Overwhelmed Non-Selectors) Emotional Profile:**
- Emotion: Total exhaustion — same as Level 3 Overwhelmed
- But trapped in GF because she didn't choose a level
- This is the HIGHEST priority GF dimension to serve

**GF Emotional Rule:** GF emails must carry such broad emotional calibration that any Level-specific trigger works for the sub-group it's meant for without alienating the others. The "Whether...Or...If..." structure from Layer 21 is the technical solution.

### 5.3 General Fallback × Layer 6 (Symptom Clusters)

| Symptom Type | GF Applicability | Why It Works for All Sub-Groups |
|-------------|:----------------:|----------------------------------|
| Universal Timing Problems | ★★★★★ | Every child at every level can have timing issues |
| "Plays while peeing" | ★★★★★ | Cross-level universal |
| "Doesn't care about wetness" | ★★★★★ | Cross-level universal |
| "Almost trained — multiple times" | ★★★★☆ | Most relevant to L1/L2, but relatable |
| "Holds BM until pull-up" | ★★★★☆ | Most relevant to L2/L3, but recognizable |
| "Sensory defensiveness" | ★★★☆☆ | Specific to L2/L3 — use as secondary hook |
| "Complete signal absence" | ★★★☆☆ | Specific to L3 — use gently, never as dominant |

**GF Symptom Strategy:** Lead with the 3 universal symptoms (Timing, "Plays while peeing," "Doesn't care about wetness") that EVERY parent recognizes regardless of level. Layer in level-specific symptoms as secondary anchors.

**Forbidden:** Lead with Level 3-specific symptoms (Complete Signal Absence, Physical Resistance, Adult Diaper Fear) in GF emails — they will alienate GF-A, GF-B, GF-C who are not at that stage.

### 5.4 General Fallback × The 5 Sub-Groups (Layer 21 Detail)

| Sub-Group | Share of GF | Share of Total | Dominant Level | Core Need | Language Style |
|-----------|:-----------:|:--------------:|:--------------:|-----------|----------------|
| **A — Diagnostic Entrants** | ~25% | ~10% | L1 | Education about what might be going on | Gentle, exploratory, no-label |
| **B — Diagnosis-Rejecters** | ~15% | ~6% | L1/L2 | Help without diagnostic terms | Symptom-first, identity-respectful |
| **C — Sensory-Difference Parents** | ~25% | ~10% | L2 | Inclusion without ASD label | Science-forward, mechanism-oriented |
| **D — Overwhelmed Non-Selectors** | ~25% | ~10% | L2/L3 | Low-pressure, no assumptions | Gentle, broad, migration-ready |
| **E — Cart/Browse Without Opt-In** | ~10% | ~14% | All | Concise recovery + symptom recognition | Action-oriented, universal |

**Cross-Sub-Group Crossover Patterns (from Layer 21):**
- GF-A → may also have GF-B tendencies (resists the label)
- GF-C → may receive ASD diagnosis and shift to Level 2
- GF-D → may have Level 3 child but never engage with level selection
- GF-E → could be any sub-group — cart action tells us nothing about psychology

**The "Whether...Or...If..." Solution (Layer 21 DNA):**
Every GF email uses this structure to reach all 5 sub-groups simultaneously:
```
"Whether [A's situation], or [C's situation] — if [universal symptom], here's what most parents miss."
```

**Example:** "Whether you're 3 weeks into wondering, or 3 years into trying — if your kid isn't progressing like 'every method' promises — here's what most parents miss."

## 8. KEY CROSS-REFERENCE INSIGHTS

### 8.1 The Level 1 / GF-A Overlap (Highest Yield Migration Path)

Level 1 and GF-A (Diagnostic Entrants) describe the SAME mom at different stages of diagnosis awareness. She enters through GF-A (wondering, no diagnosis), receives the education content, either gets a diagnosis or self-identifies, and migrates to Level 1. **This is the highest-yield migration path in the entire system** because:
- GF-A represents ~10% of total audience (~25% of GF)
- Level 1-P2 has 15-25% conversion rate
- Migration cost: zero — she self-selects through content engagement

**Email Implication:** GF-A mails should contain at least one "verbal kid story" anchor per sequence that naturally leads to Level 1 content.

### 8.2 The Level 2 / GF-C / GF-D Triple Overlap (Largest Volume)

Level 2 is the largest avatar segment (~40%) AND GF-C (~25% of GF) and GF-D (~25% of GF) are predominantly Level 2 mothers who haven't self-selected. Combined, Level 2 + GF-C + GF-D represent the single largest addressable audience:
- Level 2 explicit: ~40% × audience
- GF-C (Level 2 mapped): ~10% × audience (25% of GF × 50% total traffic)
- GF-D (Level 2 mapped): ~10% × audience
- **Total Level 2 addressable: ~60% of total paying audience**

**Email Implication:** The Welcome flow must serve Level 2 content to BOTH explicit Level 2 tags AND GF-C/GF-D non-selectors. The symptom-based detection (sensory defensiveness + schedule dependency) should be the primary migration tool.

### 8.3 The Level 3 / GF-D Silence Problem

Level 3 is the most underrepresented avatar in the data:
- Only 13.8% of Kommentare tagged (vs ~25% model estimate)
- Significantly undercounted in pop-up selection (GF-D is mostly Level 3)
- Underrepresented in active purchase conversations
- **BUT highest LTV of any segment**

The problem: Level 3 moms are invisible to the marketing system. They don't comment, don't select pop-ups, don't engage with education content. They buy reactively, triggered by specific crisis events (size progression, GI crisis, puberty approaching).

**Email Implication:** A GF-D detection mechanism must exist independently of pop-up selection. The Day 14 Post-Purchase Level-Detection Mail is the only reliable tool. If the "Non-verbal or high support needs" button is not clicked, Level 3 content must still appear in the Post-Purchase flow for GF-D recipients.

### 8.4 The Sub-Group B Identity Problem

Sub-Group B (Diagnosis-Rejecters, ~6% of total audience) is the hardest sub-group to serve. They resist Level tags AND GF mechanisms:
- They may refuse to click migration buttons (label resistance)
- They unsubscribe if marketing language assumes ASD
- They have NO natural migration path — they may stay in GF forever
- **BUT they are profitable** — they have the same symptoms and needs

**Email Implication:** GF emails must ALWAYS carry Sub-Group B-compatible language. The "Whether...Or...If..." universal structure is the solution. Never assume ASD identity in GF content. The "Whatever you call this — or don't call it" opener from Layer 21 is the master opener.

### 9.3 The "Three Lanes" Architecture

The cross-reference reveals that the BrightKidCo email program operates in three parallel lanes:

**Lane 1: Explicit Level Selection (30-50% of traffic)**
- Tags: `avatar_level1`, `avatar_level2`, `avatar_level3`
- Content: Level-specific symptom clusters, calibration, testimonials
- Highest conversion per email
- **Risk:** Misses GF audience entirely

**Lane 2: General Fallback Universal (50%+ of traffic)**
- Tags: `avatar_general` (pop-up selected) or no tag
- Content: "Whether...Or...If..." universal structure, all 5 sub-groups served
- Lower conversion per email, higher volume
- **Risk:** Too generic for Level-specific needs

**Lane 3: GF-D Overwhelmed (hidden segment within GF)**
- No explicit tag — detected through Day 14 Post-Purchase Detection Mail + symptom proxies
- Content: Relief-first, dignity-maintaining, migration opportunities
- Lowest conversion rate, highest LTV
- **Risk:** Invisible to the system if detection mail is not implemented

### 9.4 Highest-Impact Actions

1. **Implement GF → Level migration through Day 14 Post-Purchase Detection Mail.** This single mechanism addresses the largest gap (GF-D + GF-C hidden in General Fallback). Open rate 65-75% → 40-50% migration rate.

2. **Build "Deadline Pressure" email variant for Welcome E3.** This captures the Level 1 + GF-A highest-conversion combination. L1→L4→L3 sequence. Subject line: "Before September — you have more time than you think."

3. **Add sensory symptom hooks to ALL GF emails.** Cluster L2-A (Sensory Defensiveness) and L1-A (Timing Problems) are the two highest-recognition universal clusters. Every GF email should contain at least one sensory hook and one timing hook.

4. **Build the "Whether...Or...If..." universal structure into the General Fallback flow template.** This is the technical solution that serves all 5 sub-groups simultaneously. The master template should include: Universal Recognition opener → Specific Symptoms (2-3) → Mechanism explanation → Universal Permission CTA.
