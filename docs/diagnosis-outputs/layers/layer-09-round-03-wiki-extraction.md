## 2. VOICE GUIDELINES EXTRACTED FROM WIKI

### 2.1 S.C.E. Framework (Max Sturtevant — Ecom Email Copywriting)

The wiki contains a detailed ecom email copywriting guide that defines three core principles:

**S — Skimmable:**
- "Optimize for the skim, not the read"
- Avoid blocks of text — they scare people off
- Bold main points — most audience won't read copy, but will read bolded points
- Use body copy to support main points for readers who want more
- Short paragraphs (1-3 sentences)

**C — Clear and Concise:**
- "The customer's IQ drops by about 50% the second they open their email inbox"
- "Zombie brain mode" — if not clear, they won't understand; if not concise, they won't bother
- Short and sweet: email shouldn't be longer than 1-2 scrolls
- ONE takeaway per email — higher odds of being remembered

**E — Engaging:**
- Customers crave dopamine (TikTok/Instagram raised the bar)
- Punchy: short but powerful, information-dense
- Informational: people love to learn, facts drive engagement
- Entertaining: jokes, puns, puzzles — avoid repetitiveness

**Key Voice Implication for Lena:**
- Lena's emails need to be scannable even when long-form (paragraph breaks, bold reading path)
- One core message per email aligns with Lena's mechanism-first approach
- "Does this push the customer closer to a sale?" filter — but for Lena, it's "does this push the mom closer to understanding?"

### 2.2 Subject Line Framework (Wiki Course Guide)

**Subject Line Rules:**
- 2-5 words in length
- Title Case
- Builds curiosity
- Ends with 1-2 emojis (optional)
- Think of SL as headline, PT as subheadline
- Don't give away all info — hint, but require the open

**Preview Text Rules:**
- One full sentence long
- Regular capitalization
- Adds support to the SL
- Ends with "..." (optional)

**Revenue-Based Testing:**
- "Different SLs and PTs will change the frame of your email and prime the customer for sales in different ways"
- "We've had some SLs and PTs generate 3x more than others (same email copy and design)"
- "Test your SL styles based off revenue, and less about opens"
- Goal: get people to open based on SENDER NAME, not SL tricks

**Key Voice Implication for Lena:**
- Lena's subject lines should be scenario-based curiosity hooks, not urgency or benefit-driven
- Short SLs (2-5 words) work: "The signal gap." / "Still thinking it over?" / "Week 4 looks different"
- Preview text extends the scenario, doesn't repeat the SL
- Revenue testing applies: test Lena's SL approaches by conversion, not open rate

### 2.3 Copy as Emotion (Joseph Sugarman — Adweek Ch. 11)

**Three Emotion Principles:**
1. Every word has an emotion associated with it and tells a story
2. Every good ad is an emotional outpouring of words, feelings and impressions
3. You sell on emotion, but you justify a purchase with logic

**Word-Level Emotional Impact:**
- "Look at a dictionary not as a collection of words but as a collection of short stories"
- Single word changes can increase response 20%+ (John Caples: "repair" → "fix")
- Example: "old woman in the motel" vs "little old lady in the cottage" — same facts, different emotion
- "Cottage" gave copy a better emotional feel than "motel office"

**Emotional Selling Process:**
- Create emotional environment first, then justify with facts
- Logic becomes less important when reader is in emotional frame
- "If you aren't absolutely satisfied, return your product within 30 days for a prompt and courteous refund" — "courteous" makes no logical sense but conveys feeling of respect

**Key Voice Implication for Lena:**
- Lena sells on emotion (peer connection, "I know that one") and justifies with logic (interoception, SPARK stats)
- Word choice matters enormously: "signal" > "problem", "nervous system" > "behavior", "support" > "fix"
- Lena's emotional outpouring IS the trust mechanism — rough edges, not polished prose

### 2.4 Personal Communication (Joseph Sugarman — Adweek Ch. 15)

**Axiom 12:** "Every communication should be a personal one, from the writer to the recipient, regardless of the medium used."

**Key Principles:**
- Write as if writing to a single individual, not addressing a crowd
- "From me to you. Period."
- Use I, you, me — personal words for one-to-one conversation
- Avoid hiding behind a podium speaking to masses
- Byline technique: use a name to enable first-person voice
- Even in print ads (mass medium), write to one person

**Comparison Example:**
- Corporate: "We at ABC Company wish to invite all of you to visit our exhibit"
- Personal: "Hi. You might remember me from the last trade show. Well, I'd like to invite you to the next one where I will be looking forward to meeting you again"

**Key Voice Implication for Lena:**
- Lena already does this perfectly — she writes as one mom to another
- "If you reply, you get me" is exactly Sugarman's principle
- The byline is "Lena ——" not "BrightKidCo Team"
- Every email should feel like a letter from a friend, not a company communication
