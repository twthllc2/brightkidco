## 3. CONTRADICTIONS AND TENSIONS LOG

### Contradiction 1: Sub-Group E Share Math

**Tension:** R1 states Sub-Group E is "~10% of GF" but "~14% of total." Since GF is ~50% of total, E being 10% of GF would make it ~5% of total, not 14%.

**Possible explanations:**
- The "~14% of total" refers to a different denominator (all email subscribers, not just real email traffic)
- The "~10% of GF" is understated
- The figures come from different measurement periods

**Impact on email strategy:** LOW — doesn't change how we message to E, but affects budget allocation and priority decisions. Needs clarification from source data.

**Resolution needed:** Confirm whether E is 10% of GF (~5% of total) or 14% of total (~28% of GF). These are very different strategic priorities.

---

### Contradiction 2: Sub-Group B Experience Level vs. Engagement Capacity

**Tension:** R1 describes B as having "Phase 2-3 experience" (years of methods, deep frustration) and R4 says she has "the EXPERIENCE of a Level 2-3 parent." But she's only ~15% of GF and has the LOWEST migration likelihood. If she's experienced and frustrated, why would she stay subscribed to a General Fallback email flow?

**Possible explanations:**
- She stays subscribed because the symptom content is useful even without label acceptance
- She's actually less experienced than described (Phase 1-2, not 2-3)
- She disengages quickly and the 15% figure is entry rate, not active rate

**Impact on email strategy:** MEDIUM — if B subscribers churn fast, the long-term GF audience is really A+C+D, which changes lever prioritization. GF-D ("Not just for autism") becomes less critical if B isn't actually reading.

**Resolution needed:** Track Sub-Group B engagement over 30/60/90 days. If B churns within 30 days, the long-term GF strategy should focus on A, C, and D.

---

### Contradiction 3: Trust Pyramid Standard vs. Modified Orders

**Tension:** R4 documents that each sub-group has a DIFFERENT trust pyramid order (B needs T3-first, C needs T1-first, E needs T5-first). But R5 says every mail must serve ALL sub-groups simultaneously. You can't have 5 different trust orders in one email.

**Possible explanations:**
- The trust tiers aren't sequential within a single email — they're all present but weighted differently
- Sub-group-specific flows will eventually replace the unified GF flow
- The "Whether...Or...If..." pattern implicitly sequences tiers for different readers

**Impact on email strategy:** HIGH — this is a fundamental design tension. If the trust pyramid must be inverted for B (T3-first) and E (T5-first), but the mail structure formula starts with Universal Recognition (T2/T3), there's a conflict for E who needs T5 first.

**Resolution needed:** Accept that a unified GF mail cannot optimize trust order for all 5 sub-groups simultaneously. Design for the MAJORITY (A+C+D = 75% of GF) and accept suboptimal trust sequencing for B (15%) and E (10%). E gets its own cart recovery flow anyway.

---

### Contradiction 4: Sub-Group C as "Highly Profitable" vs. "Low Migration"

**Tension:** R1 says C is "OFTEN overlooked, but is highly profitable — she has money, education, and mechanism understanding." R5 says migration likelihood is "Low — unlikely to migrate to ASD levels; may stay General permanently."

**The tension:** If C is high-value but stays in General forever, the General flow must be designed to CONVERT her within the General segment, not migrate her out. But the entire migration strategy assumes General is temporary.

**Impact on email strategy:** HIGH — C requires a different conversion path than D. D migrates and then converts in level-specific flows. C must convert IN the General flow. This means the General flow needs purchase-oriented sequences, not just education + migration.

**Resolution needed:** Design a "convert-in-place" sub-flow for C within General Fallback. This likely means: education → mechanism → intellectual conviction → purchase CTA, without migration as intermediate step.

---

### Contradiction 5: Lever 2 Caution for Sub-Group E

**Tension:** R1 says "Caution with Lever 2 ('Pull-ups make this worse') — she has no context yet." R5 reiterates this. But E is captured via CART — she was about to BUY the product. She has enough context to add it to her cart.

**Possible explanations:**
- "No context" means no context for WHY pull-ups are the villain, not no context about the product
- Cart addition was impulse, not informed
- The caution is about email framing, not product awareness

**Impact on email strategy:** LOW-MEDIUM — but worth testing. If E already understands the pull-ups villain (she was at cart), L2 might actually be effective for her.

**Resolution needed:** A/B test L2 inclusion vs. exclusion in cart recovery emails.

## 4. PRIORITY-ORDERED RESEARCH RECOMMENDATIONS

### Priority 1: Day 14 Level-Detection Mail Validation (CRITICAL)

**Gap:** Sub-Group D migration projection (40-50%) is entirely hypothetical.
**Why it's #1:** This single email determines whether 25% of GF (10% of total traffic) migrates to level-specific flows. If migration is 40%, the level-specific flows get a massive subscriber injection. If it's 10%, the GF flow must do more heavy lifting.
**Research needed:**
- Design and deploy the Day 14 mail in first GF wave
- Track: open rate, click rate, which button clicked, subsequent migration to level-specific segment
- A/B test: 4-button version vs. 2-button version (simplified choice)
- Measure 30-day post-migration engagement vs. non-migrated GF subscribers
**Timeline:** First 30 days of GF email deployment
**Cost:** Low — requires Klaviyo flow setup and tracking only

---

### Priority 2: Sub-Group Identification via Behavioral Signals (HIGH)

**Gap:** No mechanism exists to determine which sub-group a GF subscriber actually belongs to.
**Why it's #2:** Without sub-group identification, all optimization is theoretical. You can't A/B test sub-group-specific approaches if you don't know who's in which sub-group.
**Research needed:**
- Design click-based sub-group detection: different links in GF emails reveal sub-group (e.g., "Is your child diagnosed?" link reveals A vs. B vs. D)
- Track search terms that led to sign-up (UTM parameters, Klaviyo integration)
- Use purchase-based hints (underwear size → age → level hint for D)
- Monitor reply content for sub-group signals
**Timeline:** Design in parallel with first GF wave, deploy in wave 2
**Cost:** Low-Medium — requires Klaviyo segment setup and link tracking

---

### Priority 3: Sub-Group B Engagement Tracking (HIGH)

**Gap:** Whether diagnosis-rejecters actually stay subscribed and engage, or churn within 30 days.
**Why it's #3:** If B churns fast, the entire GF-D lever strategy ("Not just for autism") is wasted on a disappearing audience. If B stays, GF-D is the highest-leverage element for 40% of GF (B+C combined).
**Research needed:**
- Track B subscriber engagement over 30/60/90 days
- Compare B open/click rates vs. A, C, D, E
- Identify B-specific churn point (which email do they drop off?)
- A/B test: GF-D inclusion vs. exclusion to measure impact on B retention
**Timeline:** First 90 days of GF deployment
**Cost:** Low — requires segment tracking only

### Priority 4: Adapted 49% Stat Verification (MEDIUM)

**Gap:** The claim "For kids with sensory differences of any kind, the percentage [facing toileting challenges] is similar [to 49%]" has no cited source.

**Timeline:** Before first GF wave deployment
**Cost:** Medium — requires literature search or expert consultation

## 6. SUB-GROUP MERGE/SPLIT CANDIDATES

### Potential Mergers

**B + C → "Non-ASD Parents" (currently 15% + 25% = 40% of GF)**

Arguments FOR merging:
- Both exist outside the ASD framework
- Both respond to GF-D ("Not just for autism")
- Both need symptom language, never label
- Combined would be the single largest sub-group

Arguments AGAINST merging:
- B rejects ALL labels (including "neurodivergent"); C embraces "neurodivergent parent"
- B has Phase 2-3 experience; C has Phase 2-3 demographics but different psychology
- B needs T3-first (failure acknowledgment); C needs T1-first (science)
- Merging loses the intellectual buyer profile that makes C high-value

**Verdict: DO NOT MERGE.** The identity difference (label-rejecting vs. label-adopting) is too fundamental. Merging would force language that alienates one or the other.

---

### Potential Splits

**Sub-Group D → Split by actual level (D1, D2, D3)**

Arguments FOR splitting:
- D contains subscribers with levels 1, 2, and 3 — very different needs
- Migration from D→Level-specific would create natural splits
- Level-specific content within D would be more targeted

Arguments AGAINST splitting:
- We don't know their levels (that's the whole point of D)
- Splitting before identification is impossible
- The Day 14 mail IS the split mechanism

**Verdict: DO NOT SPLIT NOW.** Let the Day 14 mail do the splitting. If migration fails (Priority 1 gap), consider forced segmentation via behavioral signals.

---

### Potential New Sub-Group

**"The Late Discovery Parents" — currently absorbed into A**

These are parents whose child is 5-8+ and they're just now realizing something is different. They don't fit A (which assumes child 2-4, first child) and don't fit B (which assumes diagnosis exists but is rejected). They're pre-diagnostic with an OLDER child.

Arguments FOR creating:
- Different triggers than A (school problems vs. pediatrician comments)
- Different urgency level (developmental window closing)
- Different content needs (not "early signs" but "what now?")

Arguments AGAINST creating:
- May be too small to justify separate treatment
- Current A profile could expand to include them
- Symptom hooks already work across ages

**Verdict: MONITOR.** Track whether A subscribers with children 5+ show different engagement patterns than A subscribers with children 2-4. If divergence is significant, split in Layer 21 v2.

## KEY FINDING

The single most important gap is **validation**. Every sub-group profile, lever rating, trust sequence, and conversion projection in Layer 21 is derived from qualitative client descriptions and generic industry frameworks. Zero BrightKidCo-specific behavioral data exists.

**Recommendation:** Design the first General Fallback wave as an EXPERIMENT, not a final product. Include:
1. Sub-group detection mechanisms (click-based, reply-based)
2. A/B tests on key assumptions (trust sequences, lever effectiveness, adapted stats)
3. Tracking infrastructure for all Priority 1-3 gaps
4. A 90-day review checkpoint to revise sub-group profiles based on actual data

The first wave is the first real data. Treat it accordingly.
