## 5. KEY FINDINGS AND PATTERNS ACROSS ALL CROSS-REFERENCES

### 5.1 The "Inclusion Paradox"

Every cross-reference reveals the same paradox: General Fallback emails must be specific enough to resonate with 5 very different sub-groups, but generic enough to not alienate any of them. The "Whether...Or...If..." pattern (Layer 20) solves this structurally, but the Trust Pyramid (Layer 13) and Conversion Levers (Layer 10) must also adapt.

**Finding:** The 5 sub-groups require different Trust Pyramid entry points:
- Sub-Group A enters at T1 (mechanism education)
- Sub-Group B enters at T1 (mechanism, label-free)
- Sub-Group C enters at T1 (mechanism, science-first)
- Sub-Group D enters at T1 (mechanism, cross-level)
- Sub-Group E enters at T5 (guarantee) — REVERSED

This means the standard Trust Pyramid order (T1→T2→T3→T4→T5) works for 4 of 5 sub-groups but must be reversed for Sub-Group E (Cart/Browse). Since Sub-Group E is only ~10% of GF, the standard order should dominate with the guarantee emphasized early as a secondary thread.

### 5.2 Sub-Group B Is the Hardest to Serve

Across all 4 layers, Sub-Group B (Diagnosis-Rejecters) consistently presents the most challenges:

- **Layer 4:** "No clean overlap with any Level — Levels 1/2/3 all assume diagnosis acceptance"
- **Layer 10:** L4 (Lena) requires caution — "autism mom" framing triggers rejection
- **Layer 13:** T3 (failure acknowledgment) is critical but must avoid diagnosis language
- **Layer 20:** Pattern C ("Whatever you call this") is specifically designed for this sub-group

**Finding:** Sub-Group B requires a dedicated language subset that never uses: "autism mom," "autistic child," "Level 1/2/3," "diagnosis," or any ASD-specific framing. The only safe language is symptom-based: "kids who process the world differently," "whatever you call this," "for kids whose nervous systems work differently."

### 5.3 Sub-Group C Is the Most Profitable Overlooked Segment

> L21 R1: "This group is OFTEN overlooked, but is highly profitable — she has money, education, and mechanism understanding."

Cross-reference confirms:
- **Layer 4:** Demographics overlap with Level 2 (30-42, ~75% college-educated, higher income)
- **Layer 10:** GF-D ("Not just for autism") is her primary entry lever — "For ADHD kids. For SPD kids."
- **Layer 13:** T1 (mechanism) must reference Kelly Mahler and interoception research explicitly
- **Layer 20:** Pattern E (symptom list) matches her OT vocabulary perfectly

**Finding:** Sub-Group C has the highest conversion potential within General Fallback if messaged correctly. She has: money, education, mechanism understanding, and a practical problem (potty training). She just needs to see the product positioned for "sensory differences" (not just autism).

### 5.4 Sub-Group D Is the Migration Goldmine

> L21 R1: "Has a REAL level (1, 2, or 3) — this is the key difference from other sub-groups."

Cross-reference confirms:
- **Layer 4:** Day 14 Level-Detection Mail: 65-75% open rate, 35-50% click rate, ~40-50% migration
- **Layer 10:** Migration anchors in every mail — Three-Family Testimonial Pattern
- **Layer 13:** Trust builds through self-discovery — she recognizes her own level
- **Layer 20:** ALL patterns work because the audience is mixed

**Finding:** Sub-Group D represents the highest-value conversion opportunity because she already has a level — she just didn't communicate it. The Day 14 Level-Detection Mail is the single most important email in the entire General Fallback flow for this sub-group.

### 5.5 The Universal Symptom Hooks Are the Glue

All 4 layers converge on the same finding: universal symptom hooks are what make General Fallback work across all 5 sub-groups.

> L21 R1: "Priority 1: 'Kids who sit on the toilet for 20 minutes and nothing happens' → Hits all sub-groups"

> L20: "Pattern E (Symptom-OR-List) is the universal foundation — every level finds their symptom in the list."

> L13: "Tier 1 (Mechanism Specificity) is the foundation — most important trust element."

> L10: "GF-A: 'For any kid whose signal doesn't come' — universal signal mechanism."

**Finding:** The symptom list IS the mechanism. Each symptom described in Pattern E is a doorway to Lever 1 ("It's not the method"). The symptom hooks simultaneously build trust (T1), validate failure (T3), and activate conversion (L1). They are the single most operationally important content element in General Fallback emails.

### 5.6 The "Whether...Or...If..." Pattern Has a Blind Spot

Layer 20's pattern library covers journey length, verbal ability, age, diagnostic status, and method count. But it does NOT explicitly cover:

1. **Label identity** — Sub-Group B needs "whatever you call this" or "for kids who process differently"
2. **Non-ASD diagnosis** — Sub-Group C needs "ADHD, SPD, or sensory differences of any kind"
3. **Cart intent** — Sub-Group E needs "you were looking at this for a reason"

**Finding:** The "Whether...Or...If..." pattern needs 2 additional axis dimensions:
- Identity axis: "Whether you call it autism, ADHD, or just 'different'..."
- Intent axis: "Whether you've been researching for months or just clicked on something interesting..."

### 5.7 Layer-Specific Content Density Must Vary Per Mail

Each sub-group requires different amounts of each content type:

| Content Type | A Needs | B Needs | C Needs | D Needs | E Needs |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Education | HIGH | Medium | HIGH | Medium | LOW |
| Mechanism | Medium | HIGH | HIGH | Medium | LOW (abbreviated) |
| Validation | LOW | HIGH | Medium | HIGH | LOW |
| Guarantee | Medium | Medium | HIGH | Medium | HIGH |
| Migration | Medium | LOW | LOW | HIGH | Medium |

**Finding:** Every General Fallback mail must contain elements for ALL content types, but the proportion shifts. The mail structure formula from L21 R1 holds:

1. **Opener:** Universal Recognition (Pattern A or B) — all sub-groups
2. **Body:** Specific Symptoms (Pattern E) — all sub-groups self-select
3. **Why-Section:** Mechanism (Lever 1) — T1 trust building
4. **CTA:** Universal Permission (Pattern B closer) — "if this fits, here's the option"
