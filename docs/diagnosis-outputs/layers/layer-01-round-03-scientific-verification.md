## Complete Claim Inventory

### CLAIM 1: Body-Signal Learning Layer™ preserves gentle "uh-oh" sensation
**Status:** SCIENTIFICALLY BACKED (mechanism principle)
**Category:** Core Product Mechanism

**Analysis:** The fundamental principle — that sensory feedback (feeling wet) is necessary for the child to learn the connection between bladder signals and toileting — is well-established in behavior analysis and pediatric occupational therapy literature.

**Sources:**
- **Azrin & Foxx (1971)** — "A rapid method of toilet training the institutionalized retarded." *Journal of Applied Behavior Analysis*. Seminal work establishing that sensory awareness of accidents is part of the learning loop.
- **Bacotti, Perez & Vollmer (2023)** — "Reflections and Critical Directions for Toilet Training in Applied Behavior Analysis." *Perspectives on Behavior Science*, 46, 447-458. Confirms the behavioral model and explicitly identifies undergarment type as a research variable affecting training outcomes.
- **Simon & Thompson (2006)** — Cited in Bacotti et al. (2023) as investigating undergarment type effect on toilet training. Directly relevant to BrightKidCo's mechanism claim.
- **CLRN Analysis** — Frames potty training as a reinforcement learning problem where superabsorbent polymers in pull-ups blur the sensory feedback loop, slowing learning.

**Strength of Evidence:** Strong. The behavioral model is foundational in ABA. The specific claim that "pull-ups block the feedback loop" is supported by both historical and contemporary research.

**Recommended Email Language:** Can be stated as fact with citation to established behavioral science.

### CLAIM 2: Interoception support for autistic children
**Status:** SCIENTIFICALLY BACKED
**Category:** ASD-Specific Mechanism

**Analysis:** Interoception deficits are a well-studied phenomenon in autism. The claim that BrightKidCo helps autistic kids connect body signals through gentle feedback aligns with established OT approaches.

**Sources:**
- **Mahler, K. & Hample, K. (2022)** — "Impact of an Interoception-Based Program on Emotion Regulation in Autistic Children." *Occupational Therapy International*. Published peer-reviewed study showing that interoception-based interventions improve outcomes in autistic children. The Mahler Interoception Curriculum is used by OTs worldwide.
- **Kelly Mahler Blog** — "Interoception, Toileting, and Potty Training" (2021). Directly states interoception plays a key role in toileting and that many children's challenges are about body signal awareness, not behavior.
- **ERIC (UK)** — "Interoception and toileting" — Confirms interoception is integral to toileting success and that children can learn to recognize body signals through structured approaches.
- **University of Utah Health** — "Sensory Strategies for Potty Training" (PDF). Confirms the connection between interoceptors in bladder/bowels and the brain is necessary for toileting.
- **Garfinkel et al. (UCL)** — Multiple publications on interoception differences in autism spectrum disorder.

**Strength of Evidence:** Strong. Interoception-autism link is well-established in peer-reviewed literature. The application to toileting is supported by OT clinical practice and multiple sources.

**Recommended Email Language:** Can be stated as fact — interoception challenges in autism are well documented. Use hedging when claiming BrightKidCo *specifically* improves interoception (use "supports" or "may help").

### CLAIM 3: Gentle moisture feedback ("not soaked, not uncomfortable — just enough to trigger awareness")
**Status:** ANECDOTALLY SUPPORTED
**Category:** Product Design Claim

**Analysis:** The principle of "just noticeable difference" (JND) in sensory thresholds is established in psychophysics (Weber-Fechner law). However, there is no published peer-reviewed study validating that BrightKidCo's specific product achieves this level of feedback optimally. Parent testimonials strongly support this claim.

**Sources:**
- Multiple customer reviews describe exactly this phenomenon ("lets her feel it just enough," "light wet feedback helps her notice")
- No direct published study evaluating BrightKidCo's specific moisture feedback level
- OT literature supports gradual, predictable sensation as better for learning than overwhelming sensation

**Strength of Evidence:** Moderate. Logical and anecdotally validated. Formal third-party sensory testing would strengthen scientific backing.

**Recommended Email Language:** "The gentle wetness signal helps toddlers notice" — avoid implying it is scientifically measured at the perfect threshold.

### CLAIM 4: Hypoallergenic fabric
**Status:** REQUIRES LEGAL HEDGING
**Category:** Material Safety

**Analysis:** "Hypoallergenic" is an unregulated marketing term in textiles. There is no FDA or FTC standard definition for what constitutes "hypoallergenic" in children's underwear. 100% cotton IS associated with lower rates of contact dermatitis vs synthetics, but the absolute "hypoallergenic" claim is legally weak.

**Sources:**
- AAP guidelines recommend cotton for children with sensitive skin
- Journal of the American Academy of Dermatology — cotton less likely to cause contact dermatitis than synthetics
- However: no regulatory body certifies "hypoallergenic" for textiles

**Strength of Evidence:** Weak legal standing. The factual basis (cotton is gentler on skin) is supported, but the absolute claim is problematic.

**Recommended Email Language:** "Made from 100% cotton — gentle on sensitive skin" instead of "hypoallergenic."

### CLAIM 5: PFAS-Free
**Status:** SCIENTIFICALLY BACKED
**Category:** Material Safety

**Analysis:** PFAS are well-documented as health hazards with strong scientific consensus. BrightKidCo claiming PFAS-free is a defensible, verifiable claim IF third-party tested. The scientific evidence for PFAS harm is robust.

**Sources:**
- **Silent Spring Institute / Environmental Science & Technology** — Study showing many children's products labeled "green" still contain PFAS. This makes BrightKidCo's PFAS-free claim actually a competitive differentiator.
- **ChemTrust** — "New study finds banned 'forever chemicals' being used in children's clothing." Links PFAS to developmental issues, immune system disruption.
- **AAP Policy Statement** — Endorses minimizing PFAS exposure in children.
- **BLUESIGN** — PFAS in clothing: 2026 bans, health risks, and safer alternatives.
- **CDC / ATSDR** — PFAS health effects research: confirmed links to immune effects, cholesterol, thyroid, and developmental outcomes.

**Strength of Evidence:** Very strong for PFAS risks. BrightKidCo should maintain third-party testing documentation.

**Recommended Email Language:** Can state "PFAS-Free" as fact IF backed by test reports. Best practice: "Independently tested PFAS-Free."

### CLAIM 6: BPA-Free
**Status:** SCIENTIFICALLY BACKED
**Category:** Material Safety

**Analysis:** BPA is a well-established endocrine disruptor. AAP endorses minimizing BPA exposure. BPA in textiles is less common than in plastics, but claiming BPA-free is a defensible safety claim.

**Sources:**
- **AAP Policy Statement (2018, reaffirmed)** — "Bisphenol A and Children's Health." Recommends minimizing exposure.
- **FDA** — BPA safety: While FDA says current levels safe for food packaging, the agency has banned BPA in baby bottles and sippy cups.
- **NIH/NIEHS** — Links BPA to endocrine disruption, developmental effects.

**Strength of Evidence:** Strong for BPA risks. BPA-free in textiles is relatively easy to achieve (BPA is not a standard textile additive), making this a low-risk claim.

**Recommended Email Language:** "BPA-Free" is safe to state as fact.

### CLAIM 7: Chemical-Free
**Status:** REQUIRES LEGAL HEDGING — HIGH RISK
**Category:** Material Safety

**Analysis:** "Chemical-free" is a marketing claim with no scientific validity — everything is made of chemicals (water is H₂O). The FTC Green Guides explicitly caution against absolute "free of" claims that are technically untrue. This is the highest-risk claim in the entire inventory.

**Sources:**
- **FTC Green Guides (revised)** — Explicitly warn against unqualified "chemical-free" claims. "Free of" claims require the product to be free of ALL substances in a category, which is impossible for "chemicals."
- **NAD (National Advertising Division) rulings** — Multiple cases where "chemical-free" claims were successfully challenged.
- **EU Green Claims Directive** — Similar restrictions on absolute environmental claims.

**Strength of Evidence:** N/A — this is a legal/compliance issue, not a scientific one.

**Recommended Email Language:** REPLACE IMMEDIATELY. Use "free from harmful chemicals," "no harsh chemicals added," or specifically name what's absent ("No PFAS, BPA, or phthalates").

### CLAIM 12: Sensory-friendly / ultra-soft fabric
**Status:** SCIENTIFICALLY BACKED (for autism context)
**Category:** ASD-Specific Feature

**Analysis:** >90% of autistic children show atypical sensory processing. Soft, seamless, breathable fabrics (including cotton, bamboo, modal) are consistently recommended by OTs and supported by research for autistic individuals with tactile sensitivity.

- Machine washable
