## PSYCHOLOGY FRAMEWORKS EXTRACTED

### Framework 1: PAS — Problem-Agitate-Solve
**Source:** copy, copywriting-body-copy-frameworks
**Psychology Basis:** Mirrors buying decision psychology — recognition of problem → emotional connection to pain → desire for solution. Works because it activates loss aversion before introducing gain.
**Structure:**
1. Problem — Identify the customer's pain point or unmet need
2. Agitate — Make the problem feel worse (the cost of NOT solving it)
3. Solve — Present product as the natural solution

**Ethical Guardrail:** Agitation must be empathetic, not manipulative. For BrightKidCo, agitation means validating the mom's real experience — not inventing new fears.

---

### Framework 2: AIDA — Attention-Interest-Desire-Action
**Source:** copywriting-body-copy-frameworks
**Psychology Basis:** Classic marketing funnel adapted for email. Walks reader through logical progression from awareness to action. Leverages the attention-desire-action cognitive pathway.
**Structure:**
1. Attention — Strong opening that stops the scroll (bold claim, stat, question, story hook)
2. Interest — Build engagement by explaining relevance or context
3. Desire — Make them want it through benefits, features, social proof
4. Action — Clear, specific CTA

---

### Framework 3: BAB — Before-After-Bridge
**Source:** copy, copywriting-body-copy-frameworks
**Psychology Basis:** Persuasion through transformation narrative. Shows the world before the product, the world after, and how the product bridges the gap. Leverages aspirational psychology and visualization.
**Structure:**
1. Before — Describe the world before the product (the problem state)
2. After — Describe the world after the product (the ideal state)
3. Bridge — Show how the product gets them from Before to After

---

### Framework 4: Storytelling Arc — Hook → Conflict → Resolution → CTA
**Source:** copywriting-body-copy-frameworks, copywriting-brand-voice-storytelling
**Psychology Basis:** Humans are wired for narrative. A well-told story outperforms formulaic sales copy. Stories activate mirror neurons and create emotional identification.
**Structure:**
1. Hook — Opening line that makes them want to keep reading
2. Conflict — The tension or challenge (relatable or dramatic)
3. Resolution — How the brand or product resolved the conflict
4. CTA — Clear next step

**Sub-Frameworks:**
- Founder Story Arc: Before → Breaking Point → Struggle → Breakthrough → Mission
- Customer Story Framework: Situation → Problem → Discovery → Experience → Result → Quote

---

### Framework 5: FAB — Features-Advantages-Benefits
**Source:** copy
**Psychology Basis:** Translates product attributes into customer value. Bridges the gap between what the product IS and what it DOES for the customer. Leverages benefit-identification psychology.
**Structure:**
- Feature: What the product has/is
- Advantage: Why that matters
- Benefit: How it improves the customer's life

---

### Framework 6: The "So What?" Test
**Source:** copywriting-body-copy-frameworks
**Psychology Basis:** Ruthless editing tool based on relevance filtering. Every sentence must earn its place by passing a value test. Prevents cognitive overload and maintains attention.
**Value Tests:**
- Does this inform the reader?
- Does this build desire?
- Does this overcome an objection?
- Does this create urgency?
- Does this establish credibility?

### Framework 7: Voice Matrix (4-Axis Model)
**Source:** copywriting-brand-voice-storytelling, brand-voice-definition
**Psychology Basis:** Brand personality psychology. A brand's voice creates a psychological relationship with the reader. Consistency builds trust; inconsistency creates cognitive dissonance.
**Axes:**
1. Formal ↔ Casual
2. Serious ↔ Playful
3. Respectful ↔ Irreverent
4. Practical ↔ Aspirational

**For BrightKidCo (Lena voice):**
- Formal [2] Casual — conversational, peer-to-peer
- Serious [4] Playful — the topic is heavy, levity is rare
- Respectful [5] Irreverent — deeply respectful of the child and parent
- Practical [4] Aspirational — grounded in real outcomes, not fantasy

### Framework 8: Relationship Ladder (Voice Evolution)
**Source:** copywriting-brand-voice-storytelling

**Stages:**
1. NEW SUBSCRIBER: Formal, educational, value-first

### Framework 9: Objection Handling (Objection-CTA Sandwich)
**Source:** copywriting-brand-voice-storytelling
**Psychology Basis:** Objections are the silent reasons customers don't buy. Addressing them BEFORE the customer voices them removes psychological barriers. Uses pre-emptive reframing.
**Structure:**
1. Benefit / Hook
2. Price/value justification or guarantee
3. CTA
4. PS: re-state guarantee, urgency, or social proof

**Common Objection Types:**
- Price → Break down cost-per-use
- Timing → Create urgency, emphasize opportunity cost
- Trust (will it work?) → Guarantees, testimonials, certifications
- Trust (is this legit?) → Trust signals, secure checkout, reviews
- Commitment fear → Reduce risk: free trials, samples, easy returns

---

### Framework 10: ICE Scoring (Conversion Psychology)
**Source:** cro
**Psychology Basis:** Prioritization framework based on psychological impact assessment. Forces explicit evaluation of confidence (how sure are we this will work?) which reduces cognitive bias in decision-making.
**Structure:**
- Impact (40%): How much lift if correct
- Confidence (35%): How sure are we
- Ease (25%): How hard to implement
- Score > 6: do it. Score < 4: skip.

### Framework 12: CTA Hierarchy of Impact
**Source:** email-cta-conversion-design
**Psychology Basis:** Placement psychology — where a CTA appears determines 50% of conversion difference. Visual contrast, copy benefit, and surrounding context all influence click psychology.
**Hierarchy:**
1. Placement — Is it visible without scrolling? (Highest impact)
2. Copy — Does it communicate a benefit or just an action?
3. Visual contrast — Does it stand out from everything else?
4. Size/tap target — Is it easy to tap on mobile?
5. Surrounding context — Does the layout guide the eye toward it?
6. Color — Does it signal urgency or calm?

**Key Psychology Rules:**
- Social proof immediately BEFORE CTA increases conversion 15-30%
- CTA after content groups with content (not footer) — spacing rule
- First-person CTAs ("Claim My Discount") outperform second-person ("Claim Your Discount")
- Action Verb + Benefit + Urgency = strongest CTA formula

### Framework 13: Segment-Based Tone Adaptation
**Source:** copywriting-brand-voice-storytelling, brand-voice-definition
**Psychology Basis:** Different psychological states require different communication approaches. A VIP customer needs exclusivity; a lapsed customer needs empathy. Tone mismatch creates psychological rejection.
**Tone Spectrum:**
- Promotional → Flash sales, discounts, urgency
- Educational → How-to, product education
- Personal → Founder stories, relationship building
- Urgent → Abandoned cart, flash sale last call
- Celebratory → Milestones, loyalty, VIP
- Empathetic → Winback, service recovery

---

### Framework 14: 80/20 Value-Promotion Balance
**Source:** copywriting-brand-voice-storytelling
**Psychology Basis:** Reciprocity psychology — giving value before asking for a sale creates psychological debt (the desire to reciprocate). Brands that over-promote trigger reactance (the urge to resist persuasion).
**Rule:** For every 10 emails: 8 provide value (education, entertainment, inspiration, community), 2 directly promote.

---

### Framework 15: Zero-Party Data Psychology
**Source:** segmentation-rfm-predictive
**Psychology Basis:** When customers proactively share information, they experience the IKEA effect — they value the relationship more because they invested in it. Quiz results and preferences create commitment consistency.
**Application:** Quiz data, preference centers, survey responses — all create psychological investment in the brand relationship.

## NEUROMARKETING PRINCIPLES RELEVANT TO BRIGHTKIDCO

### Principle 1: Loss Aversion (from PAS, Objection Handling)
**Application:** Parents are more motivated by what they'll LOSE (Pre-K spot, childcare, dignity) than what they'll GAIN. Frame consequences, not just benefits.
**Caution:** For L3, loss aversion is cruel — they've already lost. Use gain framing instead.

### Principle 2: Social Proof Cascading (from CTA psychology, RFM)
**Application:** 16,511 reviews · 4.9★ creates authority. Peer testimonials create identification. "100,000+ parents" creates belonging. Layer social proof before CTA for 15-30% conversion lift.

### Principle 3: The IKEA Effect (from Zero-Party Data)
**Application:** When parents engage with quiz, preference center, or reply-to-email, they invest in the relationship. This investment increases perceived value and loyalty.

### Principle 4: Commitment Consistency (from Relationship Ladder)
**Application:** Small commitments (opening email, clicking link, reading content) lead to larger commitments (purchase, review, referral). The relationship ladder maps this progression.

### Principle 5: Cognitive Load Reduction (from "So What?" Test, Scannability)
**Application:** Parents in crisis have REDUCED cognitive capacity. Emails must be scannable (short paragraphs, bullets, bold reading path). One idea per paragraph. Mobile-first formatting. The "So What?" test eliminates cognitive waste.

### Principle 6: Reactance Prevention (from Permission Model, 80/20 Rule)
**Application:** Over-selling triggers psychological reactance — the urge to resist persuasion. The 80/20 rule and permission-not-to-buy approach prevent reactance. Especially critical for L2 (hyper-skeptical) and L3 (resigned).

### Principle 7: Anchoring Effect (from CTA, Pricing)
**Application:** The 3+3 FREE $79.99 bundle (BESTSELLER) anchors the value proposition. The 60-day guarantee anchors risk removal. The 49% SPARK stat anchors the problem's scale.
