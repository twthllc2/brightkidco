## 2. Top Topics & Themes Across Groups

### Tier 1: Highest Volume Topics (appear in virtually every group daily)

1. **Diagnosis Journey & Acceptance** — Parents processing new diagnosis, seeking validation ("Is this normal?"), asking about next steps, sharing grief and hope
2. **Therapy Debates & Recommendations** — ABA vs. OT vs. speech vs. floortime, "what worked for your child," waitlist frustration, insurance navigation
3. **School Advocacy & IEP/504** — Accommodation requests, teacher communication, inclusion vs. self-contained classroom, legal rights
4. **Meltdowns vs. Tantrums** — Understanding the difference, de-escalation strategies, sensory overload management, public meltdown handling
5. **Sleep Difficulties** — Melatonin discussions, bedtime routines, co-sleeping debates, night waking, sleep study recommendations

### Tier 2: High Volume Topics (weekly or multi-weekly)

6. **Potty Training / Toileting Challenges** — Regression, sensory barriers to toileting, interoception difficulties, age-appropriate expectations, nighttime wetting
7. **Food Aversions & Picky Eating** — GFCF diet, sensory-based food refusal, supplement recommendations, feeding therapy, nutritional concerns
8. **Sensory Processing** — Clothing sensitivities, noise sensitivity, sensory diet activities, weighted blankets, compression garments
9. **Medication Discussions** — ADHD medication with autism, anxiety medication, sleep medication, side effects, dosage experiences
10. **Elopement & Safety** — GPS trackers, door alarms, swimming lessons, ID bracelets, school safety plans

### Tier 3: Recurring Topics (monthly or as needed)

11. **Sibling Dynamics** — Explaining autism to neurotypical siblings, attention distribution, sibling resentment
12. **Parental Mental Health** — Burnout, depression, marriage strain, isolation, self-care strategies
13. **Transition to Adulthood** — Employment, independent living, guardianship, post-school services
14. **Product Recommendations** — Training pants, sensory tools, communication devices, weighted blankets, adaptive clothing
15. **Advocacy & Rights** — ADA compliance, IDEA rights, disability benefits (SSI, DLA), legal guardianship

## 4. Group Rules & Promotion Landscape

### Universal Rules Across Groups

| Rule | Prevalence | Notes |
|------|-----------|-------|
| No self-promotion | ~95% of groups | Absolute ban in most groups. Violators removed immediately. |
| No MLM/direct sales | ~90% of groups | Specific ban on multi-level marketing products |
| No "cure" claims | ~80% of groups | Groups are sensitive to anti-vax, bleach protocol, and "cure" language |
| No link dropping | ~85% of groups | Links to products/articles only allowed when answering a direct question |
| Be kind/supportive | 100% of groups | Judgment-free zone enforced. Victim-blaming removed. |
| No medical advice | ~70% of groups | "Talk to your doctor" disclaimer common. Peer experience sharing OK. |
| Admin approval for posts | ~60% of groups | Larger groups require admin review before posts go live |

### Promotion Opportunities (Within Rules)

1. **"What worked for you?" threads** — Parents asking for product recommendations. Organic mention of BKC training pants is appropriate here.
2. **Resource sharing** — Helpful blog posts, guides, and educational content can be shared when genuinely useful.
3. **Admin relationships** — Some groups allow sponsored posts or "brand ambassador" arrangements with admin permission.
4. **Weekly promo threads** — Some groups have designated "share your product" threads (less common in autism groups, more common in general parenting groups).
5. **Expert status** — Parents who consistently provide value can build trust and mention products organically over time.

## 6. Facebook Group Landscape Summary

### Key Findings

1. **Facebook is the #1 platform for autism parent communities** — larger than Reddit (r/Autism_Parenting has ~65K; top FB groups have 100K–200K+), more private, more emotionally intimate.

2. **Private groups dominate** — Nearly all meaningful autism parenting groups are private (require approval to join). This creates trust but limits reach for brands.

3. **Self-promotion is universally banned** — Unlike Reddit where product mentions are sometimes tolerated, Facebook autism groups have strict no-promotion rules. The only viable approach is organic, value-first participation.

4. **Potty training is a TOP 5 pain point** — Alongside diagnosis, therapies, school advocacy, and sleep, potty training threads appear consistently across all major groups. Parents are actively seeking product solutions.

5. **The "what worked for you?" moment is the golden opportunity** — When parents ask for product recommendations, genuine suggestions from other parents carry enormous weight. This is where BKC can be mentioned organically.

6. **Interoception awareness is growing** — Parents are increasingly using the term "interoception" when discussing potty training challenges. BKC's positioning around body-signal awareness aligns with this trend.

7. **Regression is the emotional trigger** — Posts about potty training regression generate the highest emotional engagement. Parents are desperate, vulnerable, and open to solutions.

8. **Facebook groups are harder to scale than Reddit** — Reddit allows more organic product discussion. Facebook requires building genuine relationships over time. The payoff is deeper trust and higher conversion.

## 7. Recommended Facebook Group Entry Strategy for BKC

### Phase 1: Join & Listen (Weeks 1–2)
- Join 5–8 most relevant groups
- Observe posting patterns, tone, rules enforcement
- Identify active members and admins
- Note recurring potty training questions

### Phase 2: Engage & Help (Weeks 3–6)
- Answer potty training questions with genuine advice
- Share personal experience (if applicable) or researched knowledge
- Never mention BKC directly
- Build reputation as a helpful, knowledgeable parent

### Phase 3: Organic Mention (Weeks 7+)
- When someone asks "what training pants work?" — mention BKC naturally
- Frame as "we used [product] and it helped because [mechanism]"
- Never post links unprompted
- Let other parents ask follow-up questions

### Phase 4: Scale (Ongoing)
- Identify 2–3 group admins open to brand partnerships
- Offer product samples for review
- Create Facebook-specific content (short videos, infographics)
- Consider Facebook Group advertising targeting autism parent interests
