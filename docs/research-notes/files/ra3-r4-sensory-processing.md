## 1. SENSORY PROCESSING SUMMARY (Accessible Explanation)

### What Is Sensory Processing?

Sensory processing is how the brain receives, organizes, and responds to information from the senses — touch, sound, sight, taste, smell, movement, and body position. For most people, the brain automatically filters out irrelevant sensory input (like the feeling of clothing against skin) within minutes of putting clothes on. This is called **sensory adaptation** — the brain learns the stimulus is non-threatening and stops flagging it.

### How It Differs in Autism

In autistic individuals, sensory processing works differently at the neurological level. Research using fMRI brain imaging has confirmed these are real, measurable differences — not preferences or behavioral issues (Green et al., 2015).

**Key difference:** Many autistic individuals do not habituate to ongoing tactile input the way neurotypical people do. A scratchy seam that a neurotypical child forgets about in 90 seconds may register as **unrelenting discomfort for an entire day** for an autistic child. The sensory signal doesn't fade — it stays active, constant, and distressing.

**Three types of sensory differences:**
- **Hypersensitivity (over-responsive):** The brain amplifies sensory input. A tag feels like a razor blade. A waistband feels like a vice. These children may scream, cry, or refuse to get dressed.
- **Hyposensitivity (under-responsive):** The brain doesn't register enough sensory input. These children may not notice wetness, cold, or discomfort — which directly affects toilet training awareness.
- **Sensory seeking:** Actively craves intense sensory input. May fidget with fabrics, crash into furniture, or seek deep pressure.

**Important:** A single child can be hypersensitive in one area and hyposensitive in another. For example, a child might be hypersensitive to touch (can't tolerate tags) but hyposensitive to interoception (doesn't feel the urge to use the bathroom).

### Why This Matters for Clothing

Over 90% of autistic children experience some form of atypical sensory processing (Leekam et al., 2007; Ben-Sasson et al., 2009). Tactile (touch) sensitivity is one of the most commonly reported and most impactful on daily life. Clothing is worn against the skin for 12-16 hours per day — making fabric choice one of the most consequential sensory decisions a parent makes.

### Why This Matters for Toilet Training

Toilet training requires **interoception** — the ability to notice internal body signals like a full bladder or bowel. Many autistic children have interoceptive differences, meaning they literally cannot feel when they need to go. Pull-ups compound this problem by eliminating the external wetness feedback that could help the brain learn to connect internal signals with the action of using the toilet.

### Finding 2: Tactile Sensitivity Is the Least Understood but Most Impactful Sensory Modality in Autism

**Finding:** Autistic individuals experience touch dysfunction more often compared to other sensory modalities (auditory, visual, etc.), yet tactile defensiveness remains the least investigated sensory area in autism research. Fabrics directly impact wellbeing — cotton was consistently preferred over synthetic materials.

**Source:**
- Kyriacou, C., Forrester-Jones, R., & Triantafyllopoulou, P. (2023). Clothes, sensory experiences and autism: Is wearing the right fabric important? *Journal of Autism and Developmental Disorders*, 53, 1495-1508. doi:10.1007/s10803-021-05140-3

**Key data points:**
- 10 autistic adults participated in fabric evaluation study
- Participants described certain fabrics as causing distress that impacted their wellbeing
- Cotton and soft, natural fabrics were consistently preferred
- Tags, seams, and synthetic textures were most commonly reported as distressing
- Coping strategies included wearing only specific fabrics, cutting out tags, wearing clothes inside-out

**Email relevance:** HIGH — scientific backing for "fabric choice matters" and "cotton is preferred."

### Finding 3: Autistic People Do Not Habituate to Clothing Sensations — Distress Persists All Day

**Finding:** Neurotypical brains adapt to clothing within minutes (sensory habituation). Autistic brains often do not. A scratchy seam or tight waistband that a neurotypical child forgets about in 90 seconds remains actively distressing for an autistic child throughout the entire day. This is not "oversensitivity" — it's a fundamental difference in how the nervous system filters ongoing tactile input.

**Sources:**
- Green, S.A., Hernandez, L., Tottenham, N., Krasileva, K., Bookheimer, S.Y., & Dapretto, M. (2015). Neurobiology of sensory overresponsivity in youth with autism spectrum disorders. *JAMA Psychiatry*, 72(8), 778-786.
- Neurolaunch.com synthesis of multiple studies on sensory habituation differences in autism (2025).

**Email relevance:** HIGH — powerful explanation for parents. "Your child isn't being difficult. Their brain processes fabric differently — and the right fabric genuinely reduces their distress."

### Finding 4: Tactile Hyper-Sensitivity Impacts Self-Esteem, Self-Expression, and Social Participation

**Finding:** A 2025 study of 86 autistic adults found that clothing-related tactile hypersensitivity significantly impacts appearance dissatisfaction, self-esteem, and self-expression. Participants described feeling "trapped in safe clothes" — limited to a small rotation of tolerable garments, unable to dress for social occasions or express personal style.

**Source:**
- Ferrer Knight, A. & Birtles, D. (2025). 'I feel trapped in my safe clothes': The impact of tactile hyper-sensitivity on autistic adults. *Autism*, 29(11), 2727-2740. doi:10.1177/13623613251366882

**Key data points:**
- 86 autistic adults surveyed in the United Kingdom
- Clothing-related tactile hypersensitivity linked to appearance dissatisfaction
- Participants reported limited wardrobe rotation (only a few "safe" items)
- Impact on social participation and self-expression
- "I feel trapped in my safe clothes" — direct participant quote

### Finding 5: Interoception Is the Bridge Between Sensory Processing and Toilet Training

**Finding:** Interoception — the ability to perceive internal body signals — is critical for toilet training. Children who under-register proprioceptive and sensory input tend to have difficulty with interoception as well. Many autistic children literally cannot feel when they need to use the bathroom until it's too late.

**Sources:**
- University of Utah Health Sciences. Sensory Strategies for Potty Training. [PDF resource for parents and clinicians.]
- ERIC UK (Education and Resources for Improving Childhood Continence). Interoception and Toileting. eric.org.uk.
- Kelly Mahler, OTD, OTR/L. Interoception, Toileting, and Potty Training: Understanding Inner Body Signals. kelly-mahler.com.
- Mahler, K. & Hample, K. (2022). *The Interoception Curriculum*.

**Key data points from University of Utah:**
- "The ability for the body to make the connection with the brain that it is time to go to the bathroom requires a properly working connection between the interoceptors in the bladder and bowels and the brain."
- "Interoceptors are similar to proprioceptors and children who under-register proprioception and sensory input overall, tend to have difficulty with interoception as well."
- "Working on interoception is really not possible since these are the sensory receptors within the gut and organs...but what you can do is work on the sensory foundation."
- Recommended strategies include proprioceptive input, loose clothing to help recognize urges, and patience.

**Key data from ERIC UK:**
- "There is evidence that some children learn to recognise their body signals of a full bladder and bowel after they learn to use the potty and toilet and have developed a toileting routine."
- Interoception can develop through practice and external feedback — the body can learn to connect sensations with meaning.

**Email relevance:** CRITICAL — this is the core scientific mechanism for BrightKidCo. "Your child's brain may not be receiving the 'I need to go' signal. BrightKidCo's gentle wetness feedback helps build that connection."

### Finding 6: Sensory Processing Differences Directly Cause Toilet Training Delays in Autism

**Finding:** Sensory processing challenges — including difficulty with interoception, proprioception, and tactile processing — are a primary reason why toilet training is delayed in autistic children. The sensory environment of the bathroom itself can also be a barrier (flushing sounds, cold seats, echo).

**Sources:**
- Wiggins, L.D., et al. (2022). DSM-5 criteria for ASD and toileting delays. *Journal of Developmental and Behavioral Pediatrics*. (CDC SEED-2 study, N=2,202)
- Miss Jenny OT. Sensory Processing and Toileting Issues. missjennyot.com.
- Down Syndrome UK / June Rogers MBE. Interoception, Sensory Issues and Toilet Training.

**Key data points:**
- 49% of autistic 4-5 year olds are not toilet trained (vs. 8% of typically developing peers) — Wiggins et al. 2022
- Children may under-register the sensation of a full bladder until the accident has already happened
- Bathroom sensory triggers: loud flushing, cold toilet seat, strong smells, echo
- "Do not scold the child as if they have done something wrong when they have an accident...it is very possible that the child is truly not able to recognize and identify when they need to go" — University of Utah

**Email relevance:** HIGH — validates parent frustration and positions the product as a solution. "It's not defiance. Their brain processes body signals differently."

### Finding 8: Sensory Overload from Clothing Creates an Anxiety Feedback Loop

**Finding:** Tactile discomfort and anxiety are not just co-occurring problems — they feed each other. Children with autism who show sensory over-responsivity are significantly more likely to also meet criteria for anxiety disorders. When the nervous system is already taxed by uncomfortable clothing, the threshold for stress responses drops. Everything becomes harder: social interaction, concentration, emotional regulation, and toileting.

**Source:**
- Neurolaunch.com synthesis of Green et al. (2015), Lane et al. (2010, 2011), and related research.

**Key mechanism:**
1. Uncomfortable clothing → sensory distress
2. Sensory distress → elevated cortisol / anxiety
3. Elevated anxiety → lower threshold for further sensory overload
4. Lower threshold → more meltdowns, more avoidance, more rigidity
5. Cycle compounds throughout the day

**Email relevance:** HIGH — explains the "why" behind meltdowns. "One scratchy seam can start a cycle that ruins the entire day."

### Finding 9: Proprioception and Body Awareness Connect to Toileting Success

**Finding:** Proprioception — the sense of body position and movement — is foundational to toileting readiness. Children who under-register proprioceptive input often struggle with interoception (internal body signals) as well. Working on proprioceptive "power sensations" helps the sensory systems overall and helps the body and brain make the connection needed for toileting.

**Source:**
- University of Utah Health Sciences. Sensory Strategies for Potty Training.
- The OT Toolbox. Weighted Vests and Compression Garments. theottoolbox.com.

**Key data points:**
- "Children who under-register proprioception and sensory input overall, tend to have difficulty with interoception as well."
- "Daily frequent doses of proprioceptive input will help promote overall body awareness."
- "Self-regulation is the key to this overall. A lot of calming and regulating proprioception on a daily basis is very important."
- Easy-to-remove clothing supports independence and reduces anxiety during toileting

**Email relevance:** MEDIUM — supports the "body awareness" messaging. "Potty training isn't just about the toilet — it's about helping your child's brain connect to their body."
