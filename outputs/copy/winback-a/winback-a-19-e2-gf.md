#### HOOK — Universal Recognition (R1, R3, R4)

**Whether** you bought the 3+3 bundle three months ago or one pair to try, **whether** your child is 3 or 10, verbal or non-verbal, still in pull-ups or somewhere in between, **if** you've been wondering whether Body-Signal learning is actually working, you're not the only one.

Many parents pause after the first month. Some because nothing seemed to change. Some because something *did* change but they weren't sure what to make of it. Some because life got loud and the underwear ended up in a drawer.

All of those are normal.

---

#### VALIDATION — Permission to Pause (R5)

If you stopped using them, that's not failure. If you're not sure they're working, that's not ungratefulness. If your child seemed to regress after an initial sign, that's not starting over.

The interoception pathway doesn't build in a straight line. Some kids show awareness in 2 weeks. Others take 8. Both are normal. Some need a break and come back to find the signal clicks the second time.

The 60-day guarantee you bought with, it still stands. Whenever you're ready.

---

#### RECOGNITION ANCHORS — Do Any of These Sound Familiar? (R2, R4, R6)

Maybe you've noticed one of these since the underwear arrived, or maybe you're still waiting for one. Either way, these are the mini-wins other parents describe:

, A pause before the accident. Just one. But it happened.
, A look down at their pants after, like they *noticed* for a split second.
, Pulling at their underwear, or showing you with their body.
, Staying dry 2 hours instead of 30 minutes.
, Walking toward the bathroom, even after.
, Nothing yet. And you're still watching, still hoping.

Any one of these is real. None of them mean "trained." But they mean *something is connecting.*

#### THREE-FAMILY TESTIMONIAL PATTERN (GF Inclusion — R2, R4, R6)

Three different kids. Three different timelines. One common thread, they all came back to the same question. Here's what happened next.

> *"We bought the 3+3 in January. He wore them, nothing happened, we put them away. Then in March he started pulling at his pants, for no reason I could see. I pulled the underwear out again. Two weeks later, he paused before an accident for the first time. Ever."*
>, **Sarah**, mom of a verbal 4-year-old

> *"She wore them for two weeks. No change. We stopped. Then at a BCBA session, her therapist asked: 'Has she ever paused when wet?' I realized she had, once, and I didn't count it. We started again. Week 3, she paused and looked down. Week 6, she initiated. Just once. But she initiated."*
>, **Lisa**, mom of a sensory-sensitive 6-year-old

> *"He's 9. Non-verbal. I bought one pair thinking 'what do I have to lose.' Nothing happened for a month. I almost threw them away. Then one morning he walked to the bathroom door after breakfast. Not because he needed to go, but he walked there. That never happened before. He's not trained. But he's feeling *something* for the first time in 9 years. That's enough for now."*
>, **Maria**, mom of a non-verbal 9-year-old

If any of these sound like your child, your timeline, your doubt, your hope, you're in good company.

---

#### MECHANISM REMINDER — Why Body-Signal Learning Works Differently (R1)

None of these kids used a different method. They all used the same underwear, the Body-Signal Learning Layer, that creates a gentle, sustained "uh-oh" sensation after every accident. Not to punish. To inform.

The brain gets 30-60 seconds of sensory feedback it's been missing. Over days and weeks, it starts to recognize the pattern: *"That feeling → that's what wet means."*

Pull-ups wick that signal away. Sticker charts don't create it. Timed sits can't force it. The body has to learn to feel, and that starts with the right input.
