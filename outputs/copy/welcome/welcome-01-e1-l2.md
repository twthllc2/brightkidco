#### Mechanism

The feeling of "I need to go to the bathroom" is not a thought. It's a **body signal** — a neurological message that travels from your bladder to your brain. Your brain receives it, interprets it, and says: *time to find a toilet.*

For many autistic children, that message doesn't arrive in time. Or at all.

The science is clear on this. Nicholson et al. (2019) published in the Journal of Autism and Developmental Disorders — peer-reviewed, replicated, settled science — showing that autistic children have significantly diminished **interoceptive accuracy**: the ability to sense internal body states like bladder fullness, hunger, thirst, and temperature.

It's not training. It's **neurology**.

A landmark systematic review by Barmpagiannis & Baldimtsi (2025) examined 49 studies on interoception in autism. The conclusion: interoceptive differences are a well-established feature of autism. This is not one study. This is the weight of the evidence.

Here's what this means for you and your child:

Your child's brain and bladder are sending messages on different frequencies. The signal is either delayed, distorted, or absent. Every method you tried — scheduled sits, prompted toilet trips, sticker charts, social stories — assumed the signal was there. It wasn't.

You weren't failing. **You were working with the wrong map.**

(Source: S2 §1.1 L2 Copy Strategy — Mechanism; MASTER-RESEARCH-COMPENDIUM.md §2.1 — Nicholson et al. 2019, Barmpagiannis & Baldimtsi 2025; 04-mechanism-positioning.md §2.2; SCIENCE-PROOF-POINTS.md PP-A01, PP-A06)

## Part 2 — Creative Strategy — Visual Direction

### The Big Idea

**Visual Metaphor:** *Two radios tuned to different frequencies, with a gentle bridge signal connecting them.*

The core visual concept is "signal translation" — the idea that the child's body is broadcasting a signal the brain isn't receiving. The email's visual language centers on waves, frequencies, and gentle connection points. Not a "fix" narrative (broken → repaired), but a "translation" narrative (broadcasting → receiving). This mirrors the mechanism positioning exactly: the problem isn't behavior, it's signal-timing.

### Typography as Design

**Primary Stack (Body):** `Georgia, 'Times New Roman', Times, serif`

Rationale: Serif body text signals authority, reading comfort, and trust — not "sales email." Serifs slow the eye fractionally, which is appropriate for L2's deep-reading behavior. Lisa reads every word; serif typography honors that.

**Display Stack (Headlines):** `'Inter', -apple-system, BlinkMacSystemFont, 'Segoe UI', Roboto, sans-serif`

### Hidden Detail / Easter Egg (Level-Calibrated)

**The "Mismatched Frequency" Detail**

At the very bottom of the email — just above the Lena sign-off but visually subtle — a small inline element appears:

> `≈/≈` *Signal clarity: building...*

This is rendered in `#6B6B7B` at 12px italic Georgia, right-aligned, with 8px top spacing from the preceding content.

**Why this works for L2:**
1. Lisa reads every word. A small detail like this rewards the close reader — the parent who actually absorbs the content rather than skimming.
2. It reinforces the signal metaphor in a non-preachy way. "Signal clarity: building..." mirrors her real experience — it's not fixed overnight, it's building.
3. It's not a gimmick — no animation, no hidden message to decode. Just a quiet acknowledgment that resonates with someone who has been paying close attention.
4. Level calibration: L1 would find this confusing ("what signal?"). L3 would find it too hopeful. L2 — with her 40/60 hope-to-realism ratio — can hold both "signal clarity" (optimistic) and "building..." (honest, not there yet).

**Implementation:** Inline text with `font-size: 12px; color: #6B6B7B; font-style: italic; text-align: right;`

### Motion / Animation (Client-Constraint Aware)

**Extremely limited motion — static-first design.**

BrightKidCo operates within standard email client constraints. No AMP, no JavaScript, no auto-play video. Motion must be CSS-animated inline SVG only.

**Permitted animations:**
1. **Hero Signal SVG dash-offset:** The connecting line in the signal-path illustration uses `stroke-dasharray` and a gentle 3s CSS `stroke-dashoffset` keyframe animation (infinite alternate, ease-in-out). This creates a "signal traveling" effect — subtle enough to not trigger sensory sensitivity concerns, visible enough to reward attention on desktop.
2. **CTA button hover state** (non-email, for landing page): Not applicable to email. Email CTA is a static table-based button.

**Animation Restrictions (L2-Specific):**
- No pulsing CTA buttons (triggers skepticism — "too salesy")
- No rotating/ spinning elements (distracting for sensory-sensitive household)
- No color-shifting backgrounds (unprofessional, undermines scientific credibility)
- No animated countdown timers (L2 has zero urgency — timers create distrust)
- All animations degrade gracefully — email works 100% without any motion

**Fallback:** If the SVG animation doesn't render (Gmail, Outlook desktop), the illustration remains static and communicates the same information. The `alt` text carries the meaning.
