### DEEP MECHANISM

**Why the last 10% won't close with more practice or more prompts.**

The interoception signal for bladder fullness is subtle. For most people — neurotypical adults included — the first "I need to go" feeling arrives with enough lead time to act. But for many autistic children, that signal is compressed. The brain registers it — just milliseconds too late.

Nicholson et al. (2019) demonstrated that autistic children show significantly diminished interoceptive accuracy. The signal exists. It arrives. But by the time the brain has processed "I need to go," the event is already starting.

This is why timed methods, sticker charts, and scheduled sits hit a wall at that last 10%. They all assume the "I need to go" feeling arrives early enough to act on. When the signal arrives too late, no amount of practice closes that gap.

The **Body-Signal Learning Layer (BSL™)** is designed for this specific problem. It provides sustained, localized sensory feedback when an accident happens — a gentle "uh-oh" sensation that lasts 30-60 seconds. That's long enough for even a hypo-responsive nervous system to register: *something happened. That feeling means I peed.*

Over time, the brain learns to associate the pre-event sensation (bladder fullness building) with the post-event signal (the BSL™ feedback). The neurological loop closes. The "almost trained" gap narrows.

(Source: 2-full-email-copy-strategy.md §E7 L1 Mechanism; MASTER-RESEARCH-COMPENDIUM.md §2.1; Nicholson et al. 2019; 03-science-library.md PP-A01; 04-mechanism-positioning.md §5)

## LEVEL CALIBRATION — VISUAL (L1 SARAH)

| Calibration Dimension | L1 (Sarah) Choice | Why |

| **Visual complexity** | Clean and warm, not minimalist-cold or detailed-clinical | L1 parent needs to feel understood, not overwhelmed. She's analytical, not in crisis |
