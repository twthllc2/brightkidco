### Mechanism

Here's what the research actually says about this gap.

Interoception researcher Kelly Mahler — she calls it the "eighth sensory system" — the sense that tells us when we're hungry, thirsty, or need the bathroom. For autistic children, this sense works differently. Nicholson et al. (2019) proved what many L2 parents already sense: autistic children show significantly diminished interoceptive accuracy — the ability to sense internal body states like bladder fullness. It's not a training problem. It's a neurology problem.

The data backs this up in ways that matter for your child:
- Only **19%** of autistic children can recognize when they're thirsty (Iqbal 2022). If thirst — one of the strongest body signals — doesn't register consistently, how can we expect bladder fullness to?
- **59%** of autistic children can't reliably signal urination needs at all (Iqbal 2022).
- Critically: Nicholson et al. (2019) found this is a **childhood phenomenon** — adults with ASD show compensation. This means early body-signal intervention during these years creates neural pathways that last.

Hample, Mahler & Amspacher (2020) showed that a structured 8-week interoception intervention produces measurable improvement in body awareness. The body-signal learning approach creates the conditions for that training to happen naturally — through repeated, gentle feedback after every event, the brain builds the neural connection between the sensation and the meaning.

(Source: MASTER-RESEARCH-COMPENDIUM.md §2.1, §2.2; S2 Full Email Copy Strategy §Welcome E3 L2)

### The Big Idea

**"The signal was there all along — your child's nervous system just couldn't receive it."** A visual metaphor of a quiet radio receiver slowly tuning into a frequency. Static turns to signal. The image shows a radio antenna (representing the Body-Signal Learning Layer) picking up a transmission that was always broadcasting but never reached the listener. The visual arc moves from static/fuzz (confusion, regression cycles) to a clear signal (awareness, the first pause).

**Design Rationale:** L2 parents are the most skeptical segment — over-researched, over-promised, and burned by 40+ methods. The creative must feel clinical and honest, not promotional or emotional-pornographic. Science-visual elements dominate: diagrams, signal charts, anatomical-adjacent graphics that feel like they belong in an OT clinic or BCBA resource binder, not a marketing email.

**L2-specific:** Slightly tighter tracking on evidence sections (+0.2px) to visually differentiate "proof copy" from "conversation copy" — a subtle cue that says "this section is data, read carefully."
