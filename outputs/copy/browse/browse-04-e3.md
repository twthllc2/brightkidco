### Section 3: Mechanism Recap

Here's what we know now that we didn't know five years ago: many autistic children can't feel the internal signal that says "I need to go." It's called interoception — the sense that tells your body when you're hungry, thirsty, or need the bathroom — and research shows it works differently in autistic kids (Nicholson et al. 2019; Barmpagiannis & Baldimtsi 2025, 49-study review). Kelly Mahler, the leading researcher in this space, calls interoception the "eighth sensory system." If that signal doesn't arrive, no method that depends on it — sticker charts, timed sits, social stories, Oh Crap — can work for that child. Not because you didn't try hard enough. Because the method was asking for a signal the nervous system couldn't deliver.

The Body-Signal Learning Layer doesn't train your child. It doesn't prompt, bribe, or time them. It creates a gentle, sustained "uh-oh" sensation — 30 to 60 seconds of feedback — that gives the brain the information it's been missing. Some kids start to register that sensation within two weeks. Some take eight weeks. Both are normal.

### Section 4: Social Proof — Mini-Wins (R6)

The parents who've tried it describe success in small, real moments:

Three different kids, three different starting points, three different timelines. The common thread: the body-signal layer started giving the brain information it had been missing.

For most families, the 3+3 Bundle (6 pairs, $79.99, free shipping) gives enough rotation for consistent daily wearing. If you want to start lighter, 1 Pair ($34.99) lets you test the approach alongside your current routine.

## PART 2 — CREATIVE STRATEGY

### The Big Idea

The email is built around the metaphor of a held-open door — a gentle, unobstructed passageway with no lock, no timer, no expiration. The guarantee is the door itself; the product sits on the other side, visible but never pushed. A single warm-toned illustration of an open doorway (framed in emerald light, threshold dissolving into soft cream) runs vertically through the left margin, growing wider as the reader scrolls — the closer they get to the CTA, the more the door opens. For the cross-level calibration, the doorway contains no furniture, no figure, no age-specific detail — it's an empty threshold that each mom can imagine walking through at her own pace.

### Layout Architecture

**Controlled Cascade** — a single-column structure with deliberate breathing room. The layout follows a Z-axis depth gradient:

- **Above the fold:** Subject line → emerald hairline divider → Hook paragraph (full-width, left-aligned, max 48em measure). The door illustration sits as a left-side vertical accent — a thin emerald line that thickens into an archway shape around the guarantee section.
- **Mid-scroll (Recognition Anchors):** A bullet list with custom signal-dot bullets (see Decorative Furniture). Each anchor occupies a full-width card with generous top/bottom padding (48px). Cards are separated by 2px light emerald dividers that taper from full-width to half-width (emphasizing narrowing uncertainty).
- **Mechanism Recap:** A single inset block — 28px left indent, 2px emerald left border. The door illustration is at its widest here, framing the mechanism paragraph.
- **Social Proof Cards:** Three testimonial cards stacked vertically — each with a left-emerald quotation mark and 20px indent. No headshots (avoids age/level cues for cross-level). Each card separated by a hand-drawn squiggle divider.
- **Product + Guarantee section:** Full-width emerald-tinted block (background: #F4F7F4). The CTA button floats centered below — a solid emerald rectangle with white text and no icon. Below the CTA, the door illustration completes its arc: the archway is now fully open, threshold glowing.
- **Mobile collapse:** All cards go full-width, padding reduces to 24px, door illustration becomes a 4px emerald vertical line only (space constraint). Testimonial cards stack without dividers. CTA button expands to full-width.

### Custom Illustration / Contraption

**The Doorway Contraption** — a custom SVG illustration of an arched threshold built from three visual states across the email:

1. **Above fold (hook):** A thin emerald hairline at the left edge — the barest suggestion of a doorframe. One line, no arch, no threshold. Represents the earliest stage of consideration.
2. **Mid-scroll (anchors + mechanism):** The line fattens to 6px and curves into a partial arch that frames the mechanism block. A faint emerald glow (OKLCH 60% lightness, 10% opacity) emanates from the archway. The threshold is hinted by a 45-degree angled shadow at the base.
3. **Below CTA:** The archway is fully formed — 12px emerald border, warm cream (#FAF9F7) fill inside the door, threshold defined. A tiny warm-gold highlight dot sits at the apex of the arch — visible only to readers who scroll past the CTA.

The archway illustration is rendered entirely in CSS border-radius and box-shadow on a single `<div>` element for email compatibility (no SVG images). Fallback: a simple emerald bracket like `[ ]` for Outlook.

**Why this works across levels:**
- L1 reads it as: "The door to Pre-K readiness is still open."
- L2 reads it as: "The door to a different approach exists."
- L3 reads it as: "There's a door, but no one is asking me to walk through it right now."
- GF reads it as: "This is for anyone. Step through when you're ready."

This mirrors the reader's progression from skepticism → consideration → trust. The gold apex dot is the only non-green accent — a tiny "this is for you" moment that appears only at the very end.

**Cross-level calibration:** No pink (too gendered), no blue (too clinical), no neutral-gray (too corporate). Emerald sits in the high-trust, growth-oriented quadrant of color psychology without triggering gender or age assumptions.

### Decorative Furniture

- **Signal-dot bullets:** Each recognition anchor (—) is preceded by a 6px emerald circle — not a standard disc, not a dash. On hover (Apple Mail), the dot pulses with a 20% opacity glow.
- **Dividers:** Hand-drawn SVG squiggle lines (single stroke, 2px emerald, slight sinusoidal wave) between testimonial cards. Code: `<svg width="40" height="12" viewBox="0 0 40 12"><path d="M0,6 Q10,0 20,6 T40,6" stroke="#039902" stroke-width="1.5" fill="none"/></svg>` — replicating a pencil-sketched underline.
- **Quote marks:** Left-aligned prefixed quotation marks in Warm Gold (48px, Questrial, 0.3 opacity) before each testimonial. Creates a visual line that guides the eye downward.
- **Hairline borders:** A 1px emerald hairline at 30% opacity separates each section. On the product + guarantee card, the hairline thickens to a 2px solid top border — a visual punctuation.
- **Lena signature:** The "Lena" sign-off uses a handwritten-style font (Cookie or similar) only in Apple Mail compatible clients. Fallback for Gmail/Outlook: Questrial small-caps.
- **Background texture:** 2% micro-noise overlay on the cream background (data-URI inline CSS) for a subtle paper-grain feel. Applies to the entire email body.

### Hidden Detail / Easter Egg

**Cross-Level Universal Easter Egg: The Threshold Glow**

At the very bottom of the email, just above the footer, a tiny 4px warm-gold circle sits at the apex of the emerald door archway. To the casual reader, it's a decorative dot. But to the attentive mom, it represents something her level interprets:

- **L1:** The first sign — like a potty-training sticker chart marker
- **L2:** The BCBA's gold star — a clinical achievement badge
- **L3:** A gentle warmth — like feeling something for the first time
- **GF:** A guiding light — neutral, welcoming, undemanding

There is no text, no tooltip, no aria-label. It is purely visual. It only becomes visible to readers who scroll past the CTA and into the footer — rewarding those who read the entire email instead of clicking immediately. This reinforces the "no urgency" energy: the treasure is at the end of the path, not behind the button.

### Motion / Animation (GIF)

The only animated element is the **Signal-Dot Pulse** — a CSS keyframe animation applied to the emerald signal-dot bullets in the recognition anchor list:

```css
@keyframes signalPulse {
  0% { box-shadow: 0 0 0 0 rgba(3, 153, 2, 0.3); }
  50% { box-shadow: 0 0 0 6px rgba(3, 153, 2, 0); }
  100% { box-shadow: 0 0 0 0 rgba(3, 153, 2, 0); }
}
```

Applied to each bullet with staggered delays (0ms, 200ms, 400ms...) so the dots pulse down the list like a signal wave.

**Fallback:** Static emerald dots for Outlook/Gmail. No critical content is in the animation.

**Motion Principle (from design reference):** Only opacity and box-shadow animate — no layout-triggering transforms. Duration 2s with ease-in-out. Respects `prefers-reduced-motion: reduce`.

### Level Calibration (Visual) — Cross-Level

This is a cross-level email. One visual direction calibrated via R1-R6:

- **No level-specific imagery:** No photos of children (avoids age/diagnosis assumptions). No icons that suggest a specific communication mode (no speech bubbles, no AAC devices). No level-specific time references (no clocks for L1, no therapy-room for L2, no maturity markers for L3).
- **R1 — Symptom over label:** The door metaphor describes an experience, not a diagnosis. The only human element is the testimonial text — which uses symptom language ("almost trained," "sensory issues," "non-verbal") rather than level labels.
- **R2 — 3+ anchors:** The recognition anchor list provides 5 specific situations spanning all levels, ensuring every mom finds herself.
- **R3 — Age range:** No age-specific imagery. The door has no height marker, no child silhouette, no furniture that suggests a specific age.
- **R4 — Verbal/non-verbal:** Testimonials include both a verbal child ("talks about the potty") and a non-verbal child ("high support needs"). The hooks explicitly say "verbal or non-verbal."
- **R5 — Timeline flexibility:** The testimonial timelines span 2 weeks, 4 weeks, and 7 years. No singular timeline is visually reinforced.
- **R6 — Low-bar success:** The "pause," "look," and "tolerate" mini-wins are the only success metrics shown. No "fully trained" language appears anywhere in the copy or visuals.

**CTA button sizing:** Medium (not large — avoids urgency signaling for L3; not small — avoids appearing hidden for L1). 240px wide, 48px tall, centered. White text on emerald background. No arrow icon (too aggressive). Just a clean, rectangular doorplate.
