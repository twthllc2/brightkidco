### MECHANISM, Why 90 Days Is a Milestone (Mechanism 50%)

Ninety days of Body-Signal Learning Layer exposure means the nervous system that picks up signals slowly has had sustained, gentle feedback, 30 to 60 seconds of sensation per wear, to start building a connection that never existed before.

The science is clear: body signal differences in autism are real and measurable (Barmpagiannis & Baldimtsi 2025, 49-study systematic review). The internal "I need to go" signal doesn't arrive the way traditional methods assume. Pull-ups wick away the sensation entirely, the brain gets nothing to work with. The Body-Signal Learning Layer provides the feedback that pull-ups remove.

Some brains need 2 weeks to register the signal. Others need 12 weeks or more. Both are within the normal range for autistic children. *(R5, Timeline Flexibility)*

**"Progress Marker, Where Are You?"** A simple 3-option click bar embedded above the CTA. Not a quiz, a low-friction progress signal:

> **Your child at 90 days:**
> ○ I've seen new signals (pausing, looking, noticing)
> ○ No obvious changes, still wearing consistently
> ○ We paused, thinking about trying again

Each option links to a different landing page path:
- "New signals" → Size-up guide + re-order offer
- "No changes" → Troubleshooting tips + 60-day guarantee reassurance
- "Paused" → Re-start guide + "it's never too late" framing

This also doubles as a migration data-collection point, click patterns correlate with level indicators.

## Easter Egg

**Hidden in the P.S. reply prompt.** The line "I read every response" is the standard Lena P.S., but the Easter egg is that replies that mention a specific progress signal (e.g., "he looked down for the first time" or "she paused while playing") auto-trigger a follow-up sequence in Klaviyo with level-specific troubleshooting tips, rather than going to an unmonitored inbox.

Mechanism: A hidden Klaviyo trigger set up on keywords from the reply-to address, "looked down," "paused," "first time," "pulled at pants," "no change," "nothing", each routes to a tailored autoresponse that feels like Lena wrote it personally. The parent never knows it's automated; they just know "Lena read my note and sent me exactly what I needed."

This Easter egg serves double duty: it deepens trust (Lena really listens) and collects migration-level data from the reply content itself.
