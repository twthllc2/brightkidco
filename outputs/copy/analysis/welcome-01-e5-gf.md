Strong preview text. It completes the subject line's promise — "here's the difference" becomes "your methods were aimed at the wrong target." The preview text earns the click by reframing before they even open.

**Assessment:** The strategic execution is excellent. E5 does exactly what it's supposed to do at exactly the right point in the flow. The cross-level calibration is the most sophisticated element — it makes the email work for everyone in GF without any sub-group feeling targeted or excluded.

## 8. ONE FIX

**Tighten the mechanism section for mobile readability.**

The mechanism explanation (lines 94-110) is the most important paragraph in the email — it's where the reader decides if the product actually works. But it's also the densest block of text. On mobile (60%+ of GF readers), the numbered sequence + three-layer description + research citation + pull-up comparison all run together.

**Specific fix:** Split the mechanism into two visual blocks:

**Block 1: The Product** (what it is)
- Three-layer description: cotton inner, smart absorption, leak-resistant outer
- The sensation it creates: "a gentle, sustained 'uh-oh', 30 to 60 seconds of feedback"
- Keep the numbered sequence (1-2-3-4) but make it a visual element with the green numbered step badges already specified in the design

**Block 2: The Science** (why it works)
- The research citation (Hample/Mahler 2020)
- The comparison to pull-ups (zero feedback)
- Frame as: "Here's what the research says" section

This split creates breathing room on mobile and lets the reader process the product first, then the science. The current version asks them to hold both simultaneously, which is a lot for a skeptical parent scanning on their phone.

**Impact:** High. The mechanism section is the pivot of the entire email. If a parent doesn't understand why this product is different, nothing else matters. Making it more scannable directly improves conversion.

**Risk:** Low. The split doesn't change the content — just the visual organization. The email is already long enough that splitting a section actually improves the reading rhythm.
