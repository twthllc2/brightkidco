## 1. Big Idea

**"The Open Door"**

The visual and emotional metaphor is a door that can swing either way, stay or go, but never closes permanently. The email is an invitation, not a gate. The subscriber is in control. BrightKidCo stands at the threshold, holding the door open, waiting without pressure.

This the sunset flow's only moment of choice. The creative respects that dignity. No urgency. No countdown. Just a clear, warm path in either direction.

## 3. Custom Contraption / Illustration

**Contraption ID:** H2, "Threshold" (Open door with soft light)

**Description:**
A minimal line-art illustration of a simple wooden doorway shown from the inside looking out. The door stands open at a 45-degree angle. Beyond the threshold, a soft warm light spills in, rendered as a gentle radial warm-white-to-transparent gradient. The floor is a single horizontal line. No walls, no fixtures. The composition is intentionally bare: the door and the light are the entire story.

**Mood:** Quiet, warm, unhurried. This not a dramatic exit, it's a gentle moment of choice.

**Style:**
- Line weight: 2px, warm gray (#8A7A6A)
- Fill: None (negative space)
- Light gradient: `radial-gradient(circle at 60% 50%, #FFF5E8 0%, transparent 80%)`
- Canvas: 600×300px
- Alt text: "An open doorway with warm light spilling through, the choice is yours"

**Rationale :**
The door contraption is specifically designed for lifecycle emails where the subscriber faces a continuation-or-exit decision. It avoids "goodbye" imagery (closed doors, receding figures, dark pathways) and "urgency" imagery (clocks, countdowns, diminishing gradients). The open door with warm light signals: *you are welcome to stay, and leaving is also welcome.* This matches the S2 tone directive: "Respectful. No guilt. Clear. The door is always open." (S2 §2.7)

**Palette Chapter: "Warm Threshold"**

| Element | Color | Hex | Role |
|---------|-------|-----|------|
| Background | Cream paper | `#FCF8F3` | Canvas — soft, tactile, non-digital feel |

| CTA text | White | `#FFFFFF` | High contrast on button |
| Logo | Warm charcoal | `#2A2A2A` | Brand signature — maintains recognition |
| Divider | Warm linen | `#E8E0D8` | Subtle section break, warm tone |
| Accent (hero glow) | Soft amber | `#FFF5E8` | Light-through-door radiance |
| Footer text | Invisible gray | `#9A9A9A` | Legal — recede |

**Narrative arc:** The email moves from *warm invitation* (cream ground → amber glow hero) through *clear choice* (sage green CTA, the "stay" affirmation) to *gentle release* (warm gray permission text) to *grounded closure* (Lena's warm, human sign-off). No cool tones anywhere, every hex has a warm undertone, keeping the temperature consistently human and non-corporate.

**Rationale :**
The Warm Threshold palette is a subset of the "Earth & Warmth" chapter from the BrightKidCo color system. Sunset flow emails use the warmest sub-palette because they are saying goodbye, cool tones would feel clinical or dismissive. The sage green CTA (#3A8B6B) is deliberately softer than the standard brand green used in conversion emails (#2D8A5B or similar): lower saturation communicates "an option" rather than "a pitch."

## 7. Hidden Detail / Easter Egg

**Easter Egg, The Light Angle**

In thero illustration, the warm light from the doorway falls at an angle that, if you follow it to the bottom of the image, aligns with the start of the body text, as if the light is illuminating the words the subscriber is about to read. This a 2-degree easter egg: the light beam's lower edge is at ~15 degrees from horizontal, and the body text block's left edge subtly mirrors this angle with a 4px left indent on the first line.

**Copy Easter Egg, The Door Echo**

The phrase "our doors don't lock" in the body is echoed visually by the open-door illustration, the door in thero has no visible lock, handle, or latch mechanism. A subscriber who reopens the email and looks at the illustration after reading the copy will notice: *the door really doesn't have a lock.*

**Rationale :**
Easter eggs reward re-engagement (metaphorically perfect for a re-engagement email). The light-angle easter egg is subtle enough not to distract, but recognizable enough that a subscriber who notices it feels a moment of discovery. The door-with-no-lock reinforces the "door always open" message at a subconscious level.

**Recommended Motion (for email clients that support it, Apple Mail, iOS Mail, Samsung Mail):**

| | `transform: translateY(-1px)` | | |
| Divider line | Soft fade-in from left edge | 1.5s | On scroll/load |
| | (CSS keyframe — only for clients supporting) | | |

**No motion in:** Body text, sign-off, permission paragraph, footer. These remain static to preserve readability and avoid feeling "flashy" in a goodbye context.

**Rationale :**
Motion in lifecycle emails must be nearly imperceptible. The pulsing light is the only animated element, and it's so slow (4s loop) that it reads as ambient warmth rather than a visual effect. The CTA hover is a standard micro-interaction for any button. No entrance animations, no slides, no reveals, the email arrives fully formed and still, commanding attention through its quiet presence.

**Fallback for Gmail/Outlook (no animation support):** Static hero image with the full-opacity light gradient. The static version is nearly identical to the animated version at its peak opacity, no content is lost, only the ambient pulse.

## 9. Level Calibration (R1-R6 Cross-Level)

Since this a cross-level email (single copy for all 4 levels), it uses the R1-R6 calibration framework:

| Calibration | Application in Copy | Evidence |
|-------------|---------------------|----------|
| **R1 — Symptom neutral** | No level-specific symptom language. Uses universal hook: "We haven't heard from you" | S2 §1.22 |
| **R2 — Emotion universal** | "This stuff is hard" — applies to L1 (deadline stress), L2 (method fatigue), L3 (resignation), GF (overwhelm) | 05-customer-language-bank.md §A1-A3 |
| **R3 — Voice inclusive** | Lena's story ("older son was eight") anchors the L3/GF perspective; "tried everything" validates L2; breaks + starting again validates L1 | 11-brand-voice-reference.md §9.5 |
| **R4 — Offer neutral** | No product offer — the CTA is a mailing-list choice, not a purchase | S2 §1.22 |
| **R5 — No proof point** | No testimonials, stats, or citations. This email carries zero persuasion weight — it's purely a choice | S2 §5.1 (40% Lena / 10% Mech / 50% Goodbye) |
| **R6 — CTA universal** | "Yes, keep me updated" — works for all levels. Low aggression, permission frame | S2 §1.22; 15-level-messaging-matrix.md §5 |

**Hope/Realism calibration:** For a cross-level sunset email, the ratio is 30% / 70% (slightly realism-dominant). The copy does not promise resolution, training, or product benefits. It promises only one thing: if you stay, we'll keep sending you updates. The realism comes from the honest framing: "If not, no hard feelings."

**Why no level-specific variants:** Sunset E1 is intentionally a single variant because the decision is not about pain points or motivations, it's about whether the subscriber wants to remain engaged at all. Segmented copy at this stage would feel manipulative (V4 violation). The cross-level approach is more honest and therefore more aligned with Lena's voice.
