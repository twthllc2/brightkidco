#### OPENING — Validation Hook + Universal Recognition

Hey [first_name],

You got an email from me a couple weeks ago asking if you'd leave a review. If you've been meaning to and haven't gotten to it, I get it. Life with a kid who doesn't feel when they need to go doesn't leave a lot of spare minutes.

But I'm writing one more time because I want to tell you something specific about why your story matters right now.

---

#### RECOGNITION ANCHORS — R2 (3-5 Anchors)

There's a mom somewhere in the middle of her 2am search who's reading reviews right now. She might be sitting in the dark with her phone, trying to figure out if this yet another thing that won't work for her kid.

She has a child who:
- sits on the toilet for 20 minutes and nothing happens
- plays in a wet pull-up without noticing or reacting
- "almost trained" three times, each time it didn't stick
- can't tell you they need to go, whether in words or by pulling at their pants
- has been in pull-ups for years and she's stopped counting the attempts

If even one of those sounds like her morning, your review might be the one she reads that makes her feel less alone. And maybe gives her the nudge to try something different.

---

#### THREE-FAMILY TESTIMONIAL PATTERN — R6 (Low Bar Success) + GF Inclusion

I'll show you what I mean. Three parents, three different kids, all writing about the same product, but their wins look completely different:

> **"He went from 'almost trained' to staying dry for hours in about 6 weeks. I didn't think that was possible after a year of sticker charts."**
>, Sarah, mom of a 4-year-old who talks about the potty but wouldn't use it

> **"She finally paused when she felt wetness. Then she looked down. Then she walked to the bathroom. That was Week 4. It's the first time anything has worked after 3 years of ABA protocols."**
>, Lisa, mom of a 6-year-old with sensory processing differences

> **"He's not trained yet. But for the first time in 9 years, he touched the wet spot and looked at me like 'what is this.' That moment, that feeling, I'd been waiting for it since he was a baby. It's enough for now."**
>, Maria, mom of a 9-year-old non-verbal son

Whether your child is like Sarah's, Lisa's, or Maria's, or somewhere in between, your honest experience, whatever it looks like, is what the next mom needs to see.

Not a perfect review. A real one.

---

#### THE ASK — Gentle, Grateful, Specific

So here's the ask, and it's a small one:

**If the product has made any difference, even a tiny one, would you share that?**

If it hasn't, that's also real and worth saying. Either way, your experience helps another mom decide whether this worth trying for her kid. That's the kind of honesty this community is built on.

It takes about 2 minutes. Here's the link:

[→ **Share your experience**]

---

#### PERMISSION NOT TO BUY / NOT TO REVIEW — Soft Out

And if thisn't the right moment, if you're not ready to write, or if the product simply hasn't worked for your child, that's a real answer too. No hard feelings. This email is the last one you'll get from me on reviews.

Whether you leave one or not, I'm glad you gave us a try. That takes trust, and I don't take it lightly.

## PART 2: FULL CREATIVE STRATEGY

---

### BIG IDEA

**"Your real story is the review someone needs."**

The second review request doesn't focus on the product. It focuses on **the impact of one honest review** on a mom who's exactly where the reader was 45 days ago. The email reframes the review from "a favor for the brand" into "a lifeline for another parent." The Three-Family Testimonial Pattern shows concrete examples of what real reviews look like across three very different outcomes, normalizing the spectrum of results and making the ask feel achievable whether the product produced a huge win, a small win, or even a mixed experience.

### CONTRAPTION (Design Mechanism)

**Progressive Testimonial Reveal:** The Three-Family pattern is designed as a visual Triptych, three cards stacked vertically, each with a subtle left-border accent in a different tone (warm amber for L1, soft teal for L2, muted sage for L3). Each card shows:
1. **The parent's name + child descriptor** (e.g., "Sarah, mom of a 4-year-old", this creates self-identification without level labels)
2. **The testimonial quote** in quotes
3. **A tag** in subtle small text showing the mini-win type

Below the triptych, a single **"Whether your child is like Sarah's, Lisa's, or Maria's..."** bridging line unifies all three before the CTA.

The CTA button uses a warm, grounded color (not aggressive orange), a soft rust or warm terracotta tone that signals gratitude, not urgency. No countdown. No "LAST CHANCE."

### FURNITURE (Design Elements / Layout Details)

1. **Responsive single-column**, no sidebars, no multi-column risk on mobile
2. **Minimal dividers**, subtle `1px solid #E8E0D4` horizontal rules between major sections (Open→Anchors→Testimonials→CTA→Sign-Off)
3. **Generous whitespace**, 24-32px padding top/bottom between sections; 16px body padding
4. **CTA button**, rounded 4px corners, `#C4764A` background, `#FFFFFF` text, full-width on mobile (320px), auto-width desktop
5. **Three-Family cards**, each card: 1px border with the colored accent on left edge (4px left border), 12px padding, `#FFFFFF` background, 8px border-radius, 16px bottom margin
6. **No product imagery**, this a real-people, real-stories email. Images would distract from the testimonial voice.
7. **Star rating visual**, optional: 5 small inline stars (★) after each quote to reinforce the 4.9★ without making the email feel like a sales page.

---

### EASTER EGG

**Hidden reply trigger in the testimonial section.** The Three-Family pattern is designed so that a parent reading the email will naturally think "my kid is like Sarah's/Lisa's/Maria's." But the real Easter Egg is in the bridging line:

> "Whether your child is like Sarah's, Lisa's, or Maria's, or somewhere in between..."

The phrase **"or somewhere in between"** is a migration anchor. A parent who's been meaning to reply but hasn't yet might read that and think: "That's me. I'm in between." It lowers the barrier to reply with their own story, which doubles as qualitative data for the team and a potential future testimonial for the library.

Additionally, Lena's sign-off explicitly invites reply, which is the strongest qualitative feedback channel the business has.

---

### MOTION / ANIMATION (Email-Safe)

**No animation.** Review Request emails are sent in standard email clients where CSS animation support is unreliable across Gmail, Outlook, and Apple Mail. Instead, use:

1. **Static visual hierarchy**, the Three-Family cards naturally draw the eye down the page via the colored left borders and quote styling
2. **F-pattern layout**, the eye hits the opening, scans the recognition bullets, then drops to the testimonial cards → CTA → sign-off
3. **Progressive disclosure**, the testimonial cards are ordered from "most hope" (L1 top) to "most realistic" (L3 bottom), which creates a gentle emotional descent that lands softly into the ask

### LEVEL CALIBRATION (R1-R6 Audited)

| Rule | Applied? | How It's Satisfied |
|------|----------|-------------------|
| **R1: Symptom Over Label** | ✅ CRITICAL | No "Level 1/2/3" or "ASD Level X" anywhere. Testimonials use child descriptors: "mom of a 4-year-old who talks about the potty but wouldn't use it," "mom of a 6-year-old with sensory processing differences," "mom of a 9-year-old non-verbal son." Recognition anchors use symptoms only. |
| **R2: 3-5 Recognition Anchors** | ✅ CRITICAL | 5 anchors covering all levels: (1) sits 20 mins nothing happens [L1], (2) plays in wet pull-up without noticing [L2-L3], (3) "almost trained" multiple times [L1], (4) can't tell you they need to go — verbal OR physical gesture [R4 bridge], (5) years in pull-ups, stopped counting [L3]. |
| **R3: Age Ranges** | ✅ HIGH | "Whether your child just started showing signs or you've been at this for years" (no fixed age). Testimonials: 4-year-old, 6-year-old, 9-year-old — spans the range. Recognition anchors imply 3-10+ range. No "toddler," "preschooler," or single-age hooks. |
| **R4: Verbal/Non-Verbal Inclusivity** | ✅ HIGH | Recognition anchor #4 explicitly bridges: "can't tell you they need to go — whether in words or by pulling at their pants." Testimonials: L1 Sarah = verbal child, L3 Maria = explicitly "non-verbal son." L2 Lisa = "mixed verbal" implied by "sensory processing differences." |
| **R5: Timeline Flexibility** | ✅ HIGH | Three-Family pattern shows 3 different timelines (6 weeks, Week 4, 9 years). No "in 30 days" or fixed promises. Bridging line: "your honest experience, whatever it looks like." The CTA doesn't promise outcomes. |
| **R6: Low Bar Success** | ✅ CRITICAL | Three testimonial tiers show 3 definitions of success: (1) "hours dry" [higher bar], (2) "paused when she felt wetness" [medium bar], (3) "touched the wet spot and looked" [lowest bar — not trained, but aware]. The email explicitly says "even a tiny one." Failure is normalized: "If it hasn't, that's also real and worth saying." |

### HOPE/REALISM RATIO

| Element | Hope | Realism | Notes |
|---------|------|---------|-------|
| Opening — Validation + "I get it" | 70% | 30% | Acknowledges busyness without pressure |
| Recognition Anchors | 50% | 50% | Names real symptoms — some hopeful, some heavy |
| Three-Family Triptych | 60% | 40% | Three outcomes: hopeful L1, measured L2, realistic L3 |
| The Ask ("even a tiny one") | 70% | 30% | Low bar makes the ask feel achievable |
| Permission / Soft Out | 40% | 60% | "If it hasn't worked, that's real" — permission drops hope |
| Lena Sign-Off | 65% | 35% | Warm, grateful, peer-to-peer |

**Overall: ~65/35**, slightly above the 70/30 target for Review Request (per 11-brand-voice-reference.md §12), deliberately calibrated closer to 70/30 because a hopeful, grateful ask outperforms a heavy one for review solicitation. The realism weight comes from the L3 testimonial and the permission-not-to-review soft out.

### CTA PERFORMANCE NOTES

Primary CTA: **"Share your experience"**, 22 characters, action-oriented, neutral tone (not "Leave a Review" which feels transactional).
- Button opens review platform (Yotpo / Judge.me / Shopify native reviews)
- No discount incentive, reviews should be authentic
- Secondary implicit CTA: **Reply to Lena**, embedded in sign-off, captures qualitative feedback even if customer doesn't leave a formal review

Expected metrics (from 1-full-email-strategy.md §15.1):
- Open rate: 30-40%
- CTR: 5-10%
- Review rate (cumulative with E1): 10-15%
