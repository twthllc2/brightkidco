## 6. STRATEGY

**Strategic purpose:** Re-engagement and churn prevention at Day 29.

**Context:** By Day 29, the initial purchase excitement has faded. Parents are in the "plateau" the email describes — they've been using BSL underwear for 3-4 weeks, the novelty is gone, and they're questioning whether it works. This email's strategic job is to prevent three outcomes:
1. Parent stops using the product (churn)
2. Parent requests a refund (revenue loss)
3. Parent disengages from the email sequence (list decay)

**How it achieves this:**
- **Normalizes the plateau.** By naming it and explaining the neuroscience, the email transforms "nothing is happening" into "the most important phase." This directly counters churn motivation.
- **Gives a tracking framework.** Parents who track feel like they're making progress even when outcomes aren't visible. Tracking = engagement = continued use.
- **Mentions the guarantee early.** "Full refund, no questions" at line 123 de-risks the reader's emotional investment. Paradoxically, mentioning the refund reduces refund requests — the parent feels no pressure, so they're more likely to continue.
- **P.S. reply offer.** The free tracking guide drives replies, which improves inbox placement for subsequent emails. This is a long-game play for E3-E6 deliverability.

**Strategic gap:** The email doesn't reference the next email in the sequence. There's no "Next week, I'll share..." or "In my next email..." teaser. For E2, a forward-looking hook would keep the reader anticipating E3 and reduce the chance they unsubscribe before it arrives.

**Strategic score: 8/10.** Excellent churn prevention. Strong trust mechanics. Missing a forward-hook to E3.

## 8. ONE FIX

**Problem:** The email is 1,250 words. For a Day 29 re-engagement email in a nurturing sequence, this is long. The signal catalog table (7 rows), the tracking framework (5 items), and the timeline section (3 tiers) each add density. A parent scrolling on mobile at 7am with coffee in one hand and a toddler hanging off the other will hit the fold and see a wall of text before reaching the emotional payoff at the close.

**The fix:** Cut the signal catalog from 7 rows to 4. Keep: the pause, the waistband touch, the dry stretch extension, and the post-accident signal. Remove: the facial expression, the looking down, and the vocalization rows. These three are less actionable for a parent scanning quickly — they're interesting but not essential. The remaining four are the most recognizable micro-signals and the ones a parent is most likely to notice in real time.

This single cut removes ~150 words, reduces the table height on mobile by 43%, and pushes the tracking framework and timeline sections above the fold. The email lands at ~1,100 words — still comprehensive, but no longer demanding.

The three removed signals can appear in E3 or E4 when the parent is deeper in the sequence and more invested in the detail.
