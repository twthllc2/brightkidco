Oh Crap stressed you both out. Sticker charts didn't stick. Timer sits became a battle. You did everything the books told you — M&Ms, naked weekend, eight-hour holds. Every method assumed the same thing: that {{ first_name }} can feel when he needs to go.

He can't. Not because he's stubborn. Because the "I need to pee" signal doesn't arrive in time.

 

You didn't fail. The methods did.

Sticker charts, timer sits, Oh Crap — they all assume the "I need to go" feeling arrives loud and clear. But for many autistic children, that signal is delayed or absent entirely.

That's not defiance. That's neurology.

 

The Interoception Gap

This is called the interoception gap — the space between the body sending a signal and the brain receiving it. The signal arrives, but milliseconds or minutes too late.
