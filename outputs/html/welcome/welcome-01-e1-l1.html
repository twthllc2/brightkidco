He talks about the potty

He talks about the potty, "I need to go," he says, looking right at you. Then he pees on the floor 30 seconds later.

"He says 'I need to pee' AFTER it's already happened."

Your child knows what the potty is. He wants to use it. He tells you he's going. But his body doesn't give him the message until the toilet is irrelevant.

 

This isn't your fault. And it's not his.

For months, maybe longer, you've been asking the same questions. Is he being stubborn? Does he need more time? Did I do something wrong?

Here's what almost no one told {{ first_name }}: {{ first_name }}'s brain and bladder are sending messages on different frequencies. There's nothing wrong with either one. They just aren't connected the way traditional potty training assumes they are.

"He had a perfect week, then a month of accidents."

 

The science behind the gap

This gap has a name: interoception — the body's ability to sense internal signals like hunger, thirst, and the need to use the bathroom.

>90%

of autistic children have sensory processing differences.

Leekam et al. (2007); Ben-Sasson et al. (2009)

 

Interoception can be trained.

Hample, Mahler & Amspacher (2020) showed that targeted interoception intervention produces measurable improvement in body awareness. The gap isn't permanent.
