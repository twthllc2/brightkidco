If they don't feel when they need to go, here's why — and what that means for every method you've tried.͏͏͏͏͏͏͏͏͏͏͏͏͏͏͏͏͏͏͏͏͏͏͏͏͏͏͏͏͏͏

Whether your child is 3 or 10, verbal or non-verbal, diagnosed or still figuring it out, if they don't feel when they need to go, you're in the right place. The signal between body and brain travels on a different frequency for many kids. That's not failure. That's neurology.

 

Chances are you've seen some of these:

• He sits on the toilet for 20 minutes. Nothing happens. Then he pees 30 seconds after standing up.
• She plays in a wet pull-up without noticing.
• He's 'almost trained' more than once.
• She knows what to do but the signal arrives too late.
 

Here's why the methods haven't worked:

Interoception is the body's ability to sense internal signals like hunger, thirst, and bladder fullness. Research shows this works differently in many kids (Barmpagiannis & Baldimtsi 2025, 49-study review). Standard methods assume the signal is there. If it's not, they can't work.
