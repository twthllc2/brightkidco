Interoception: why some kids feel the signal and others don't.

You have five senses you can name: sight, sound, touch, taste, smell. But there's a sixth sense you've been using your whole life without noticing — interoception.

Interoception is the sense of what's happening inside your body. It's how you know you're hungry, thirsty, hot, cold, or need to use the bathroom. Most people feel these signals automatically. But for many autistic children, the interoception system processes those signals differently — faintly, late, or not at all.

This isn't a behavior choice. It's a neurological wiring difference.

Why pull-ups make it harder:

Pull-ups are designed with wicking technology that instantly pulls moisture away from the skin. That's great for preventing rashes. But for a child whose interoception signals are already weak, it removes the only sensation that could train the nervous system. The body does its thing, the pull-up absorbs, and the child learns nothing. No sensation = no learning.

How the learning unfolds

The Body-Signal Learning Layer works directly with interoception. When a child wets the underwear, they feel it immediately — a cool, wet sensation against the skin. That sensation is the input their nervous system needs to start building the connection.

Over time, the brain learns to recognise the precursor signals. The sequence goes from:

"I'm wet" (after) → "I'm getting wet" (during) → "I need to go" (before)

Each stage is a genuine neurological achievement. With a verbal child, you have the advantage that they can tell you when they notice something. That accelerates the loop.

What the research says:

Kelly Mahler, an occupational therapist leading interoception research, found that intentional interoceptive activities — like wearing sensory-awareness clothing — can significantly improve body-signal recognition in autistic individuals. The nervous system can learn this skill at any age.
