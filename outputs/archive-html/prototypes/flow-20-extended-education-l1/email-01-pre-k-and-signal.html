He gets it but doesn't get it

The Level 1 paradox — and the science that explains it.

Hi {{first_name}},

You've probably noticed the pattern by now: your child talks about the potty, sometimes even walks toward it, sits for a moment — and then nothing. Five minutes later, the pull-up or underwear is wet.

It's one of the most frustrating things about Level 1 autism and potty learning. He gets it intellectually, but his body isn't sending the signal.

That's not defiance. It's not stubbornness. It's a signal-timing gap — and it's a sensory problem, not a behavior problem.

What's actually happening:

Most children feel the "I need to go" signal building over 10–30 seconds. Your child's brain processes the signal too — but it arrives at the last second, right when it's too late. He's not ignoring it. His nervous system hasn't learned to recognise the early warning signs.

How the Learning Layer helps

The Body-Signal Learning Layer works by creating a sensation that does arrive early enough for his nervous system to notice — a wet signal that's immediate and unmistakable, unlike a pull-up that wicks everything away.

Over time, his brain starts associating that wet sensation with the moment before it happens. The signal arrives earlier. The alert becomes actionable. That's how the gap closes — not through effort, but through exposure and time.

For a verbal child like yours, the timeline is often shorter — many Level 1 parents report first signals within 2–4 weeks of consistent wear, with stronger awareness building over 6–10 weeks.

For Pre-K parents:

If your child is starting Pre-K in the next few months, you have a real advantage: a clear time horizon that gives your child's nervous system a defined window to adapt. Start now, stay consistent, and most families see meaningful progress before the first day of school.
