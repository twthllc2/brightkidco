How to track your child's body-signal progress — and why the small things matter more than the big ones.

The progress you can't see

Tracking mini-wins that matter more than being "fully trained."

One of the hardest parts of this process is knowing what to look for. Most of us are trained to measure success by one metric: did they use the toilet? And when the answer is "no" day after day, it feels like nothing is working.

But with body-signal learning, progress doesn't start at the toilet. It starts before that.
Real mini-wins to watch for

1. Pause. They stop mid-play and look down. Even if the accident happens right after — that pause is the signal arriving.

2. Tug. They pull at the waistband or touch the wet spot afterward. They're noticing the result — which is the first step toward noticing the process.

3. Squat or shift. A subtle body change before wetting. Many kids do a small squat, bounce, or leg-cross when the release is coming.

4. Vocalization. A sound, a word, or a gesture that indicates awareness — even if it's after the fact. "Uh oh." "Wet." Pointing down.

5. Dry stretches. Staying dry for 1.5–2 hours instead of 30–45 minutes. This means the signal is starting to register.

6. Initiation. Any move toward the bathroom. Even if they don't make it. Even if they get there and nothing happens.

A simple tracking method

Keep a small note on your phone or a sticky note on the fridge. Each day, write down one thing your child did that showed awareness — no matter how small.

On days where nothing seems to happen, write "wore the underwear." That counts too. Consistency of input is the single most important factor.

Over two weeks, you'll see patterns: certain times of day, certain activities, certain body cues. That data is gold.
