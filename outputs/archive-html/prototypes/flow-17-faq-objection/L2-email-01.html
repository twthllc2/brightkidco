Let me guess — you've worked with a BCBA. Maybe more than one. You've tried social stories, picture cards, scheduled sits, maybe even a private consultant. Each time you thought, "This is the one." And each time, the progress faded.

Here's what I want to say clearly: Your BCBA did everything right — for a child whose internal signal already arrives. The methods she used are evidence-based. They work for kids who feel the "I need to go" sensation. The problem isn't the method. It's that your child's body may not be sending that signal at all.

This is not behavioral. It's neurological.

Research shows that many autistic children have reduced interoception — the body's ability to feel internal signals like a full bladder. Without that signal arriving at the right time, no behavioral reward system can create awareness. The signal has to come first.

Interoception researcher Kelly Mahler calls it the "eighth sensory system" — and it's the one most potty training methods unknowingly depend on. A 2025 review of 49 studies confirmed that interoceptive differences are well-established in autism. (Barmpagiannis et al., 2025)

This isn't a gap in your BCBA's expertise. It's a gap in what behavioral methods can access. ABA builds the behavioral foundation — the routines, the communication, the tolerance. What ABA can't do is create a body-sensing signal that hasn't arrived yet.

That's where the Body-Signal Learning Layer™ comes in — not as a replacement for your BCBA's work, but as a sensory bridge that behavioral methods can then build on.
