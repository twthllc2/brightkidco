Three weeks in — the most common challenges

And why regression isn't failure — it's recalibration

 

Hi {first_name},

Three weeks. That's a real commitment. I want to talk about two things that come up at this stage — because almost every parent hits one of them.

1. "Progress feels too slow"

This is the #1 concern at Week 3. If you've seen some mini-wins but nothing dramatic, that's exactly where you should be. The kind of learning happening right now — sensory pathway mapping — doesn't happen in a straight line.

Think of it like learning to hear a new language. At first, everything sounds like noise. Then one word separates from the background. Then another. Then suddenly you realize you understand whole phrases. That's what's happening inside your child's nervous system — signal separation, one synapse at a time.

2. "We saw progress — now it feels like backsliding"

This is the one I really want to talk about. If your child showed awareness (a pause, a look, a longer dry stretch) and then seemed to stop — that's not regression in the way you think.

It's sensory recalibration.

Here's what happens: the brain detects the new sensation (the wetness signal). It starts building a pathway. Then it hits a point where the pathway needs to be reorganized — like a construction site rerouting traffic. During that reorganization, external progress pauses. That's not losing ground. That's the brain consolidating.

 

Research confirms this pattern — interoception resolution in autism follows a non-linear trajectory with natural consolidation periods (Nicholson et al., 2019). The pause is part of the process.

 
 

What to do right now

Keep doing what you're doing. Consistency is the only thing that matters at this stage. The underwear goes on in the morning. The wetness signal keeps arriving. The brain keeps learning — even when you can't see it on the outside.

• Don't add pressure. Don't ramp up "potty sits." The learning is happening in the underwear, not the toilet.
• Watch for the same mini-wins. If they paused once, they'll pause again — consolidation takes time.
• Celebrate the pauses, not the outcomes. Your job is to be the observer, not the trainer.

You've come this far. Three weeks of consistent body-signal learning is more than most families attempt. You're building something real — even when it doesn't feel like it.
