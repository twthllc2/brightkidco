Week 1 looks like nothing happening. That's by design.

Think of it this way: for years, your child's nervous system has been trained to ignore the wetness signal. Pull-ups are engineered to wick moisture away — every time your child pees in a pull-up, their brain gets zero feedback. Nothing to learn.

Now, for the first time, the Body-Signal Learning Layer is creating feedback. Your child's brain has to figure out "what is this new sensation?" That takes time. It's a neurological process — not a behavioral one.

What's actually happening inside:

Interoception — the ability to feel what's happening in your body — is an 8th sensory system that develops differently in autistic children. Research (Hample, Mahler & Amspacher, 2020) shows that interoception can be trained. The underwear is literally teaching your child's brain to recognize a sensation it's been ignoring. That rewiring doesn't happen in 3 days. It happens in weeks.

 

Hold the line. Here's what Week 2 typically brings:

Most parents start seeing the first real shifts in Week 2–3. Not "using the potty." That's further ahead. But:

  • A pause — that moment where they stop mid-activity and look down
  • A pull at the wet fabric — they're registering the sensation
  • A longer dry window — from 20 minutes to 45 minutes

Those are not random. Those are the first cracks in the learning barrier.

 
 

I know Week 1 can feel discouraging. You put in the effort, you watched, and maybe nothing happened. That's not a sign to stop — it's a sign the process has started. You just can't see it yet.

The parents who see results are the ones who don't stop in Week 1. Hold the line.
