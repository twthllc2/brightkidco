You haven’t failed

The problem wasn’t you — it was the piece nobody told you about.

Let me guess the thought that creeps in at 2 AM: “Maybe I’m not doing this right.” “Maybe his autism is too much for any method.” “Maybe I started too late.”

I’ve been there. Both of my sons put me through that guilt seesaw — “Is it me, is it him, is it the method?”

Here’s what I wish someone had told me: the CDC-funded SEED-2 study confirmed that 49% of autistic 4- and 5-year-olds are not toilet trained. That’s not bad parenting. That’s a documented neurological difference.

Your child’s body isn’t sending the signal the way a neurotypical child’s does. That’s not something you caused, and it’s not something you can fix with more consistency, more rewards, or more scheduled sits.

Research shows that only 19% of autistic children can reliably recognize thirst — one of the most basic internal signals. Recognizing a full bladder is even harder. Your child isn’t being defiant. The signal isn’t arriving.

You’ve been fighting a battle where the tools you were given were designed for someone else’s nervous system. That’s not your failure. And now you know the real problem.

What the research says

  • Nicholson et al. (2019) — Children with autism have significantly reduced interoception, confirmed across multiple measures.
  • Barmpagiannis & Baldimtsi (2025) — Systematic review of 49 studies confirms interoceptive differences are a consistent feature of autism.
  • Mahler (2016) — Interoception is the eighth sensory system, and it CAN be trained with structured practice.
