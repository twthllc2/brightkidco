Why pull-ups block the signal
The technology that keeps your child dry may also be keeping them from learning.
[IMAGE: Pull-ups vs Body-Signal Layer — signal comparison]

Hi {first_name},

This is a hard topic to address, so I'm going to be straightforward: if your child is still in pull-ups during the day, the very technology that keeps them comfortable may be preventing the signal from arriving.

Here's the mechanism. Pull-ups are designed with moisture-wicking technology that pulls wetness away from the skin and traps it in an absorbent core. The child feels dry — even when they're wet. And if they feel dry, their nervous system gets no feedback.

The Body-Signal Learning Layer works differently. It's designed to preserve a localized wetness sensation against the skin for 30–60 seconds. That sustained feedback is what teaches the nervous system: "This feeling means something."

One way to think about it:

Pull-ups ask: "How can we keep the child comfortable?"

Body-Signal Layer asks: "How can we teach the child to notice the signal?"

Both are valid questions — but they have opposite answers.

I want to be careful here. I'm not telling you to throw away all your pull-ups today. Many Level 2 families use pull-ups for sleep, for long car rides, for times when a wet accident would cause more stress than it's worth. That's practical parenting, and there's nothing wrong with it.

But I would invite you to consider: what if daytime pull-ups are the single biggest barrier to progress? Not because they're "bad" — but because their design directly counteracts the mechanism your child needs to develop awareness.

"We switched to daytime underwear-only at Week 5. The first two days were rough — more laundry, more cleanup. But by Day 3, my son started pausing before accidents. That never happened in pull-ups. Ever."

A practical path if you want to try this: start with just one hour after breakfast when your child's body is calmest. Use the Body-Signal Layer during that hour instead of the pull-up. Build from there. Add alongside, don't fight the pull-up.

If your child isn't showing awareness after 6–8 weeks of consistent wear, daytime pull-up removal might be the missing step. Some children's nervous systems need the full sensory signal before they can begin to recognize it.
