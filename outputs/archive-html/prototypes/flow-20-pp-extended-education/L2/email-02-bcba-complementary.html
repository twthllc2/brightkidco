Hi {first_name},

If you're working with a BCBA or OT — and most Level 2 families are — you might be wondering how this product fits into an already full therapy schedule. I get that. You've invested years and significant resources into your child's team.

Let me be clear: this is not a replacement for your BCBA. It's a sensory tool that works alongside the behavioral framework they've built.

Here's the simple breakdown:

ABA addresses: The behavior — scheduled sits, reinforcement, antecedent strategies, prompt-fading.

OT addresses: The sensory processing — tactile defensiveness, proprioceptive input, regulation.

Body-Signal Layer™ addresses: The interoceptive signal — teaching the nervous system to recognize the feeling of a full bladder before the behavior needs to happen.

Think of it like this: your BCBA can teach your child to sit on the toilet, to follow a schedule, to communicate the need. But they can't teach the sensation of needing to go. That has to come from the nervous system itself — and that's what the Body-Signal Layer supports.

One BCBA we work with put it well:

"We can shape the behavior once the sensation registers. But if the brain isn't receiving the signal, there's nothing to reinforce. The Body-Signal Layer is filling a gap that behavior analysis alone can't reach."

— BCBA, 8 years experience, specializing in autism toileting

Practical suggestion: Bring 30 days of data to your next BCBA session. Note any moments where your child paused, looked down, or showed awareness — even if they didn't make it to the toilet. That data is valuable for both the behavioral and sensory sides of the team.

Many BCBAs incorporate sensory feedback into their protocols once they see evidence that the signal is emerging. The behavioral + sensory combination is where real, lasting progress happens.
