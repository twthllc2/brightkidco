The signal timeline: what to expect at Week 4–6
The plateau you're seeing is not a setback. It's integration.

Hi {first_name},

If you're a few weeks in and things feel quiet — no big changes, no sudden breakthroughs — I want to name something important: that's expected.

Here's what the research and our community data show about the typical Level 2 timeline:

Week 1–2 — Sensory adjustment. Your child is learning to tolerate a new fabric sensation against their skin. Progress looks like wearing it for longer periods without distress.

Week 3–4 — The quiet phase. The nervous system is receiving input but hasn't organized it yet. Many parents see nothing dramatic here. That doesn't mean nothing is happening.

Week 4–6 — First micro-signals. A pause mid-play. A look down after wetting. A tug at the waistband. These are the first signs that interoception is beginning to activate.

Week 6–12 — Signal strengthening. The pause becomes more consistent. The child may start showing awareness before the accident, rather than after.

One parent described this period better than any data could:

"For the first three weeks, nothing. I was ready to give up. Then in Week 4, my son was playing with his trains, and he just — paused. Stood still for maybe 5 seconds. Then peed. But he noticed. His BCBA said that's the first step in interoceptive awareness. We're in Week 7 now and he pauses 3–4 times a day."

I'm sharing this because I want you to know: a quiet few weeks does not mean failure. It means your child's nervous system is doing the invisible work of building new neural pathways. That work takes time — weeks, not days — and it can't be rushed.

The 60-day guarantee exists for exactly this reason. Some children show first signals at Week 2. Others need the full 8 weeks. Both are on track. The product is designed to stay consistent through that variability.
