Interoception: the 8th sensory system
This is the piece your child's nervous system has been waiting for.
[IMAGE: Illustration of interoceptive pathway — brain receiving body signals]

Hi {first_name},

You've been at this for a few weeks now. By this point, you've probably noticed something: the underwear isn't a magic switch. Your child doesn't suddenly know when to go.

That's not a failure of the product. That's how the nervous system works — and I want to explain why.

There's a concept called interoception. Researcher Kelly Mahler describes it as the 8th sensory system [Mahler, 2015] — separate from sight, sound, touch, taste, smell, vestibular, and proprioception. Interoception is the ability to feel what's happening inside your body.

A full bladder. An empty stomach. A racing heart. The urge to yawn. Most people feel these signals automatically. But for many autistic children, the interoceptive signal is quiet, delayed, or doesn't register at all.

Think of it this way:

Your BCBA's protocol teaches what to do when the signal arrives.

The Body-Signal Learning Layer™ teaches the brain to detect the signal in the first place.

The distinction matters more than you might think. Your BCBA has been doing essential work — building the behavioral scaffold, establishing routines, shaping the response. But behavior-based protocols work with the assumption that the internal sensation is already present. They teach the reaction. They can't teach the sensation.

Here's what Michael Kelly, a pediatric OT who works extensively with interoception, says about this:

"We spend so much time teaching kids what to do that we forget to teach them how to feel what their body is telling them. The feeling has to come first."

The Body-Signal Learning Layer works with interoception — providing sustained, localized wetness feedback that the nervous system can learn to recognize. It's not replacing ABA. It's adding the sensory piece ABA can't access.

This is why some children start showing awareness around Week 3–4: not because they've learned a new behavior, but because their nervous system has begun noticing something it couldn't feel before.

Bring this to your next BCBA session. Ask about interoception. Many OTs already work with it. The behavioral and the sensory working together is where real progress happens.
