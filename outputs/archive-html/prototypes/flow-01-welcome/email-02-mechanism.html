THE MECHANISM

It's not defiance. It's a signal-timing gap.

Every human body has an internal messaging system called interoception — the ability to feel what's happening inside. Your heartbeat. Your hunger. The feeling that says "I need to use the bathroom."

Most children's brains receive this signal clearly and early. They learn to connect "I feel something" with "time to go to the potty."

For many autistic children, that signal arrives differently — it's quieter, slower, or doesn't register at all until it's too late. This isn't a behavior problem. It's a neurological timing difference.
WHY METHODS FAIL

Every method you tried assumed the signal was there.

Sticker charts — they reward a behavior your child can't yet control because they can't feel the trigger.

Timer sits — you're putting them on the toilet at intervals, but without the internal signal, it's just sitting.

The Oh Crap method — it relies on the child recognizing the urge. If the urge doesn't arrive, there's nothing to catch.

Naked training — it teaches the consequence of not using the toilet, but it can't teach the body to send the signal earlier.

Social stories and picture cards — they address understanding, not sensation. Your child may fully understand and still not feel it in time.
