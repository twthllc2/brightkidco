The science behind the gap

It was never about effort.

If you've been worried that he's "almost trained" for months — that he talks about the potty but can't reliably act on it — I want you to hear this: that's not a failure of effort. It's not a failure of parenting. It's a signal-timing gap.

Research from Nicholson et al. (2019) showed that autistic children process internal body signals like bladder fullness differently from typically developing kids. The signal arrives — just milliseconds too late. By the time he says "I need to go," it's already happening. That's why timed sits and sticker charts couldn't solve this. They assumed a signal that wasn't reaching his brain in time.

You didn't fail. The methods weren't built for his nervous system.

The mechanism

The interoception pathway

Here's what's actually happening inside his body:

Interoception is the "eighth sensory system" — the sense that tells us when we're hungry, thirsty, or need the bathroom. Kelly Mahler, the leading researcher on this, describes it as the body's internal communication network. For autistic children, that network has a delay — the signal from the bladder travels, but it doesn't arrive at the brain's "action center" with enough lead time to do anything about it.

This is where the Body-Signal Learning Layer comes in differently than anything you've tried before. It creates feedback — over and over, every time — that his brain can finally connect to the action of using the potty.

Think of it like this: you can't teach someone to catch a ball in the dark. The BSL layer turns the lights on. Repetition builds the neural pathway. That's the interoception pathway — and it's why Pre-K readiness for an autistic child isn't about training. It's about wiring.
