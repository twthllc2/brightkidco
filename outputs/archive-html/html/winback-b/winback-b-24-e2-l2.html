The core finding hasn't changed, but the evidence has deepened.

Nicholson et al. (2019) established that autistic children show diminished interoceptive accuracy — the brain doesn't receive the "I need to go" signal the same way. This has been confirmed by a 2025 systematic review of 49 studies (Barmpagiannis & Baldimtsi), making it one of the most robust findings in autism research.

But here's what's less commonly discussed — and what I want to zero in on today:

Key finding

Interoception can be trained.

Hample, Mahler & Amspacher (2020) showed that an 8-week targeted interoception intervention produced measurable improvements in body awareness. Not cured. Not "fixed." Trained. The brain can learn to recognize signals it's been missing — if it has the right input.
