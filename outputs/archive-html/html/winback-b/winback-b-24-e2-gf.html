That means every method that assumed the signal was there — sticker charts, timer sits, scheduled potty breaks, rewards systems — was working with an assumption that didn't match your child's neurology. Not your fault. Not the method's fault. Just a mismatch between the approach and the wiring.

The science — 49 studies

One finding from that review stood out: interoceptive differences are present across the full autism spectrum — from children who are verbal and academically on track to children with high support needs. It's not a "severe" thing. It's an autism thing.

A 2019 study from Nicholson and colleagues tested this directly: autistic children showed significantly reduced accuracy in sensing their own heartbeat — a basic internal signal that most people process unconsciously. The researchers called it "diminished interoceptive accuracy," and it resolved by adulthood. In other words: the signal arrives. It just arrives late. And that delay — those missed milliseconds — is the entire reason conventional potty training struggles.

And here's where pull-ups add another layer: their moisture-wicking technology — the feature parents pay a premium for — actively removes the one external signal the brain could use to learn. Pull-ups keep the child completely dry, which means the only feedback the body can register ("something happened") is wicked away before the brain can process it.

It's not that your child won't learn. It's that the feedback loop has been interrupted at both ends — the internal signal arrives too late, and the external signal is engineered away.
