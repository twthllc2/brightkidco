Why the signal doesn't arrive

It's not the method. It's the assumption.

Every sticker chart, timer sit, and reward system assumes your child feels the sensation of a full bladder and then chooses whether to act on it. But research consistently shows that many autistic children have a measurable difference in interoception — the eighth sensory system that tells the brain when we're hungry, thirsty, or need the bathroom. (Mahler 2016; Nicholson et al. 2019)

The signal from bladder to brain is delayed or absent. It's not defiance. It's neurology.

A 2025 systematic review of 49 studies confirmed that interoceptive differences are a well-established feature of autism (Barmpagiannis & Baldimtsi 2025). This is settled science.

And here's the part that matters for your child: interoception can be trained. Hample, Mahler & Amspacher (2020) demonstrated that interoception intervention produces measurable improvement. The childhood window is the intervention opportunity (Nicholson et al. 2019).
