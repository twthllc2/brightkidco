The mechanism

The feeling of "I need to go to the bathroom" is not a thought. It's a body signal — a neurological message that travels from your bladder to your brain. Your brain receives it, interprets it, and says: time to find a toilet.

For many autistic children, that message doesn't arrive in time. Or at all.

The science is clear on this. Nicholson et al. (2019) published in the Journal of Autism and Developmental Disorders — peer-reviewed, replicated, settled science — showing that autistic children have significantly diminished interoceptive accuracy: the ability to sense internal body states like bladder fullness, hunger, thirst, and temperature.

It's not training. It's neurology.

A landmark systematic review by Barmpagiannis & Baldimtsi (2025) examined 49 studies on interoception in autism. The conclusion: interoceptive differences are a well-established feature of autism. This is not one study. This is the weight of the evidence.

Here's what this means for you and your child:

Your child's brain and bladder are sending messages on different frequencies. The signal is either delayed, distorted, or absent. Every method you tried — scheduled sits, prompted toilet trips, sticker charts, social stories — assumed the signal was there. It wasn't.

You weren't failing. You were working with the wrong map.
