The deep mechanism

The interoception gap, explained simply

Let me explain what's actually happening — in plain language, not textbook jargon.

Interoception is the "eighth sensory system" — a term coined by researcher Kelly Mahler. It's the sense that tells you when you're hungry, thirsty, hot, cold, or need the bathroom. Most people don't think about it because it runs automatically. The brain monitors internal body states, detects changes, and sends alerts. Hunger grumbles. Thirst registers. Bladder fullness triggers the "I need to go" signal.

For autistic children, this system operates on a different frequency.

The Iqbal (2022) study found that 59% of autistic children cannot reliably signal when they need to urinate. 71% cannot signal defecation needs. That's not a training gap. That's a neurological difference in how the body-to-brain communication pathway functions.

Nicholson et al. (2019) measured interoceptive accuracy directly and found that autistic children scored significantly lower than their neurotypical peers. But here's the critical finding: the difference was in accuracy, not absence. The signal is there — it's just too late or too weak for the child to act on it in time.

Think of it like a phone call where the other person's voice cuts in and out. The call connects — the information arrives — but by the time you understand what was said, the conversation has moved on. The Body-Signal Learning Layer (BSL™) is like improving the signal strength on that call. It doesn't change what the caller is saying. It makes sure the message arrives clearly and stays long enough for the brain to register it.

Respecting the therapy relationship

I want to be careful here, because I know how much trust you've placed in your BCBA and OT. You've built a relationship over 4, 5, maybe 6 years. They know your child. They've helped you through meltdowns, IEP meetings, school placements. I'm not here to tell you they were wrong.

Here's what I will say: BCBA protocols are excellent at building external skills — requesting, waiting, transitioning, complying with adult directions. Toilet training within ABA typically relies on scheduled sits, positive reinforcement for successful voiding, and prompt-fading. These methods work for children who can feel the internal signal of bladder fullness.

For children whose interoception system processes signals differently, scheduled sits train waiting — not sensing. The child learns to sit when told, not to go when the body signals. That's why you see success at school (where the prompt system is consistent) and failure at home (where the natural cue should be driving the behaviour).

Your BCBA built the foundation. This adds the sensory layer that was missing.

What to expect

Most parents see the first sign of body awareness within 2 to 4 weeks. That first sign might be small — a pause, a look down, a hand reaching toward the waistband. It might not look like progress. But it's the first time the child's brain registered the signal at all.

Full training typically takes 8 to 12 weeks. Some children take longer, especially those with deeper interoceptive differences. Some show progress in waves — a good week followed by a quiet one. That's normal. That's how the nervous system learns.
