Here's what all three families have in common: their children weren't getting the body signal.

Interoception — the eighth sensory system, as researcher Kelly Mahler calls it — is how your brain knows when you're hungry, thirsty, or need the bathroom. For children with sensory processing differences, this signal is delayed, weakened, or absent.

"He doesn't have full bladder awareness yet and doesn't realise he needs to go until it's happening."
— Real parent

Research consistently shows this is a real, measurable neurological difference — not a behavior problem, not defiance, not a parenting gap. A 2025 review of 49 scientific studies confirmed this is settled science (Barmpagiannis & Baldimtsi 2025).

And here's the good news: interoception can be trained. Hample, Mahler & Amspacher (2020) showed that an 8-week intervention produced measurable improvement in body awareness in autistic children. The key is giving the brain the feedback it's been missing.
