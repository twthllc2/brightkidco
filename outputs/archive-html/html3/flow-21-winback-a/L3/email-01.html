It has been a while since we last wrote. If you've spent years trying — ABA, OT, scheduled sits, sticker charts, every method you could find — and your child still doesn't feel when they need to go, that is not a failure of effort.

One parent described it this way: "He's never shown a single sign of feeling wet or dirty." Another said: "There are no readiness signs. There never have been."

When the interoceptive signal never arrives, no amount of training can force it. That is not your fault. That is not your child's fault. It is a missing neurological connection — and it is more common than most people know.

Research has shown that children with autism often have a reduced ability to sense internal body signals (Nicholson et al., 2019). The SPARK study, by Wiggins and colleagues, found that nearly half of autistic 4–5 year olds are not yet toilet trained. Your child is not behind. The methods were designed for a signal that was never arriving.

As one mother put it: "He'll get there when he gets there. Or he won't. Either way, he's my son." That is not giving up. That is choosing peace after years of searching.

 

What one mom learned at age 9.5

A mom in the autism community shared that her son—non-verbal, high support needs—was 9.5 years old when his body finally registered what it never had before. Not fully trained. Just… aware. For the first time, he paused.

She had stopped actively training years earlier. She had made peace with the idea that he might never be dry. And then, quietly, something shifted.

"He paused. He felt something."

That moment—a pause—is a win at this stage. Not training. Not independent toileting. Just one moment where the brain and body connected. Some families see real change. Some don't. Both happen. Late is not never.

 

You have spent years keeping your child clean, comfortable, and safe. That is not "managing." That is love in action. If you ever want to explore body-signal awareness—at your pace, when you're ready—the option is here.
