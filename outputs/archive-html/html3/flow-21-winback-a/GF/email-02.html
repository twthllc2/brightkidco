Here's the thing most parents don't realize about potty training an autistic child: it's not about behavior. It's not about motivation. It's not about consistency. It's about whether the 'I need to go' signal is actually arriving in the brain.

Every standard method — from Oh Crap to sticker charts to timed sits — assumes that signal is already there. For many kids, it just isn't. The body sends it, but the brain doesn't catch it in time.

That's not failure. That's neurology. And neurology can change.

It's called interoception

Interoception researcher Kelly Mahler describes it as the eighth sensory system — the ability to feel what's happening inside your body. For many autistic children, this signal can be delayed or muted.

Pull-ups make it worse: they wick moisture away, which means the brain never gets the feedback it needs to connect 'I'm wet' with 'I should use the potty.'
