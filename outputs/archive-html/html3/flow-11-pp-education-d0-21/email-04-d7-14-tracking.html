Tracking mini-wins

How to notice the small signals that matter most

BrightKidCo
 

If you watch other parents talk about potty training, they describe a moment when everything clicked. For autistic children, that moment often doesn't exist. Progress is smaller, slower, and easier to miss.

Here's what parents are actually seeing in weeks 1-2:

"My son paused mid-play for the first time. He didn't make it to the bathroom. But he stopped. That's new."

"She walked toward the bathroom after the accident. That direction tells me she's starting to connect the dots."

"He pulled at his pants after wetting them. First time he's acknowledged the sensation in years."

"I found her standing by the potty after the accident. She didn't say anything — she just stood there. That's more than we had before."

 

What counts as progress

Don't measure in "how many times on the potty." Measure in "how many times they noticed." Here are the signals to watch for:

✓ Looked at the wet spot — even once, even for a second. That's the first sign of recognition.
✓ Pulled at or touched their pants — a hand on the waistband, a tug at the fabric. The body is signaling.
✓ Paused mid-accident — even a one-second pause means the signal is starting to arrive.
✓ Walked toward the bathroom — even after the accident. The direction is the signal.
✓ Tolerated underwear for 30+ minutes — the sensory adjustment is working, even without awareness yet.

What these signals mean for your child's brain

Every time your child notices — a pause, a look, a tug — a neural connection is being strengthened. The brain is learning: "That sensation means something." This is interoception in action.

Research has shown that children with autism often have a reduced ability to sense internal body signals. But those pathways can grow. A 2025 review of 49 studies confirmed that interoceptive differences are well-established in the autism community — and that body awareness can improve with the right sensory feedback.

The mini-wins you're seeing now — even if they seem too small to count — are proof that the learning loop is active. The nerve pathways are being built. Progress isn't a straight line, and it doesn't look like a finish line. It looks like a pause that wasn't there last week.
