When you're used to methods that promise results in days or weeks, the slower pace of body-signal learning can feel like nothing is happening. But something is happening. It's just happening at the level of the nervous system — which doesn't run on calendar time.

For high-support children whose interoceptive system processes signals differently, we typically see first signs of awareness somewhere between 8 and 12 weeks. Some children show it sooner. Some need longer. Both are normal.

What realistic progress looks like

Weeks 1-4: Acclimation

The child's nervous system is learning that a new sensation exists. This phase may look like nothing visible. Internally, the brain is building pathways it never had before.

Weeks 4-8: First flickers

Some children pause mid-accident. Some look down. Some tolerate the underwear longer. These are not "almost trained" — they are the nervous system registering input it previously filtered out.

Weeks 8-12+: Building awareness

The pause becomes more consistent. Some children start showing signals — pulling at the underwear, seeking a parent, showing discomfort they never showed before. Not every child reaches this stage. But many do.

These are ranges, not promises. Every child's nervous system is unique.
