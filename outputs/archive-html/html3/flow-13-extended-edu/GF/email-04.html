What progress looks like

The verbal signal

Your child says "wet," "uh-oh," or "I need to go" — not always before, but sometimes after. That's the brain connecting the feeling with the word.

The non-verbal signal

Your child pulls at their pants, looks down at the wet spot, walks toward the bathroom, or pauses mid-accident. Any of these are the body communicating.

The tolerance win

Your child wears the underwear for longer without asking for the pull-up, or without showing signs of sensory distress. That's the nervous system adjusting.

The dry stretch

Dry for 2 hours instead of 30 minutes. Or 3 instead of 1. Or waking up dry in the morning for the first time. Any stretch longer than before is progress.

The quiet build

Nothing visible yet. Your child seems the same as seven weeks ago. This is still progress — the neural pathways are being laid down. Some children show nothing for 8-12 weeks, then things start to shift.

Success isn't one thing

At BrightKidCo, we measure progress differently. Success isn't "trained in 30 days." Success is:

✓ Looking at the wet spot for the first time

✓ Pulling at their pants in awareness

✓ Walking toward the bathroom (even after the accident)

✓ Staying dry 2 hours instead of 30 minutes

✓ Pausing mid-accident for the first time

✓ Tolerating underwear for 30 minutes without distress

Any one of these counts. Not all at once. Just one.
