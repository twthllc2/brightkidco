You're about five weeks in. By now, you've probably noticed something — even if it's small. Or you might still be waiting. Both are valid. Let me help you read what you're seeing.

Every child sends signals about what they're feeling. The trick is knowing what to look for — because the signals don't always look like what the books describe.

What I hear from parents at week five

• "Sitting on the potty… nothing happens… then peeing on the floor 10 minutes later."

• "She doesn't care about peeing herself one bit. She'll just keep playing in the puddle."

• "He's starting to pause mid-accident. Just for a second. Then continues playing."

• "She looked at the wet spot on her underwear yesterday. First time she's ever noticed."

• "We've been at this for years with zero progress. Still nothing visible. But I'm trying to be patient."

What each signal means

The pause

A pause — even half a second — means the brain registered something. That's the interoception signal starting to arrive. This is the first mini-win.

The look

Looking down at the wet spot is a huge milestone. It means the child is connecting "the feeling I just had" with "this wetness." That's body-brain connection in action.

The dry stretch

Staying dry for 2 hours instead of 30 minutes means the brain is learning to hold the signal a little longer. Your child is building control without even knowing it.

Nothing visible yet

Some children show no visible change for 8-12 weeks. The neural connections are forming — they just haven't reached the surface yet. Keep going.

Permission to pause

If your child is showing resistance, fear, or distress around the underwear — it's okay to take a break. The nervous system learns best when it feels safe. A week off won't undo the progress. Sometimes it helps.

This is not training. This is learning. And learning happens in cycles, not straight lines.
