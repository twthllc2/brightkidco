Two months in — why regression isn't failure

Every Level 2 family hits regression cycles. Here's how to navigate them.

 

{first_name} — if the last email in this series hits when you're in the middle of a regression cycle, I want to say this clearly: regression is not a reset to zero.

"We had 3 weeks of success, then total regression. Every change in routine sets us back." — this is the most common pattern for Level 2 families. An illness, a vacation, a change in school schedule, a sensory overload day — and the initiative that was building disappears.

Here's what's actually happening: the body-signal connection is still there. What regresses is the behavior (going to the bathroom, pulling down pants, initiating), not the awareness (the ability to feel when an accident is happening). The nervous system doesn't unlearn the sensory feedback — it just needs a lower-demand environment to re-access it.

One parent of a 6-year-old Level 2 child put it this way: "He goes for the teacher but not for me. At school he's trained. At home he acts like he's never seen a toilet. I thought we'd lost everything. But his BCBA said: school is lower-demand. The behavior transfers when the nervous system feels safe enough."

This is a key insight for Level 2 parents. Location-specific training — going at school but not at home, or at home but not in public — is not a sign that learning failed. It's a sign that the body-signal awareness is conditional on the nervous system's safety assessment.

What helps: return to the underwear consistently after the break in routine passes. Don't push the toilet behavior. Let the sensory feedback rebuild for a few days or a week before re-introducing the toilet. The awareness layer rebuilds faster the second time — sometimes in days instead of weeks.

 

Practical regression recovery steps

When you see regression, here's what to do:

• Drop the toilet expectation for 3-7 days. Return to wearing the underwear as sensory training only. The goal is awareness, not output. The toilet behavior will re-emerge when the awareness is stable.

• Identify the trigger. Was it illness? Vacation? A new schedule? A sensory overload event? Knowing what triggered the regression helps you plan for it next time.

• Communicate with your BCBA. Share what you're seeing. Many BCBAs have regression recovery protocols — and now you have data from the body-signal layer that gives them more information than they've ever had.

• Celebrate the baseline. If your child went from zero awareness to some awareness, and is now at less awareness — that's different than never having had awareness. The neural pathway existed. It can be re-activated.

"Every illness erases what we built. At first I panicked. Now I know: it took 6 weeks the first time. It took 10 days the second time. The body remembers — it just needs a gentler re-introduction."
