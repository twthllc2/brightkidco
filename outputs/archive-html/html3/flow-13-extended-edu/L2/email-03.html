Why withholding is sensory, not defiance

The most misunderstood symptom in Level 2 potty learning — and what the research says

 

{first_name} — if your child holds bowel movements until the bedtime pull-up goes on, I want you to hear this: it's not stubbornness and it's not control.

Withholding in Level 2 children is a sensory protection mechanism. The child's nervous system has learned that releasing feels unsafe — typically because the sensation of elimination has been associated with discomfort, overstimulation, or the stress of a bathroom environment that triggers sensory overload.

One parent described it like this: "He'll sit for 30 minutes, get up, and immediately go in his pull-up. It's like his body won't release until the pull-up is on. I used to think he was being difficult. Now I realize the pull-up is what feels safe to him — the sound, the feel, the routine."

Many families dealing with withholding also manage chronic constipation or daily Miralax use. The cycle compounds: the longer the child holds, the harder the stool becomes, the more painful elimination feels, the more the child avoids it. This is not a potty training problem alone — it's a sensory-medical feedback loop that needs a different kind of input.

Your BCBA has been addressing the behavioral side. The sensory piece needs a different kind of input — and that's where the body-signal connection matters. When the underwear preserves the gentle wetness sensation, the brain slowly learns that elimination produces a predictable, non-threatening signal.

"We have GI complications — chronic withholding, daily Miralax. The bathroom flushing makes him cover his ears. After 8 weeks with the underwear, I've reduced his Miralax dose. His GI is improving." — that shift starts with the nervous system learning it can trust the signal, not fight it.

 

What you can do about withholding

A few approaches that Level 2 families have found helpful:

• Separate the sensation from the bathroom — let your child experience the wetness signal in a low-stimulus room first, not the bathroom. The body needs to learn that the signal is non-threatening before it can connect to toilet behavior.

• Track accident timing — if your child holds all day and releases only in the pull-up at night, you know the association is between pull-up comfort and release. The underwear worn during awake hours starts to change that association gently.

• Sensory-friendly bathroom adaptations — dim the light, offer noise-canceling headphones for the flush, use a softer toilet seat cover. If the bathroom environment itself is triggering the withholding reflex, address the environment.

• Share data with your BCBA — bring day-by-day tracking of when and where accidents happen, what the bathroom environment was like, and any signs of awareness your child has shown. Your BCBA can use this to adjust the existing protocol.
