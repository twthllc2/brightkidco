Three weeks in — the sensory recalibration window

Three weeks of body-signal wear. Here's what's happening beneath the surface.

 

{first_name} — three weeks in. You've been at this consistently — underwear on, pull-up over it for outings, letting your child feel the sensation when it happens.

If nothing dramatic has happened, that's the norm for Level 2 kids. The body-signal connection doesn't switch on like a light. It builds — neuron by neuron — and the first three weeks are almost entirely invisible.

The piece that many families don't realize is that your BCBA has been building the behavioral structure for years. Scheduled sits, data sheets, prompt fading — that's all the external framework. The Body-Signal Learning Layer adds what that framework can't provide: an internal feedback loop the child's brain can start registering.

One mom described her son this way: "He'd sit for 20 minutes on the toilet with the BCBA protocol. Nothing. Then he'd get up, go to his room, and pee in the corner 5 minutes later. It wasn't defiance — he genuinely didn't feel it coming."

What you may be seeing right now is phase one of sensory recalibration. Your child may pause more often. May touch the front of the underwear. May seem more aware of wetness after the fact. These are not accidents — they are the nervous system starting to register input it was filtering out.

Interoception researcher Kelly Mahler calls the body's ability to sense internal cues the eighth sensory system. For many autistic children, that system needs a new kind of input to wake up. The underwear's wetness-preservation layer creates milliseconds of sustained sensory feedback that the brain can start to recognize. Some families see shifting at week 4. Others at week 8. Both are normal.

 

What mini-wins look like at this stage

The goal at three weeks is not dry underwear. The goal is awareness. Here's what to watch for:

• Pausing mid-activity after an accident — that's the brain registering the sensation

• Pulling at the front of the underwear — this is early body-awareness behavior

• Looking down at the wet spot — the visual confirmation loop starting

• Tolerating the underwear longer each day without distress

"She looked down. At the wet. First time. I cried." — that moment of recognition is the milestone, not dry pants.

Regression cycles are normal. Some kids show three days of progress and then a day of nothing. Others show nothing for weeks and then a jump. The body-signal connection doesn't build in a straight line. If your child has had even one moment of noticing — that's the foundation.
