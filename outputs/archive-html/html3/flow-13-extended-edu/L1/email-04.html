How verbal shifts signal growing interoceptive awareness

Hi {first_name},

You're nearly 7 weeks into this approach. At this point, I want to talk about something that's often overlooked: your child's language around potty.

For verbal Level 1 children, the way they talk about using the toilet changes before the accidents stop. The words they use — or don't use — are one of the earliest signals that the interoceptive connection is forming.

A mom whose daughter is 4, verbal, Level 1 described it perfectly: "She knows everything about potty — she could explain it, narrate it, talk about it for hours. But when it came to actually going, she'd say 'not now' and pee 10 minutes later."

That's not defiance. That's a child who understands the concept intellectually but can't feel the real-time signal. The change happens when the language shifts from abstract ("I know about potty") to embodied ("I need to go right now").

Three verbal shifts that signal progress

1. From "after" to "during"

Early on, children say "I peed" or "I'm wet" after the accident. Over time, as the signal strengthens, you may hear them say it during — "I'm peeing!" — which means they're feeling it happen in real time.

2. From "no" to "wait"

When you ask "Do you need to go?" and they say "no" but then have an accident 10 minutes later — that's the signal arriving too late. A shift to "wait" or "not right now" or "in a minute" means they feel something but can't yet time it.

3. Body language before words

Many L1 children show physical signals before they have the words — pulling at pants, crossing legs, shifting weight, pausing mid-play. These non-verbal cues are often the earliest sign that the brain is registering the sensation.

If you notice any of these shifts — even if accidents are still frequent — that's real progress. The language centers of the brain are connecting with the sensory signals. Words follow awareness.

What to do with this information

When you notice a verbal shift, your response matters. The goal is to reinforce the connection without creating pressure.

✓ "You felt that! Good for you for noticing."

✓ "Your body just told you something. Let's listen to it."

✓ "That pause you did — that's your body practicing. Good job."

Avoid "Why didn't you say something?" or "You just went!" — those create shame around the very awareness you're trying to build. Celebrate the recognition, even when the action doesn't follow yet.

What progress sounds like

“

"My son is 4, verbal, diagnosed Level 1. He talks about everything. Knows all his letters, can read basic words. But potty training was a disaster. He'd talk ABOUT going. But that had never happened before."

That's the shift. From talking about potty as a concept to feeling it as a real-time body experience. When you hear that change, you'll know the interoceptive connection is building.

Start listening for the small verbal shifts. They're often the first sign that something is clicking.
