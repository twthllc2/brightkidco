What first signals look like and why "nothing yet" can still be progress

Hi {first_name},

You're about a month in. I want to talk about timelines — not the kind that create pressure, but the kind that help you know what's normal and what's worth celebrating.

For Level 1 children — verbal, aware, able to sit on the toilet — first signals typically appear somewhere between 2 and 6 weeks of consistent Body-Signal Learning Layer use. That's a range on purpose. Some children show a clear shift at week 2. Others need until week 6 or a little longer. Both are within the range of what we see.

If you're at week 4 or 5 and thinking, "I don't see anything yet" — that's not a sign of failure. The signal doesn't always announce itself dramatically. Sometimes it's barely visible: a longer pause before the accident. A glance downward. A hand moving toward the waistband.

One parent described it this way: "He looked down. At the wet. First time. I cried." That moment — that tiny shift from "it happened" to "I noticed it happened" — is the beginning of the interoceptive feedback loop.

Another mom shared: "By week 6, she was pulling at her pants right before an accident. That had never happened. In pull-ups she'd just... go. No awareness at all."

If you're not there yet, that's okay. The underwear is doing its job every time it creates a moment of awareness — even if that moment comes after the fact. Each "uh-oh" is a data point the brain is starting to register.

Why 2 to 6 weeks makes sense

The Body-Signal Learning Layer works by giving the brain something pull-ups block: a sustained wetness signal that lasts 30-60 seconds. This signal is the feedback the brain needs to connect "I feel something" with "I need to do something about it."

For a child who has been in pull-ups for years, the brain has learned to ignore the absence of sensation. It takes time — weeks, not days — for the brain to start paying attention to a signal it hasn't had before. Research has shown that children with autism often have a reduced ability to sense internal body signals (Nicholson et al., 2019). That's not stubbornness. That's neurology.

First mini-wins timeline for L1:

Weeks 2-3 Pausing after an accident
Weeks 3-5 Pulling at pants before/after
Weeks 4-6 Saying "wet" or "uh-oh" after
Weeks 6-8 Occasional "I need pee" before

What if Pre-K is only 8 weeks away?

I know the calendar is real. Pre-K enrollment doesn't wait for anyone. If September is 8 weeks out and you're wondering whether there's enough time — the answer is: it depends on what "ready" means for your child and your school.

Many Pre-K programs accept children who are not fully trained, especially when parents communicate the plan and show progress. A child who is having occasional accidents but can communicate awareness is in a very different position from a child with no awareness at all — and many schools recognize that difference.

"Pre-K accepted him in underwear with weeks to spare. He still has 1-2 accidents a day at home when he's distracted. But Pre-K accepted him. That was the goal."

The goal isn't perfection. The goal is progress. And 8 weeks is a meaningful amount of time for building the interoceptive connection.

You're in the window where signals start. Keep going. Every day of consistent use is a day the nervous system is learning.
