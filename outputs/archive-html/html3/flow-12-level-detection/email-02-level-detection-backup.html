Potty training looks different for every autistic child. What works for one family may not work for another — and that's not because you're doing something wrong. It's because the underlying reason is different for each child.

The Body-Signal Learning Layer works the same way for everyone: by preserving the wetness signal so the brain can learn to recognize it. But which signals to watch for, and how progress shows up, depends on your child's specific experience.

Below are four descriptions. Read each one and see which fits your child best. (More than one can apply — that's normal too.) The descriptions help us send you the right information for your child's specific needs.

What they all have in common: the “I need to go” signal isn't reaching your child's brain in time. The body sends it — but for some children, that connection is delayed. For others, it's muted. For some, it hasn't formed at all.

That's not about your child's ability. It's not about your parenting. It's neurology. And the good news: that connection can be built.
