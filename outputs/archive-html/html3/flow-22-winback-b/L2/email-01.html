None of that was wasted. Your BCBA built the behavioral foundation. But there's a piece that methods driven by prompts and rewards can't address: the internal body signal. Interoception researcher Kelly Mahler calls it the eighth sensory system — the ability to feel what's happening inside. For many autistic children, that signal doesn't arrive reliably.

 

Why the signal matters more than the prompt

“He can go to the bathroom with on the dot prompting every hour but he does not care if he goes on himself and doesn't feel uncomfortable,” one mom told us. “He's 6. We've been at this for 2 years.”

This is prompt-dependency — and it's not a training failure. The child can follow the routine but can't feel the underlying signal. The Body-Signal Learning Layer™ is designed to do one thing: preserve a gentle wetness sensation long enough for the brain to register it. Where pull-ups wick the signal away in seconds, our underwear keeps it present so the nervous system can build the connection.

According to the SPARK study by Wiggins and colleagues, nearly half of autistic 4–5 year olds are not yet toilet trained. Not because their parents didn't try — because the methods assumed a signal that wasn't arriving.
