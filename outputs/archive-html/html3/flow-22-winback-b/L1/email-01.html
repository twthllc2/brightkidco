He gets it — but he doesn't get it

The one piece most potty training methods miss — and why Pre-K could still work

 

I'm Lena. We haven't talked in a while — and I respect your inbox, so I won't make this long. But if your child is still struggling with potty training, there's one thing I really want you to know before Pre-K enrollment season closes.

Your child knows what potty training is. Talks about it. Understands the steps. Can probably explain them to you. Has good days where everything clicks and bad days where nothing works. You've tried sticker charts, maybe the Oh Crap method, timed sits — and some of it worked, temporarily. But reliably? Not yet.

That's not a parenting failure. And it's not a stubborn child. It's a signal-timing gap. The internal "I need to go" feeling often doesn't arrive in time for autistic children. Research has shown that kids with autism often have a reduced ability to sense internal body signals — meaning they feel the need only after it's already happening, or not at all.

This is the pattern I hear most from parents with kids like yours: almost trained, but not quite. A few great days, then a total regression. Perfect at daycare, accidents at home. And Pre-K enrollment is right there on the calendar, making every accident feel heavier than it should.

"Pre-K starts in 3 months and I'm panicking."

— From a BrightKidCo parent survey

The Body-Signal Learning Layer doesn't teach your child to use the toilet. It teaches their brain to recognize the feeling that comes before. That gentle wetness signal that pull-ups erase is exactly what an autistic brain needs to build the feedback loop between "I feel something" and "I should get to the potty." Some kids show first mini-wins in 2 weeks. Others take 8 weeks. Both are normal.

 

Why "almost trained" isn't your fault

Your child can sit on the potty. Has done it before. Some days they go four times without a single accident. Then the next day they refuse entirely. That's not defiance — it's sensory regulation fluctuating. Some days the signal arrives clearly. Other days it doesn't.

According to the SPARK study by Wiggins and colleagues, nearly half of autistic 4-5 year olds are not yet toilet trained — not because their parents didn't try, but because most methods assume a body signal that isn't arriving.

The good news: building that signal connection takes time, but it's real. Some families see first signs within weeks. And the window before Pre-K is still open — many parents who start in spring see meaningful progress before fall enrollment.
