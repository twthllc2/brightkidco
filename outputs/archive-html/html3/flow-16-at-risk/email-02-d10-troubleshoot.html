If it didn't click right away — that's normal.

Most parents tell me the first few days are confusing. The child doesn't react. The underwear gets changed five times. Nothing seems to be happening. That's because the connection takes time to form.

Here's what's actually happening: your child's body has been sending the “I need to go” signal, but the brain hasn't been catching it. The Body-Signal Learning Layer keeps that signal present. The brain needs consistent exposure to the wetness sensation before it starts to register “this means something.”

Think of it like learning any new sensory input. Your child might show awareness as:

• A pause mid-activity — even one second

• Pulling at their pants or looking down after an accident

• A longer dry stretch — 2 hours instead of 30 minutes

• Tolerating the underwear for longer without asking for a pull-up

None of these mean “fully trained.” They mean the signal is starting to arrive. And that is the first real step.

If your child is 3 or 10, verbal or non-verbal — the process is the same. The signal needs to be felt before it can be acted on. Some children show first signals in two weeks. Others need two months. Both are real progress.

Pay attention to both verbal cues (words like 'wet' or 'uh-oh') and non-verbal ones (pulling at pants, a sudden pause, looking toward you). Both are valid ways of saying 'I felt something.' No pressure to have it figured out right now. The underwear will be here when you're ready.

The Mechanism

Traditional potty training methods assume the “I need to go” signal arrives reliably in the brain. For many autistic children, it doesn't. The signal is there — the body sends it — but the brain doesn't register it in time. That's not defiance. That's neurology.
