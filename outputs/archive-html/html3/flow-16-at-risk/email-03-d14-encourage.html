Small steps
still count

Here's something I wish someone had told me earlier.

Progress in body-signal learning doesn't look like potty training progress. It's not a straight line from pull-ups to independence. It's smaller. And it matters just as much.

You might be watching for the “big moment” — the first time they use the potty on their own. That can take a while. But the things that happen before that moment are where the real work happens.

“She looks down at the wet spot for the first time.”

“He pauses mid-play for a second before continuing.”

“She tugs at her pants or looks toward you after an accident.”

“He tolerates wearing the underwear for 30 minutes without asking for a pull-up.”

Any one of those is a win. Not “fully trained.” A win. Because each one means the nervous system is starting to register the signal.

Don't measure in “how many times on the potty.” Measure in “how many times they noticed.”

Some children show these first signals in two weeks. Others need two months or more. Both are real. For kids aged 3 to 10+, the same sensory mechanism is at work.

And if your child gives a signal — a word, a gesture, a pause — notice it. Celebrate it quietly. That's the beginning. If today isn't the day for active work, that's okay too. The laundry will be here when you're ready.
