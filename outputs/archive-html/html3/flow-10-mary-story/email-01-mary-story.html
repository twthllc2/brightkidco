A story about watching, learning, and finding a different way

Before BrightKidCo, I spent years working with autistic children and their families. What I saw changed how I think about potty training — and eventually led me to build something that didn't exist before.

I watched parents try everything. Sticker charts, timed sits, reward systems, the naked method, social stories — method after method — and nothing moved the needle on awareness. Their children would sit on the potty for 20 minutes with nothing happening, then pee two seconds after standing up. Or they wouldn't react at all when accidents happened, playing right through a wet diaper. Or they'd been in pull-ups for years without any change in how they registered the sensation.

And every one of those parents told me the same thing in different words: “I have tried everything, reward systems and punishments and nothing seems to work.”

That sentence stopped me cold. Because it wasn't true that nothing worked — it was that nothing they'd been given was designed for the right problem. Every method they tried assumed the “I need to go” signal was arriving in the brain loud and clear. For their children, it wasn't.

That was the moment I knew we needed a different approach — one built from the body up, not the behavior down. Not more charts or rewards or prompts. A way to help the nervous system actually feel the signal so the learning could start.

“

We've been working on potty training for 8 months now with absolutely zero progress

— Parent of an autistic child

What I learned about the missing piece

The “I need to go” signal is a body-brain connection. For many autistic children, that signal is delayed or muted. The body sends it, but the brain doesn't catch it in time. That's not defiance — it's neurology. And no amount of sticker charts or timed sits can build a connection the brain hasn't learned to recognize yet.

Here's what I realized: if we could create a signal the brain could feel — a gentle, persistent wetness cue that lasts long enough for the nervous system to register it — we could build that connection from scratch. Not through prompting, not through rewards. Through the body's own learning mechanism.

That's what the Body-Signal Learning Layer does. It preserves the wetness signal that pull-ups wick away. That 30-to-60-second window is how the brain finally gets the chance to connect “I feel something” with “I should use the potty.” It's the feedback loop that nothing else provides.

I feel like a failure because he's still in pull ups

— Parent of an autistic child
