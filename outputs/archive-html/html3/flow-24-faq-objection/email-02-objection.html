Let's talk timelines — honestly, without the hype.

BrightKidCo

You've been told "most kids get it in a weekend." Your child didn't. And now you're wondering — how long is this going to take?

The honest answer: it depends on your child's nervous system — not on a calendar. Some children show first signals in 2 weeks. Others need 8 weeks. Some need 6 months. And here's the thing: all of those count as real progress.

The reason most methods fail on a timeline is straightforward: they assume the "I need to go" signal is already arriving in the brain. If the signal isn't getting through, no amount of timed sits or sticker charts will create it faster. The signal has to form first.

Why there's no single answer

The "I need to go" signal is a body-brain connection. For many autistic children, that signal is delayed or muted. The Body-Signal Learning Layer doesn't train your child — it creates the conditions for that connection to form. And like any neurological connection, it forms on its own schedule.

Two weeks for some. Eight weeks for others. Both are within normal range. The nervous system learns on its own schedule — weeks for some kids, months for others.

Here's what progress actually looks like: a longer dry stretch. A pause before the accident. A look down. Those count. Those are the beginning. Don't measure in "how many times on the potty." Measure in "how many times they noticed."
