What to tell your pediatrician

Most pediatricians aren't trained in autism-specific potty training. They give the tools they have — and those tools were designed for neurotypical children. That doesn't mean your doctor was wrong. It means the tools weren't designed for your child's neurology.

The research your doctor will care about:

✓ A 2025 review of 49 studies confirmed interoceptive differences are well-established in the autism community. (Barmpagiannis et al., 2025)

✓ Nearly half of autistic 4-5 year olds are not yet toilet trained. (SPARK study, Wiggins et al.)

✓ The type of undergarment a child wears directly affects potty training outcomes. (Simon & Thompson, 2006)

✓ An 8-week study by interoception researcher Kelly Mahler showed body awareness can significantly improve with the right sensory feedback. (Hample, Mahler & Amspacher, 2020)

Readiness isn't a switch — it's a bridge

"Readiness" in traditional potty training means a child can feel the signal and act on it. But what if your child can't feel the signal at all? Waiting doesn't help — the signal won't appear on its own if the nervous system can't perceive it.

The Body-Signal Learning Layer doesn't wait for readiness. It creates the conditions for it. Readiness isn't about waiting — it's about the right neurological conditions.

Some kids show first signals after 2 weeks. Others need 8 weeks. Both are normal progress. Readiness builds with the right neurological conditions.
