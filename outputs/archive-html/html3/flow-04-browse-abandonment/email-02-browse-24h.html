The hidden sense — interoception

 

If your child has accidents and doesn't seem to notice — doesn't pause, doesn't look down, doesn't react — you're not alone in that.

I hear this from parents every single day, in different ways:

"My son sits on the potty for 20 minutes and nothing happens. The second the pull-up goes on — pee."

"My daughter doesn't notice when she's wet. She'll play in a wet diaper all day."

"We've been trying for 2+ years with zero progress. Pull-ups. Timed sits. Rewards. Nothing changes the awareness."

"He's almost trained — can hold it, uses the potty sometimes — but still has daily accidents."

 

Interoception: the 8th sense

Interoception is the ability to feel what's happening inside your body — hunger, thirst, your heartbeat, and yes, the need to use the bathroom. Interoception researcher Kelly Mahler describes it as the eighth sensory system. For many autistic children, this signal can be delayed or muted.

It's not that your child won't feel it — it's that their brain isn't receiving the message in time. Research has shown that children with autism often have a reduced ability to sense internal body signals. The training methods you tried assumed the signal was there. It wasn't your fault.

This is where sensory readiness comes in. The brain needs to be in a calm, receptive state to register new sensations. When the nervous system is overwhelmed, that connection doesn't form. The Body-Signal Learning Layer is designed to work with that nervous system — not against it — by keeping the wetness signal present just long enough for the brain to catch it.

What progress really looks like

Some children show first signals within weeks. Others take months. Both are normal progress. The nervous system learns on its own schedule — and that's okay.

Success isn't "fully trained." Success is:

  • Looking down at the wet spot for the first time
  • Pulling at their pants in awareness — whether they say a word or use a gesture
  • A longer dry stretch, even 2 hours instead of 30 minutes
  • Pausing mid-accident for the very first time

As one parent said: "He looked down. At the wet. First time. I cried." That moment — that tiny pause — is where the connection begins. Don't measure in "how many times on the potty." Measure in "how many times they noticed."
