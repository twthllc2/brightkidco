The Science

Why more pairs matter

The Body-Signal Learning Layer works through sustained exposure. Every time your child wears the underwear, the gentle wetness signal sits at the edge of their awareness, waiting for their brain to register it.

One pair means limited exposure. Your child wears it, then goes back to a pull-up or diaper — and the signal stops. Six pairs means consistent signal time, day after day. It means the connection has more opportunities to form.

Research has shown that interoceptive differences are common in the autism community (Nicholson et al., 2019). The signal is there — the body sends it. But the brain doesn't always catch it in time. Consistent, gentle feedback during the day is how the connection builds.

Simon & Thompson (2006) showed that the type of undergarment a child wears directly affects potty training outcomes. Azrin & Foxx (1971) established that sensory awareness of accidents is essential to the learning loop. The mechanism is grounded in decades of research — it's about signal, not willpower.
