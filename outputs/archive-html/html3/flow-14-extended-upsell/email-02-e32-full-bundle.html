The Science

Consistency builds the connection

The 'I need to go' signal is a body-brain connection. For many autistic children, that signal is delayed or muted. It's not that your child won't feel it — it's that their brain isn't receiving the message in time.

Traditional training methods work because they build on an existing signal. If the signal isn't there, no amount of sticker charts or timed sits will create it. The signal has to come first.

Pull-ups are designed to wick moisture away — great for sleep, but for learning, they block the wetness signal before the brain can register it. The Body-Signal Learning Layer keeps that signal present for 30–60 seconds, giving the brain time to connect "I peed" with "I should use the potty."

According to the SPARK study by Wiggins and colleagues, nearly half of autistic 4–5 year olds are not yet toilet trained. It's not defiance — it's neurology. And the good news: that connection can be built, with the right consistent sensory feedback.
