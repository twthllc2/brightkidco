The Mechanism

Interoception researcher Kelly Mahler describes it as the eighth sensory system — the ability to feel what's happening inside your body. Hunger. Thirst. And yes, the need to use the bathroom. For autistic children, this signal can arrive late or not at all.

A 2025 review of 49 studies confirmed interoceptive differences are well-established in the autism community (Barmpagiannis et al., 2025). The body sends the signal. But for some children, the brain doesn't catch it in time. That's not defiance. That's neurology.

The Body-Signal Learning Layer works by keeping that signal present — not absorbed, not wicked away — so the brain has time to make the connection. An 8-week study by interoception researcher Kelly Mahler showed body awareness can significantly improve with the right sensory feedback (Hample, Mahler & Amspacher, 2020).

What does progress look like? It might be a word — "wet," "uh-oh," or a new sound. It might be a gesture — pulling at their pants, pausing, or looking down at the wet spot. Both are the same thing: the brain starting to register what the body feels.
