Here's what connects all of them

The “I need to go” signal is a body-brain connection. For many autistic children, that signal is delayed or muted. It's not that your child won't feel it — their brain isn't receiving the message in time.

Traditional methods like sticker charts and timed sits work by building on an existing signal. If the signal isn't arriving, no amount of prompting can create it. The signal has to come first.

Look for any signal — a pause, pulling at their pants, looking down, saying “wet,” or showing you with a gesture. Whether your child communicates verbally or through body language, the signal is there.
