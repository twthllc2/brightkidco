Still having
accidents?

Here's what's actually happening — and what to try next

You're about six weeks in. And maybe you're thinking: "We're still having accidents. Is this working?"

That's the most common question I hear at this stage. The answer might surprise you: accidents don't mean it's not working. In fact, for many children, accidents are how the learning happens. The signal doesn't arrive perfectly the first time. It arrives in fragments. A pause here. A look there. A longer stretch between accidents.

Let me break down what's probably happening — and what you can do about it.

What parents tell me at week 6

You're not alone in this

Here's what I hear from other parents at this exact point in the journey:

"She still has accidents every day. But she pauses now. She never used to pause."

"He's inconsistent — two good days, then three bad ones. I can't figure out the pattern."

"We see zero change. She doesn't react to the wetness. Nothing."

"He walks toward the bathroom after the accident — but he still doesn't make it. Is that progress or not?"

Sound familiar? Every single one of these is normal. And every single one points toward the same truth: the signal is starting to get through, even if it's not landing every time.

Why it's inconsistent — and why that's actually good

Inconsistency is not a sign of failure. It's a sign that the interoception pathway is being built. Think of it like learning any new sensation — the first time you feel something, it's faint and unreliable. Over repetitions, it becomes stronger and more predictable.

The Body-Signal Learning Layer works because it keeps the wetness signal present long enough for the brain to register it. But the first 30-60 connections are going to be weak. The brain doesn't know what to do with this new information yet.

Some children show first signals within weeks. Others need months. Both are within normal range. The most important thing you can do right now is trust the process — the signal is being built whether you can see it or not.

Practical troubleshooting

Three things to try this week

1

Keep the underwear on consistently

The most common reason progress stalls is inconsistent wear. The signal needs repetition. Aim for a few hours every day during awake time. If the child resists, start with 30 minutes and build up. Consistency matters more than duration.

2

Watch for the signal, not the destination

Don't measure by "did we make it to the potty?" Measure by "did they pause? Did they look down? Did they touch their pants?" Every signal, no matter how small, counts as progress. The potty comes later.

3

If you see zero change — wait and watch

Some children need 8-12 weeks before any visible signal appears. If nothing has changed yet, that's not failure. Some nervous systems take longer to build the connection. You still have time — the 60-day guarantee means you decide what works.
