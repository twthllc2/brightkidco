You didn't fail

Why she goes for the teacher, not for you

Prompt-dependency isn't training. And it's not your fault.

 

You tried Oh Crap. I tried it too. Both my sons went through it, and neither one came out trained. You tried sticker charts — my kids couldn't feel the signal sticker charts depend on. You tried timed sits — same problem. Your child isn't failing at potty training. Your methods were solving the wrong problem.

Think about everything you've tried. ABA protocols with scheduled sits every 90 minutes. OT sensory strategies. Social stories with Velcro images. Picture communication cards. The naked method. Four different potty seats because of sensory issues. A weighted blanket before sitting. Reward charts tied to special interests. The BCBA data sheets tracking every attempt. Your BCBA did everything right — for a child whose signal arrived. So did you.

The reason she goes for the teacher but not for you? That's prompt-dependency. She can follow the routine at school because the structure carries her. But the internal signal — the "I need to go" feeling — never connects. At home, when the prompts drop away, the system breaks. That's not a parenting problem. That's a neurological gap that no amount of sticker charts or scheduled sits can fill.

One parent described it this way: "He'd sit. He wouldn't go. Then he'd wait until I changed him into a pull-up, and within 5 minutes, he'd pee. Classic prompt-dependency, his BCBA confirmed." Another parent said: "He has the words but can't connect them to internal sensations. He echoes 'do you need to pee?' instead of answering." This is the essence of the interoception gap — verbal doesn't mean independent.

 

You're not alone in this

The SPARK study found that nearly half of autistic 4-5 year olds are not toilet trained (Wiggins et al.). Half. Even with ABA, OT, speech, and parents who have dedicated years to the goal. This isn't a reflection on your effort, your consistency, or your parenting.

A 2025 review of 49 studies by Barmpagiannis and colleagues confirmed that interoceptive differences are well-established in the autism community. The research is clear: many autistic children process internal body signals differently. The methods you tried — every single one — assumed the "I need to go" signal was arriving reliably. For your child, it likely wasn't.

That's not failure. That's method mismatch. And the good news is: method mismatch can be corrected once you know what the actual problem is.
