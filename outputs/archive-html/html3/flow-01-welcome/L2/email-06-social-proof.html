Real stories from families who were where you are.

 

I could tell you how our underwear works. But I'd rather share what one mom told us — because her story might sound familiar.

She wrote to us after about 8 weeks. Her son is 6, mixed verbal, in ABA since age 3 — 20 hours a week. Sensory processing differences are his major challenge. She had tried everything: BCBA protocols, OT sensory work, social stories, sticker charts, scheduled sits, reward systems, the Oh Crap method, picture cards, a weighted blanket before sitting. She told us she'd stopped counting the methods.

Here's her story in her own words:

"Ethan is 6. Sensory processing differences are his major challenge. His BCBA, his OT, his speech therapist — they've all worked on toileting. He's been on a 90-minute scheduled sit protocol for 18 months. He'd sit. He wouldn't go. Then he'd wait until I changed him into a pull-up, and within 5 minutes, he'd pee. Classic prompt-dependency, his BCBA confirmed.

We have GI complications — chronic withholding, daily Miralax. The bathroom flushing makes him cover his ears.

I bought the 3+3 bundle in October — kind of as a 'why not' purchase. I was skeptical. I expected it to fail. Week 1: more accidents. We had a meltdown about wet underwear. Hard. Week 4: he paused. He felt the wetness. He looked at me. First time in 6 years. Week 8: he initiates maybe 30% of the time at home. School is still BCBA-prompted, but home is different. I've reduced his Miralax dose. His GI is improving."

She ended with: "Still not 'trained' in the way other kids are. But for the first time in years, his body is sending him a signal his brain can register. That's what was missing."

That pause he made at week 4 — looking down at the wet underwear — is the first step of body-signal learning. It's not "trained." It's something more fundamental: the connection between the body and the brain has started to form. The rest builds from there.
