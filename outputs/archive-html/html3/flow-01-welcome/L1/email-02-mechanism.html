It's not defiance. It's a signal-timing gap

The real reason sticker charts, timer sits, and bootcamps all failed

BrightKidCo
 

Lena here again.

Both my sons went through an Oh Crap attempt. It stressed everyone out, and neither one came out trained. It took me years to understand why.

Here's what I learned: traditional potty training methods assume the "I need to go" signal arrives reliably in the brain. For many autistic children — especially Level 1 kids who are verbal and "almost trained" — it doesn't arrive in time.

"He sits on the potty for 20 minutes, nothing happens, then pees the second the pull-up goes back on."

That's the signal-timing gap. His body sends the signal — but his brain doesn't catch it in time. It's not stubbornness. It's not you doing it wrong. It's neurology.

Research confirms that children with autism have a reduced ability to sense internal body signals (Nicholson et al., 2019). The signal is there — the body sends it — but the brain doesn't register it soon enough.

That's why sticker charts failed. That's why timer sits didn't work. They all assumed a signal that wasn't arriving. Your child wasn't being difficult — his nervous system was working differently.
