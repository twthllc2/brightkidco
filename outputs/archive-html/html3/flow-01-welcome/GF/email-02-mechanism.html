Wherever you are — just starting or years in — if your kid isn't progressing like methods promise, here's what most parents miss.

Whether your kid is 3 or 10, verbal or not — the nervous system piece is the same. And it changes everything about how you approach this.

The missing piece

It's not behavior.
It's signal-timing.

 

The body signal your child may not feel

There is a sense most people don't know they have. It's called interoception — the body's ability to feel what's happening inside. The sensation of a full bladder. The recognition that it's time to go. Interoception researcher Kelly Mahler calls it the eighth sensory system.

For many children with nervous system differences, that internal signal doesn't arrive the way traditional potty training methods depend on. The brain and the bladder aren't connected yet. Not because of anything the child is doing wrong — or anything you've missed. The wiring needs time and the right conditions to develop.

Sticker charts, timer sits, scheduled prompting — every standard method assumes a signal that may not be reaching your child. That's not a failure of your effort. It's a method mismatch with how their nervous system processes information.

The problem was never your child's willingness. It was never your consistency. It was a signal that hadn't arrived yet.

What most methods assume vs. what's actually happening

Every method you've likely tried is built on the same hidden assumption: that your child feels the "I need to go" signal and chooses not to respond. That's why sticker charts work for neurotypical kids — they need motivation, not awareness.

But if the signal isn't arriving, motivation doesn't help. You can't reward a child for acting on a message their brain never received. That's not stubbornness. That's a nervous system difference.

This is why it's not about timing your child's toilet sits. It's not about finding the right reward. It's about helping the nervous system learn to detect the signal in the first place.

Some families see a shift in 2 to 4 weeks. Others need 8 to 12. Some kids show awareness gradually — a pause, a glance down, a hand to the waistband. Others take longer. All of it counts. The nervous system learns at its own pace.
