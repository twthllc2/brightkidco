What we covered together

Four pieces that most methods miss

The signal-timing gap

Most potty training methods assume a child can feel the signal from their body saying "I need to go." For children whose nervous systems process signals differently, that signal does not arrive the way standard approaches depend on. The problem is not behavior or motivation. It is interoception — the brain's ability to sense internal body states.

The pull-up problem

Standard pull-ups are designed to feel dry even after use. For a child who already struggles to register body signals, a pull-up that wicks moisture away removes the very feedback the nervous system needs to learn. The child never feels the consequence, so the brain never connects the sensation with the action.

The body-signal mechanism

The Body-Signal Learning Layer works with the nervous system instead of against it. A gentle wetness cue reaches the skin at the moment of release, creating a feedback loop that helps the brain connect the internal signal with the external result. It does not train the child. It supports the body in learning to register what it is already doing.
