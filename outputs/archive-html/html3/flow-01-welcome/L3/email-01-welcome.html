What nobody told you

You have tried everything. ABA therapy. OT with deep pressure and sensory integration. Picture schedules, Velcro boards, social stories. Specialty toilet seats with side bars. Music therapy. A private potty consultant. Maybe even a residential program.

Here is the truth most methods never address: traditional potty training assumes the "I need to go" signal arrives reliably in the brain. For many autistic children, it doesn't. The signal is there — the body sends it — but the brain does not register it in time. That is not defiance. That is not a failure to learn. That is neurology.

Research by Nicholson et al. (2019) confirms that children with autism have a reduced ability to sense internal body signals. That is the interoception gap — the body-brain connection for potty signals has not yet formed. Sticker charts, scheduled sits, rewards — they all depend on a signal that may not be reaching your child. That is not your fault. It is a method mismatch with your child's nervous system.

 

I want to be honest with you about what this looks like in practice. Some kids show their first body-signal awareness after 4 weeks. Others need 8 to 12 weeks. Some need longer. Both outcomes are normal. We are not promising training. We are offering a missing piece — a way to help your child's nervous system register what it has not registered before.
