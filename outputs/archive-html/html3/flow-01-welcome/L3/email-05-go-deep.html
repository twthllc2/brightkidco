The Body-Signal Learning Layer is not a solution for these battles. It is one small piece that addresses a specific gap: the body-brain signal connection. It is designed to work alongside specialty diapers, alongside your current routine, at whatever pace works for your family. Not as a replacement.

 

The thing pull-ups erase

Pull-ups are designed to keep your child dry. For sleep and daily management, that is essential. But for learning? The wetness signal is the only way the brain can connect "I peed" with "I should use the potty." Pull-ups wick that signal away before the brain can register it.

Simon & Thompson (2006) showed that the type of undergarment a child wears directly affects toilet training outcomes. For a child whose interoception is already delayed, a product that removes the only available feedback signal makes learning harder — not because anything is wrong with the child, but because the learning loop is broken.

Our underwear does the opposite. The Body-Signal Learning Layer intentionally preserves a gentle wetness signal — the "uh-oh" feeling — for a brief window. That window gives the brain time to register: "Something happened. This is what wet feels like." Over time, with repetition, that connection starts to form.

 

To be clear: we are not asking you to replace pull-ups or specialty diapers. Those are essential for your family's daily reality. We are asking you to consider adding our underwear alongside them — for short periods, at a pace that works, when your child can tolerate it.

Some families report first signals after 4 weeks. Others after 8 to 12 weeks. Some see no change. All of those outcomes are real, and none of them mean you did anything wrong.

The 60-day guarantee exists so you can try without risk. If your child cannot tolerate the underwear, or if it simply does not work for your family, send it back. No forms, no questions.
