There is a story that matters for families like yours. A mom in the autism community shared that her son — non-verbal, high support needs, 9.5 years old — finally showed his first sign of body awareness after years of nothing. Not trained. Just aware. And for her, after years of giving up, retrying, and giving up again, that was everything.

Maureen Bennie's son was 9.5 when something in his body finally registered what it never had before. She had stopped actively training years earlier. She was focused on dignity and daily care. And then one day, his nervous system connected something that had been missing.

I share this not as a promise that it will happen for your child. Every child's nervous system is different. But I share it because stories like this are real. They happen. And they remind us that the body's signals can arrive at any age.

 

Stories from families like yours

"I had stopped trying. He was 8. We were focused on keeping him clean and calm. Then we started using the underwear during his calm afternoon window — maybe 45 minutes a day. After three weeks, he paused mid-accident. Just paused. Looked down. It was the first time in his life he had shown any awareness. I cried. And I am not someone who cries anymore."

— parent of an 8-year-old, non-verbal, high support needs

"We put the underwear on after his bath, just for the 30 minutes before bed. No pressure. No 'let's sit on the potty.' Just wearing it. After about 6 weeks, he started pulling at the front when he was wet. He had never done that before. It was small — anyone else would have missed it. But I saw it. That was the first time he communicated anything about body awareness. He still had accidents. He still didn't use the potty. But he noticed. And that was more than we had in 9 years."

— parent of a 9-year-old, Level 3, AAC user

"She wears the underwear during her preferred activities — swinging, watching videos. We started at 15 minutes. Now she tolerates it for about 2 hours before she gets uncomfortable. That is not success in the way most people define it. But two years ago she would tear any underwear off within seconds. So yes, that is progress. And it happened because we went at her pace, not at anyone else's schedule."

— parent of a 10-year-old, minimal verbal, high support needs

 

Every family's story is different. Some see a change in 4 weeks. Some in 12. Some see no change at all, and that is also real. But if even one of these stories felt familiar — if the idea of "he paused" or "she pulled at her pants" sounds like a milestone you would celebrate — that is the only permission you need to keep reading.

In a survey of our customers, 92% reported clearer awareness of when their child needed to go. That is not a guarantee — every child is different — but it tells us that for many families, the mechanism works.
