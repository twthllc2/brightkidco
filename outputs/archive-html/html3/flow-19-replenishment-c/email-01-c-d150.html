Five months is a long time to stay with something.

If you're reading this, you've been on this path for a while. Maybe you've seen progress you're proud of. Maybe it's been slower than you hoped. Maybe you've taken breaks. However this journey has looked for you and your child, it's all valid.

At five months, here's what real progress looks like across different families:

• Some children are self-initiating — walking to the bathroom, giving a signal, saying or gesturing "I need to go"
• Others have built strong awareness but still need support with timing — they feel the signal but don't always make it in time
• Some are still in the awareness-building phase — longer dry stretches, pausing, noticing accidents after they happen
• A few children are still building tolerance — wearing underwear longer each day, which is itself a win
• And for some, the progress is internal — happening in ways that aren't visible yet but are still real

Why It Takes Time

The nervous system doesn't follow a calendar

For some children, the body-brain connection for the potty signal forms quickly. For others, it takes many months of consistent signal exposure. Neither path is right or wrong — they're just different nervous systems responding at their own pace.

You may have stopped trying traditional methods. That's not failure — that's wisdom. But if you ever want to try again, the reason nothing worked is: the signal wasn't reaching the brain. The mechanism hasn't changed, and it still explains why.

▶ Success isn't "trained in X days." Success is "today he looked down at the wet spot." Progress is a longer dry stretch. A pause before the accident. A hand on the pants. Those count.
