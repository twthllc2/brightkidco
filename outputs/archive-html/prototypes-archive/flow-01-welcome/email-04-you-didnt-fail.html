The first thing I want to tell you: you didn't fail.

I know it feels that way. I know you've spent nights on Reddit, money on methods, and energy you didn't have. I know you've heard "just be consistent" and "they'll get it when they're ready" more times than you can count.

Let me show you why every method you tried was solving the wrong problem — and why that's not your fault.

Method #1

Oh Crap! / The Naked Weekend

The promise: Three days without pants, and they'll figure it out.

The truth: I tried it too. Both my sons went through it, and neither one came out trained. The method assumes your child feels the signal and just needs motivation. For many autistic kids, the signal isn't there to act on. The result? Accidents without containment, chaos without learning.

Method #2

Sticker Charts & Reward Systems

The promise: Incentivize success. One sticker per dry trip.

The truth: My kids couldn't feel the signal that sticker charts depend on. Rewards require awareness that something happened. If the signal arrives too late — or not at all — the connection between "I need to go" and "I get a sticker" never forms. The chart stays empty. The parent feels like a failure.

Method #3

Timed Sits / Scheduled Toileting

The promise: Take them every 20-30 minutes. Eventually it sticks.

The truth: Timers teach a schedule, not a signal. Your child learns to sit on command — or to resist — but not to feel the internal cue. For an autistic child, running on an external schedule can feel arbitrary and dysregulating, turning potty time into a power struggle.

Method #4

Pull-Ups as a Bridge

The promise: A middle ground between diapers and underwear.

The truth: Pull-ups absorb the exact sensation your child needs to learn from. The feedback loop — "I peed, I felt it, I learned" — is interrupted every time it tries to form. Your child feels nothing, learns nothing, and stays in pull-ups longer than necessary. Not because you gave up — because the product design was working against you.

Your child isn't failing at potty training.

Your methods were solving the wrong problem.

Whether you've tried 2 methods or 20 — the reason is the same. Every method assumed the signal was there. For your child, it wasn't there yet.

That changes everything. Because it means you weren't doing it wrong. You were working with the right effort — just the wrong map.
