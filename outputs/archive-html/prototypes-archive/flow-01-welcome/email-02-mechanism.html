The Sensory Gap

Every human body has an internal signaling system called interoception — the ability to feel what's happening inside. Hunger. Thirst. The need to pee.

For autistic kids, this system often works differently. The "I need to go" signal arrives too late, too weak, or not at all. Research shows this is a documented neurological difference — not stubbornness, not defiance, not a lack of trying. (Nicholson et al. 2019, Journal of Autism and Developmental Disorders)

This is why sticker charts and timer sits fail. They assume a child can feel the signal and chooses not to act. If the signal never arrives, no amount of rewards or scheduled sits will build it.

The good news: the body can learn this signal. It takes time, and it needs the right kind of feedback. Our Body-Signal Learning Layer was designed specifically for this — to create a gentle, predictable sensation that helps the brain start building the connection.
