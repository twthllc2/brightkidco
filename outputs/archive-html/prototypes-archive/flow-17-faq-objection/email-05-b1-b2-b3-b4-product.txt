Sometimes the things holding us back aren't big doubts — they're specific, practical questions. Let me answer the ones I hear most often.

"We tried underwear before. It didn't work. Why would this be different?"
Standard training underwear catches the accident — that's all it does. Pull-ups wick the signal away entirely so the child feels nothing. The Body-Signal Learning Layer is a fundamentally different mechanism: it delivers sustained, localized wetness for 30-60 seconds so a hypo-responsive nervous system can register the sensation. It creates the feedback loop that standard underwear and pull-ups actively prevent. Research shows undergarment type directly affects training outcomes (Simon & Thompson, 2006).

"What if my child won't wear them — or rips them off?"
We expect sensory acclimation. Start by leaving them next to the potty. Let your child explore them. Wear them over a pull-up first — the learning layer can still work through it. 100% cotton, hypoallergenic, thin fit. Fun character designs (Melon Pop, Lily Pop, Fynn Fox, Coco Cloud). Gradual is the goal — no pressure to switch immediately.

"My child has GI issues / withholds. Can this really help?"
The withholding cycle is real — and it's medical, not behavioral. Autistic children are 4x more likely to have GI symptoms (McElhanon et al., 2014, Pediatrics). The Body-Signal Learning Layer works alongside your medical team's recommendations. It doesn't replace GI protocols. It helps the body start recognizing the signal that gets lost in the withholding-anxiety cycle.

"Will this conflict with our BCBA's program / therapy?"
Your BCBA has built the behavioral foundation. This adds the sensory layer. Behavioral protocols teach compliance — sitting, following a schedule. The Body-Signal Learning Layer teaches the body to feel. Together they're powerful. Share our research with your BCBA — most see the value immediately.

"What if my child is too old / too severe?"
There's no age cutoff for body awareness. The research is clear: interoceptive differences resolve or compensate over time. Maureen Bennie's son trained at 9.5. "The vast majority of autistic children CAN be potty trained" — Dr. Elizabeth Cross, Kennedy Krieger/Johns Hopkins.
