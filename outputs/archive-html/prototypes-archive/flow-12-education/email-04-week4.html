Three weeks in. This is the point where many parents start asking real questions. Let me address the most common ones — based on what I've seen with my own boys and thousands of families in our community.

"Progress feels slow — what am I doing wrong?"

The honest answer: probably nothing. The interoception research shows that building a new sensory pathway takes time. For some children, 3 weeks is enough to see clear signal recognition. For others, it takes 6-8 weeks. Both are normal.

The key question isn't "how many accidents?" — it's "is awareness growing?" If your child pauses more often, looks at their underwear, or stays dry for slightly longer — the pathway is forming.

"We saw progress — now it's gone. What happened?"

"Regression isn't failure — it's sensory recalibration. The pathways you built in the first three weeks are still there, even if it doesn't look like it."

Regression is one of the most common — and most misunderstood — parts of this process. What looks like a setback is often the nervous system reorganizing. The body is learning at a deeper level. The awareness doesn't disappear — it integrates.

Research shows that interoception can be trained, and the childhood window is the most responsive time for this learning (Nicholson et al. 2019). Regression doesn't erase the neural pathway — it means the brain is recalibrating.

Long-term success tips:

1. Keep the underwear on consistently. Switching between pull-ups and BSL confuses the signal. Pick one and commit.

2. Watch for triggers. Some children show more awareness at specific times of day. Morning and after meals are common windows.

3. Reduce prompted sits. If you're taking them every 30 minutes, try every 90. The goal is child-initiated awareness, not parent-prompted compliance.

4. Name the feeling. "I see you noticed something. That's your body telling you something." Language reinforces the neural connection.

5. Take breaks if needed. I took breaks with both my boys. A week off doesn't reset progress — sometimes it accelerates it.

What the research tells us

The science is clear: sustained sensory feedback is required for operant conditioning in toilet training (Bacotti, Perez & Vollmer, 2023). The Body-Signal Learning Layer provides exactly that — sustained, gentle feedback that pull-ups and regular underwear can't match.

And the strongest predictor of long-term success?

Consistency. Not intensity. Not a specific method. Just showing up, day after day, and letting the nervous system build the connection at its own pace.

You're doing that. That matters more than you know.
