I want to talk about what progress looks like when the path is longer. Not the picture-perfect version. The real one.

For some children, progress doesn't look like sitting on the toilet. It looks like pausing during play. Looking down at their waistband. Putting a hand to their stomach. Tolerating underwear for 10 minutes before asking for something familiar back.

These are not failures. These are signals that the nervous system is registering something new.

I think about Maureen Bennie's story often. Her son was 9.5 years old when he finally trained. Not 3. Not 4. Not 5. Nine and a half. And that wasn't a story about failure — it was a story about a body that needed more time to build the connection.

If your child is older and still not there, you are not behind. You are on your child's timeline. And that timeline is valid.

The learning layer was designed for children at every stage. It doesn't promise overnight results. It creates a gentle, consistent signal that the brain can learn to recognize — at whatever pace the nervous system needs.

If your child tolerates it for 10 minutes one day and 15 the next, that is progress. If they pause and look down, that is progress. If nothing changes and you just need someone to say "this is hard and you're doing the right thing" — that is also valid.
