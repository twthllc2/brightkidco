Your verbal child — the language-to-body connection

Hi {first_name},

Seven weeks in. Something I hear from Level 1 parents around this time: "He can talk about the potty perfectly. He knows the routine. He just won't do it."

This is the paradox of the verbal autistic child in potty training: the language is there, but the body signal hasn't caught up. The words exist in the cognitive brain. The sensation needs to land in the insula — the part of the brain that maps physical body states. They are two separate systems, and they develop on different timelines. That's not stubbornness. That's neurology.

How to bridge the language-to-body gap:

1. Use body-focused language, not routine language. Instead of "It's time to sit on the potty" (routine cue), say "Notice what your body is feeling right now" (interoceptive cue).

2. Play "what does your body say?" Make it a game. Pause and ask: "What is your body telling you right now?" Model it yourself.

3. Name the pre-signals. When you see shifting, squirming, or pausing — name it aloud. "I see you moving. That might mean your body needs to go to the potty."

4. Separate the language from the action. Some kids can tell you ALL the steps but cannot feel the signal. The language is a scaffold, not a shortcut.

When the words and the body finally connected

"My son could give a full lecture on the potty routine. But he'd still have accidents while standing right next to it. I started asking 'what does your body need?' instead of 'do you need to go?' It took a few weeks, but one day he looked at me and said 'my body feels tight.' He walked to the potty and went. That was the moment it clicked." — Amanda, mom of a 4-year-old
