When a parent tells me they've tried everything, I believe them. And then I explain why every method they tried was working against a problem they couldn't see.

The problem isn't behavior. It's signal timing.

Interoception is the body's ability to sense what's happening internally — hunger, heartbeat, a full bladder. Research shows that many autistic children process interoceptive signals differently. The signal is there, but it arrives at a different volume or with a different timing than traditional potty training methods expect.

Sticker charts assume the child feels the signal and needs motivation to act on it. Timer sits assume the child can predict when the signal is coming. Neither works when the signal itself hasn't been established.
