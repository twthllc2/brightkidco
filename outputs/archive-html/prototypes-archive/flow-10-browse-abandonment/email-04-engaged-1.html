Here's how the Body-Signal Learning Layer works, step by step:

1. The learning layer is sewn into the underwear at the point where wetness first occurs — right where the body would naturally signal.

2. When your child pees, the learning layer holds a small amount of moisture against the skin, creating a tactile signal. It's not uncomfortable — it's noticeable. And for a child whose brain wasn't receiving the internal signal, this external tactile cue becomes the bridge.

3. Over time, the brain learns to associate that sensation with the internal signal. Parents tell us their children start pausing, looking down, even walking toward the bathroom. That's the interoceptive pathway forming.

4. The outer cotton-blend layer catches the rest — so you're not cleaning up messes constantly while the learning happens.

The pull-up problem explained:

Pull-ups are designed to wick moisture away instantly — great for skin health, counterproductive for signal training. When wetness disappears before the child can feel it, the brain never learns to connect the sensation with the need to go. The Body-Signal Learning Layer does the opposite. It holds the signal long enough for the brain to notice.
