## 1. WHO SHE IS

### Demographics
- **Age range:** 28-38, skewing early-30s. Younger than L2 (30-42) and L3 (32-48).
- **Gender:** ~95%+ female.
- **Location:** ~65% USA, 15% UK, 15% AU/NZ, 5% CA. UK/AU use "nappies," not "diapers" (V22).
- **Income & employment:** Upper-middle / middle skew. ~70% are working — juggling caregiving with a job or career.
- **Education:** ~60% college-educated. Reads, Googles, processes written copy. Educated consumer, not clinical expert.
- **Marital status:** Married/partnered ~85%; single moms ~15%. Her partner's voice is *blaming* ("Why can't you?") — not L2's "sabotage" or L3's "abandonment."
- **Insurance:** 10-15% Medicaid. Math reframe is OK here (unlike L3). $79.99 3+3 bundle is the dominant offer; $34.99 1-pair is the trust-builder starter.

### Family Situation
- She has at least one **neurotypical older child or sibling** who potty trained without drama. That contrast haunts her. The "he's the only one still in pull-ups" comparison runs in the background of every email she opens.
- Her partner's patience is thinner than hers — *"just let him be a kid," "my sister trained hers at 2 — what's wrong with you?"* — never cruel, but casually confident in a way that cuts.
- She has at least one close mom-friend whose kids trained on time. Their success is a silent knife at every playdate.

### Child Specifics
- **Age range:** 3-5 years, concentrated at 3.5-4.5 (the Pre-K window).
- **Verbal ability:** **Verbal, conversational.** He can name body parts, say "I need to pee" — *just not in time* — sing the potty song, identify the potty. He just can't act on the urge before the event.
- **ASD severity:** **Low support needs.** Mainstream Pre-K or Kindergarten. NO IEP for potty, NO BCBA. May or may not have a formal autism diagnosis — many L1 moms are in the "he's quirky but verbal" zone and receive the diagnosis during the Pre-K evaluation process.
- **Support needs:** Low. SPD prevalence is moderate (not L3's ~98%). ADHD follows the SPARK 32.9% comorbid baseline. Anxiety common. GI issues exist but aren't the dominant cluster.
- **SPD profile:** Moderate. He may dislike the feel of wet clothes briefly but doesn't have meltdown-level sensory storm. Flushing is annoying, not self-injurious.

### Daily Life Rhythm
- **Morning:** Out by 7:30-8:00 for Pre-K drop-off. Pull-up under clothes, "just in case." She checks at pickup — sometimes dry, sometimes soaked.
- **Midday:** Working or caring for younger siblings. The 2pm pickup is the daily report: "He had one accident. He had two. He had a great day."
- **Afternoon:** Unstructured play, snack, second potty attempt (he sits 15-20 minutes, nothing happens, then wets his pants five minutes after getting up).
- **Evening:** Bath, dinner, books, bed. Pull-up on for sleep — overnight dryness often a separate issue not yet addressed.
- **Weekends:** Playdates, errands, outings. "I can't leave the house without three outfit changes" anxiety is rising.
- **2am research:** Yes. Queries: *"Why does my 4 year old say he needs to pee after he goes?"* *"Preschool won't take him in pull-ups."* *"Autism potty training."* This is the L1 keyword set.

## 2. WHAT SHE'S TRIED

### Methods She Has Attempted (10-20 on average)
Naming these specifically in copy is how she recognizes we know her. Grouped by approach:

**Mainstream "easy" methods:** (1) **Oh Crap method** — the most-mentioned failure: *"Oh Crap stressed us both out."* 3-day naked weekend. (2) **Sticker charts** — printouts, hand-drawn, "1 sticker = 1 pee." Bored by week two. (3) **Reward systems** — M&Ms, screen time, character underwear. **Reward Bribery 2.0** (bigger prizes). (4) **Big kid underwear shopping trip** — excited for one day, then back to pull-ups. (5) **Books** — "Oh Crap," "Potty Training in 3 Days," "The Potty Book."

**Scheduled / behaviorist methods:** (6) **Timed sits** — every 30-60 minutes. He pees immediately after getting up. (7) **Verbal prompting** — *"Do you need to go potty?"* every 30 minutes. He says "no" reflexively. (8) **Pull-ups as transition** — he treats them like diapers. (9) **Social stories** — "Everyone Poops," custom laminated stories. (10) **Elimination communication** — watching for cues, holding him over the toilet. (11) **Different potties** — small seat, music potty, character potty, seat reducer. (12) **Public bathroom practice** — drag-along attempts at restaurants, grandparents' houses. (13) **3-day naked weekend** — disastrous. Couches, rugs, floor puddles.

**Authority / advice channels:** (14) **Pediatrician consultation** — *"He's not ready yet. Give it six months."* She's now given it six months. Twice. (15) **Family advice** — *"Just wait until he's ready." "My kids trained by 2."* (16) **Internet rabbit holes** — r/pottytraining, r/Autism_Parenting, Facebook groups, Amazon reviews. (17) **Peer comparison** — asking other moms what worked. Their answers don't translate.

**Punishment / surrender:** (18) **Punishment** — even if she's ashamed to admit it. Time-outs, frustrated voices, the "big kid" shame lever. She regrets it. (19) **Nothing** — putting it off for months because she can't face another failed attempt.

(Underlying count of distinct methods tried: 10-20.)

### How Long She Has Been Trying
**6-18 months on average.** Not years like L2/L3. Relatively early in her journey — which is why her hope is still at 70/30. The 6-month mark is when most L1 moms hit the "this isn't normal" inflection. The 12-month mark is when they start Googling autism-specific potty training. The 18-month mark is when she enters the funnel.

### What's Worked Partially vs. Failed Completely
**Worked partially:** *Verbal cooperation* — he *will* go when placed on the toilet, just won't initiate; *routine-based success* — prompt-dependent, not internal-signal-driven; *short windows of confidence* — a perfect week then evaporates (*"He had a perfect week, then a month of accidents"*); *Pre-K partial success* — "mostly trained" at school with teacher prompting, accidents at home.

**Failed completely:** *Self-initiation* — he will not walk to the bathroom on his own; *the "uh-oh" reaction* — no look-down, no potty dance, no pre-event signal recognition; *nighttime dryness* — same interoception gap; *consistency* — *"some days he's great, other days zero"*; *pull-ups as bridge* — he treats them like diapers, no "step-down" sensation.

### Who Has Given Her Advice
- **Pediatrician** — *"Wait until he's ready."* (Heard 2-3 times. She no longer believes it.)
- **Mother-in-law / mother** — *"My kids trained by 2."* (Crippling comparison.)
- **Facebook mom groups** — well-meaning but contradictory, occasionally judgmental.
- **Reddit r/Autism_Parenting** — the goldmine discovery moment where she learns the interoception connection.
- **A friend with an autistic older child** — usually the trigger person. *"Hey, my son did the same thing. Look into interoception."*
- **Pre-K teacher** — sometimes: *"He might need an evaluation."* Often the diagnostic trigger.
- **NOT a BCBA.** L1 moms typically do not have a BCBA. Mentioning BCBA protocols is irrelevant and out-of-frame.

## 3. WHERE SHE IS EMOTIONALLY AT PROGRAM ENTRY

### Her State at Signup
**Hopeful but critical.** Not desperate (L2), not resigned (L3). She has a deadline, hope, and a BS detector she doesn't yet trust us to pass.

Emotional peak mix at signup:
- **Confusion — 80% peak.** *"Why is this so hard? Other kids just... did it."*
- **Panic/Fear — 90% peak** (her highest). Pre-K deadline. *"What if he starts kindergarten in pull-ups?"*
- **Guilt/Shame — 80% peak.** *"I feel like a failure because he's still in pull-ups."*
- **Hope/Relief — 80% peak** (also her highest). She still believes a solution exists.
- **Skepticism — 30% peak.** Guarded but open. Not yet the L2 wall.

### What Triggered Her to Look For Help NOW
**The Pre-K enrollment letter** (6-8 weeks before school starts): *"Children must be toilet trained to attend."* This letter moves her from "we'll figure it out eventually" to "I need a solution in six weeks."

Secondary triggers: a friend with an older autistic child suggesting interoception; a teacher gently suggesting evaluation; a successful week followed by an unexplained regression; a pediatrician saying "let's wait" one too many times.

### Her Self-Blame Level
**80% peak.** She believes, in her quiet moments, that she has failed at something every other mom figured out. If her kid is still in pull-ups at 4, it must be her fault. The mechanism reframe — *"You solved the wrong problem"* — dissolves this. She has not heard it before. She needs to.

Her self-blame shows up as: *"I must not be consistent enough." "Maybe I started too early." "Maybe I started too late." "Maybe I let him have too many screen days." "His sister was so easy. What did I do differently?"*

### Her Hope Level (70/30)
**70% hope, 30% realism — the highest ratio across all four levels.** She believes a solution exists and has not yet exhausted her belief. The 30% realism is the voice that says *"Maybe he's just not ready"* — fed by the pediatrician, the in-laws, every other mom whose kid trained at 2.5 without effort.

**Crushes her hope:** another month of accidents; a friend telling her to "just wait"; a regression; a failed attempt at a new method.
**Restores her hope:** mechanism explanations; parent quotes from moms like her; real progress (even a single accident-free day); the 49% SPARK stat; Lena's peer voice.

### Her Skepticism Toward Marketing Claims
**30% peak.** Guarded but open. Marketing polish doesn't yet trigger instant shutdown — but it makes her scan for the lie. She's the mom who Googles "BrightKidCo reviews" before clicking "buy" and reads the FAQ before trusting the headline.

**Trips her skepticism:** big promises (*"Easy," "Quick," "Guaranteed"*); AI-isms (*"Revolutionary," "Game-changer," "Unlock"*); polished stock photos of NT happy families; urgency that feels manipulative (*"Last chance," "Time is running out"*).

**Passes her skepticism:** mechanism specificity (*"Interoception," "Signal-timing gap"*); Lena's voice; a peer quote from a mom exactly like her; the 60-day guarantee; the 49% SPARK stat.

### Her Trust in Brands She Has Never Heard Of
**Low-to-medium initial trust.** She'll open the email (she signed up), read the first paragraph, and form a trust judgment in the first 2-3 sentences. If "marketing," she's gone. If "this person gets it," she stays.

She came in through a pop-up that promised 10% off and a "potty training plan built for kids like yours." She's mildly skeptical of that headline. Welcome E1's job is to convert "mildly skeptical" into "okay, keep going."

## 4. HER TRUST THRESHOLD

### What Destroys Trust Instantly
- **"Easy" / "Simple" / "Effortless."** She believes it should be easy. If we say it is, we lose her.
- **"Quick" / "Fast."** 6-18 months of trying. "Quick" feels like a lie.
- **"Just like other kids" / "Just like NT kids."** Comparison shame trigger.
- **"Wait until he's ready."** What her pediatrician said. If we repeat it, we are her pediatrician.
- **"Just try harder" / "Be more consistent."** Things she tells herself. We must not echo them.
- **"Your child is severely affected."** Her child is verbal, bright, quirky, not severe.
- **"Non-verbal kids need..."** Her kid is verbal. She'll skip the sentence.
- **"Years of failed attempts."** Months, not years.
- **"BCBA protocol."** She doesn't have a BCBA.
- **"Adult diapers."** Her child is 4. Catastrophic.
- **"Forever in pull-ups."** She has not accepted "forever."
- **"Severe sensory issues."** Moderate, not severe.
- **"Late training is normal."** She wants to train him on time. Normalizing feels like giving up.
- **"Level 1/2/3" labels.** Never name levels in copy.
- **Subject line no-gos (§11.4):** *"Last chance," "Miracle solution," "Severe autism," "Years of struggles," "Time is running out," "Save your kid."*

### What Builds Trust Slowly
The trust pyramid, in order — reversing order kills conversion:
- **Tier 1 — Mechanism specificity.** *"Your child's nervous system processes the signal — just not fast enough."* The foundation everything sits on.
- **Tier 3 — Failure acknowledgment.** *"You haven't failed potty training. You solved the wrong problem."* The emotional unlock.
- **Tier 2 — Lena's peer voice.** *"Both my sons went through this."* Peer, not expert.
- **Tier 4 — Realistic timelines.** *"Some kids 2 weeks. Others 8. Both normal."*
- **Tier 5 — 60-day guarantee.** Risk removal. *"Try it for 60 days. By your judgment."*

Plus: specific failed-method naming (sticker charts, Oh Crap, timed sits, pull-ups); L1-anchor recognition blocks (*"Sits 20 minutes, nothing happens," "Says 'I need to pee' after it's already happened," "Says 'no' when asked," "Some days great, other days zero"*); the 49% SPARK stat (universal normalization); permission-not-to-buy tone (soft "nice to have" at L1, vs required at L2).

### Her BS Detector Sensitivity
**Medium-high.** Not as jaded as L2 (90% peak). But not naive — she's been Googling for months.

**Trips her BS detector:** stock photography of NT happy families; marketing voice ("empower," "transform," "leverage"); unqualified claims ("98% of parents see results"); absence of mechanism (just promises); heavy borders, centered marketing text, "BUY NOW" buttons; any brand-wide forbidden phrase; "guaranteed" without 60-day specifics.

**Does NOT trip her BS detector:** long emails (she reads); specific symptoms named in detail; Lena's signature; the 60-day guarantee framed as "your judgment"; a peer quote from an L1 mom; the 49% SPARK stat with proper citation.

## 5. HER DECISION SPEED

### 2-7 Days From Signup to Purchase
The **fastest decision period across all four levels.** She signs up, reads, decides, buys. The welcome flow (D0/1/3/5/7/10/14/21) covers the entire decision arc with room to spare.

**Best conversion cell: L1 × P2 (First Real Attempt) — 15-25%.** The highest-converting cell in the entire matrix. If we get the L1 message right, she converts.

### What Accelerates vs. Delays Her Decision

**Accelerates:**
- **Pre-K deadline.** The closer the deadline, the faster she buys. *"Six weeks to go." "Preschool starts September 3rd."* Calendar-driven urgency is the L1 accelerant.
- **A friend's success.** *"My friend's kid trained in 2 weeks on these."* Social proof from someone she knows.
- **Mechanism click.** The moment interoception lands — *"OH, that's why Oh Crap didn't work"* — she's 70% to purchase.
- **The 49% stat.** *"49% of autistic 4-5 year olds aren't trained. You are not behind."*
- **The 60-day guarantee.** Risk removal.
- **Subject lines that mirror panic without manufacturing it.** *"Six weeks until Pre-K starts"* (calendar) vs *"LAST CHANCE!"* (manufactured).
- **Cart abandonment (1h/24h/72h) with Pre-K timing anchor.** Her primary recovery lever.

**Delays:**
- **Financial hesitation.** $79.99 is real money. Math reframe ("less than two months of pull-ups") helps but doesn't eliminate this.
- **Partner disagreement.** *"I want to try this, but he thinks we should just wait."* The L1 partner dynamic is *disagreement*, not L2 sabotage or L3 abandonment. We must NEVER suggest she has to convince him. *"This is your decision."*
- **Skepticism higher than expected.** If the welcome flow lands poorly, she lingers in cart-recovery before leaving.
- **60-day guarantee not being explicit.** If she can't find the return policy, she hesitates.

### What Kills the Sale Entirely
- **An "Easy" claim in the cart-recovery email.** Single biggest kill.
- **A "Last chance" countdown manufactured artificially.** She can smell manufactured urgency.
- **A partner-conflict trigger.** Never imply she needs her partner's permission.
- **Shipping cost surprise at checkout.** Always show shipping clearly.
- **Any forbidden phrase from §8.1 or §8.5.** Even one, in a single email, can take her out of the funnel permanently.
- **A non-L1 image.** A non-verbal kid, a kid in adult diapers, a sensory-meltdown moment. She does not recognize herself in those images.
- **Subject line that names her child wrong.** *"Your nonverbal child..."* — she doesn't have a nonverbal child. She has a verbal one who can't feel the signal.

## 7. WHAT SHE NEEDS FROM US

### The Brand Promise That Resonates With Her
> "It's not behavior. It's a signal-timing gap. And there's a third option — between pull-ups and underwear — designed for a nervous system that needs to feel the signal to learn it."

Three moves in one breath:
1. **Reframe blame** — she didn't fail, she solved the wrong problem.
2. **Name the problem precisely** — signal-timing gap, interoception, body-signal translation.
3. **Offer the third option** — between pull-ups (too dry = no feedback) and regular underwear (too messy). The product is the bridge.

What does NOT resonate: *"A miracle solution"* (she's past that); *"A proven system"* (she's been through those); *"Finally, something that works"* (conditional optimism, not "finally"); *"You deserve this"* (patronizing).

### The Mechanism Explanation That Makes Sense to Her

> "Your child's brain and body are sending signals on different frequencies. Most potty training methods assume the child can feel when they need to go. But autistic children often can't feel that signal until it's too late. It's not because they're not listening. It's because the signal arrives late. Pull-ups make it worse — they absorb the wetness so the brain gets zero feedback. Regular underwear makes a mess. We made a third option — underwear that lets him feel a gentle 'uh-oh' sensation without ruining the couch."

Why this lands: uses "nervous system" (scientific, not patronizing); names her specific methods (pull-ups, regular underwear); introduces a third option (the product); does not blame her or her child.

### The Social Proof She Trusts

**Other moms like her — NOT experts.** Hard rule for L1.

**What works:**
- L1 peer quotes: *"He had a perfect week, then a month of accidents."*
- L1 success moment quotes: *"He went today! Once! But he went!"*
- Lena's voice: *"Both my sons went through this."*
- The 49% SPARK stat with citation.

**What does NOT work:**
- BCBA endorsements (she doesn't have one — out of frame).
- Pediatrician endorsements (she's tired of pediatricians).
- "16,511 reviews, 4.9 stars" framed as social proof (feels like marketing).
- Founder story in Phase 1 (Mary S. is for the Phase 2 dedicated mail only).
- Clinical trial language ("N=5,679 in a controlled study") — she doesn't speak this language yet.

## 10. THE ONE THING

### The Single Most Important Thing L1 Moms Need to Hear

> **"It's not behavior. It's signal-timing. And there's a third option between pull-ups and underwear that lets him feel what his body is trying to tell him."**

This is the **mechanism reframe + third-option introduction** in one breath. It does four jobs simultaneously: (1) absolves her — she didn't fail, the methods were solving the wrong problem; (2) explains the problem — interoception gap, signal-timing, neurological not behavioral; (3) names the product positioning — third option between pull-ups and regular underwear; (4) restores hope — there IS a path that respects his nervous system.

Every L1 email should orbit this sentence. Not literally repeat it, but be derivable from it. The hook is the mechanism. The CTA is the third option. The proof is Lena's voice. The timeline is realistic. The guarantee is 60 days.

The 49% SPARK stat is the supporting pillar: *"49% of autistic 4-5 year olds aren't toilet trained. You are not behind. You are not a failure. Your child's nervous system just processes body signals differently — and that's a problem we can work with."*

These two together — mechanism reframe + normalization stat — are the heartbeat of L1 communication. Every email should contain at least one; the most important emails contain both.

### The Single Most Dangerous Thing We Could Send Her

> **"Easy potty training for autistic kids — guaranteed in 3 days."**

Or any variation that combines the forbidden words into one sentence: easy, simple, quick, guaranteed, train, fix, cure, miracle, breakthrough, just like other kids.

This sentence (or any near-equivalent) would: (1) trip her BS detector (marketing voice); (2) reinforce her self-blame (it should be easy and I'm failing); (3) out-frame her child (her kid is not "autistic kids" generically — he's verbal, 3-5, low support); (4) set a false timeline (3 days = setting her up to fail again); (5) violate brand-wide §8.1 on at least 4 words; (6) violate §8.5 L1 list on at least 3 phrases; (7) lose her permanently, and she'll tell her mom-friends.

The dangerous content is not just what we say — it's what we imply by what we don't say. If we don't name the mechanism, we're marketing. If we don't acknowledge her specific failed methods, we're guessing. If we don't anchor to Pre-K reality, we're generic. If we don't sound like Lena, we're a brand. Every one of those omissions is dangerous.

The safe content — the content that converts — is specific, peer-voiced, mechanism-anchored, and Lena-signed. Every email should feel like it could only have been written by someone who has been through this. Because Lena has. And Sarah will know.

## APPENDIX: L1 Email Build Checklist

**Subject Line** — 40-50 chars. Mirror / mechanism / observation hook. No "Last chance," "Miracle," "Severe," "Years of struggles," "Time is running out," "Save your kid." No exclamation. Pre-K timing OK (L1 only).

**Opening (2-3 Sentences)** — Mirror her reality (*"He says 'I need to pee' AFTER it happens"*) OR name a method she tried. No "Easy," "Simple," "Quick," "Guaranteed." No level labels.

**CTA** — Full-width. Brand-Green #039902 / white text. Permission-toned (*"See if this works for him"*). 48px+ height. Links to ASD PDP: `/products/potty-training-underwear-for-sensory-sensitive-toddlers-sp`.

**Sign-Off** — Lena only. Position-matched closing: "Talk soon" (E1-E2), "With understanding" (E3-E5), "With care" (E6+). Max 2-3 sentences + max 2-line P.S.
