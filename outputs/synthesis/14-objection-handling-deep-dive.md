### Objection D1 — "I've tried everything. Why would this be different?"

**Intensity:** ●●●●● Very High (all levels)
**Frequency:** 150+ direct mentions
**Phase:** Phase 2-3
**Trust Tier:** #3 Failure Acknowledgment + #1 Mechanism

#### Verbatim Parent Quotes

| Quote | Source |
|-------|--------|
| "We have tried everything from being calm & gently redirecting to firmly making it known it is not okay & applying consequences." | Ellie Brooks Pecoraro, Kommentare |
| "I've tried everything it seems like." | Destiny Jog, Kommentare |
| "I have done everything the book says to do. I dedicated so much time & energy and she's just not having it." | Laurynn LeAnn Howard, Kommentare |
| "I tried everything I could think of and every piece of advice I received." | Kommentare-3 |
| "We've spoken to health visitors that referred us to the family rapid team and we tried everything, and they acknowledged that we've tried everything, but we're still having no luck." | Kommentare-5 |
| "We have done it all. Bribes, rewards, punishments, praise, nothing works." | DOC1 |

#### Underlying Fear

The parent has exhausted her emotional reserves. "I've tried everything" is not a statement — it's a trauma response. She's afraid that:
- Nothing will ever work for her child
- She's wasted thousands of dollars and years of effort
- She's failed as a parent
- Any new product is just another disappointment waiting to happen
- The problem might be unsolvable (her child's autism is "too much")

#### Exact BKC Response Copy

> You didn't fail. You showed up 100 times with sticker charts, social stories, timed sits, ABA protocols, and 4 different potty seats. You gave everything you had.
>
> The problem was never your effort — it was the problem those methods were solving.
>
> They were training behavior. Your child needed a different signal.
>
> Sticker charts: rewards can't create or clarify internal body signals (Kelly Mahler).
> Oh Crap: expects a "pee-pee dance" signal that never arrives for autistic kids.
> Timer schedules: replace the internal signal with an external one — creating prompt-dependency.
> Social stories: explain the WHAT but not the HOW-IT-FEELS.
> ABA protocols: teach behavioral compliance without sensory awareness.
>
> Every single one assumes your child's body sends a clear "I need to go" signal in time to act on it. For kids with sensory processing differences, that signal doesn't arrive the way the textbooks say it should.
>
> You haven't failed. You solved the wrong problem.

#### Supporting Proof Point

- **Primary:** Lever 1 (Sensory Gap) — "You've tried [method]. Here's why it couldn't work for your child's nervous system"
- **Secondary:** Lever 5 (49% SPARK Stat) — "49% of autistic 4-5 year olds aren't trained. This isn't a you problem."
- **Authority:** Kelly Mahler — "Rewards can't create or clarify internal body signals"
- **Failed Methods:** #2 Oh Crap, #3 Timer Schedules, #5 ABA, #1 Sticker Charts

> Pee training and poop training are not the same skill.
>
> Poop requires different muscle groups, different timing, and often has a different sensory profile. Many kids who are fully day-trained for pee take months longer for poop.
>
> The interoception gap affects both systems — but poop has an additional fear component. If pooping has been painful in the past (even once), the child learns to hold it. That's not behavior — it's a protective reflex.
>
> What helps: the same body-signal mechanism. The underwear creates sustained sensation for BOTH pee and poop. The signal pathway is the same. It just may take longer for the poop system to connect.

- **Secondary:** Lever 3 (Guarantee) — time to address both systems
