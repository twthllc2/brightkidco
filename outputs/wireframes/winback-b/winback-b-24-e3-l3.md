## 2. Strategy Box

**Big Idea:** "The Open Door — dignity doesn't expire." This is not a final close. It is a door left ajar, deliberately, warmly, without hinges of pressure. The creative metaphor: an interior door, slightly open, with warm light spilling through. Not dramatic. Not urgent. Just... available.

For L3 specifically, this Big Idea must pass the Dignity Test before anything else. The door is not a sales tunnel or a conversion funnel. It is an opening that says: "You are in control of whether and when you enter. I am not going to ask twice."

**Contraption (Design Mechanism):** "The Verbatim Triad Block (The Mirror Wall)" — Six L3 parent quotes, organized into two groups of three, presented without marketing interpretation. Group One ("The Exhausted Enumeration") lists the methods and attempts L3 parents have made. Group Two ("The Resigned Peace") shows the language of parents who've moved from struggle to acceptance. Each quote is attributed with a short italic line beneath. No Lena analysis between them. Just the quotes. They serve as a mirror — Maria reads herself in other parents' words. The card uses Mint #DBFFCD background, 12px border-radius, 24px padding.

**Emotional Arc Position:** E3 is the final email — "This my last email unless you tell me to keep going." For L3, the emotional arc is: Dignity acknowledgment → Validation through verbatim quotes → Gentle re-invitation (no pressure) → Text-only CTA → Silence after the P.S.

**Component Selection Rationale:**
- HERO-20 (Handwritten Letter Opener): Minimal. No hero image. Just the subject line as headline. Dignity-first means no visual interruption.
- LETTER-02 (Handwritten Opener): Long-form paragraph stack with generous 48px section spacing.
- SP — Verbatim Triad (CONTRAPTION): 6 verbatim parent quotes in Mint card. The emotional anchor of the email.
- EDU-1 (Definition Block): Brief mechanism in the re-invitation section. No eyebrow. No stat box. Just text.
- ProductShowcaseSoft: 1 Pair $34.99 only — "add alongside, not replacement." Deliberately small offer.
- CTA-4 (Gentle Invite): Text link only. "When you decide, it's here." No button. No pill. No uppercase.
- TRUST-1 (60-Day Seal): Guarantee stated in text, not as visual badge.

**Differentiation Note:** L3 is the most visually minimal of all four E3 wireframes. No hero image. No product photo. No CTA button (text link only). No stat boxes. No decorative furniture beyond the Verbatim Triad card and thin separators. The palette is cream-dominant with gray prominent, minimal green. The email reads like a letter, not a brochure. Zero animation.

### Block: Verbatim Triad (Section 2)
I've heard versions of this from parents who've been in your shoes. Parents who've tried everything, and I mean *everything*, not as a phrase but as a lived reality:

*"We did 40 hours of ABA a week for 2 years. He's still in pull-ups."*

*"We even did a residential program for 3 weeks. Back in pull-ups within a month."*

*"I spent $2500 on a private potty consultant. Same result."*

And then there are the moments of quiet, resigned peace, the language that tells me this parent has moved from active struggle to something deeper:

*"We stopped active training 2 years ago to focus on dignity."*

*"He'll get there when he gets there. Or he won't. Either way, he's my son."*

*"Maybe one day. Maybe never. We're at peace either way."*

I know this because my older son was 8 before we figured out body-signal learning. One of the parents I hear from most, Maureen Bennie, her son was 9.5 when he finally trained. Another family in our community: 8 years. Late is not never.

I'm not saying your child will train by a certain age. I'm not making promises about outcomes, and I never will. What I am saying is that the nervous system continues to develop. The body-signal pathway doesn't close at 7 or 8 or 10. It's always there, waiting for the right input.

Research shows that body signal differences in autism are real, measurable, and persistent, but the capacity for the brain to learn to register body signals exists well beyond early childhood. Nicholson et al. (2019) demonstrated that diminished body awareness doesn't mean the pathway is absent, it means the signal needs reinforcement.

The Body-Signal Learning Layer is designed for exactly this: sustained, gentle feedback that gives a deeply nervous system that picks up signals slowly something it can finally register.

### Block: Gentle Re-Invitation (Section 3)
This my last email unless you tell me to keep going.

So here's what I want to leave with you. One mechanism. One offer. No timeline attached to either.

**The mechanism:** The Body-Signal Learning Layer creates a gentle, sustained "uh-oh" sensation, 30 to 60 seconds of feedback that gives a brain that's been missing the signal time to register: *something happened.* Pull-ups wick that sensation away completely. Regular underwear provides a quick, diffuse signal that a slow to pick up signals brain may not catch. The 3-layer Body-Signal system is built specifically for this: cotton inner layer (gentle, sustained signal), smart absorption (protects without blocking the feedback loop), and a leak-resistant outer barrier.

It's not a cure. It's not a magic fix. It's not going to change who your child is, because there's nothing to change. His autism is part of him. What the Body-Signal Layer does is give his brain *information*, the kind of information a nervous system that's been operating without a key signal can finally use.

**The offer:** We offer a single pair for $34.99, deliberately small, deliberately low-commitment. You can add it alongside whatever you're currently using. Nothing replaces your routine. Nothing asks you to stop using pull-ups or specialty diapers. Just an addition. A quiet experiment with no strings attached.

60 days to try it. Your timeline. Your judgment.

If nothing changes, no pause, no look, no awareness shift, no tolerated-wearing moment, full refund. No questions asked. No pressure to explain why. The guarantee is the only honest response to the parent who's been burned before.

I'm not asking you to hope. I'm asking you to consider a mechanism you may not have encountered before. And to know that the door is open whenever, and if ever, you decide to walk through it.

## 8. Render Notes

### Dark Mode
- Background inversions: #FBF7F1 → #1A1A1A, #FFFFFF → #2D2D2D, #FBF7EB → #2A2A2A
- Text: B.ink → #F5F0E8, B.soft → #9CA3AF
- Mint #DBFFCD → #2A4A3A
- CTA link: stays underlined, color adjusts for contrast
- Sign-off: stays light background (letter-like consistency)

### Responsive
- Breakpoint: 480px
- All font sizes scale down proportionally (body: 16px minimum)
- Verbatim card: full-width padding, no horizontal scroll
- CTA link: minimum 48px touch target height
- Section spacing: compresses to 32px between sections on narrow viewports
- Quotes reflow naturally without breaking attribution lines

### Email Client Compatibility
- **Gmail:** Inline CSS only. CTA underline renders correctly.
- **Outlook:** No hover state on CTA link. Static underline in body gray.
- **Apple Mail:** CTA hover color shift works (200ms ease-in).
- **Mobile clients:** All elements stack single-column. CTA underlined in green.

### Accessibility Notes
- Body text minimum: 16px on mobile (WCAG AA)
- CTA link: minimum 48px touch target height
- Color contrast: B.ink on #FFFFFF = 14.7:1 (exceeds WCAG AAA)
- CTA link B.soft on #FFFFFF = 7.0:1 (WCAG AAA)
- Alt text: Not applicable (no images)
- prefers-reduced-motion: No animations to disable (email is fully static)

### Critical Motion Rule for L3 (Absolute)
- NO countdown timers
- NO progress bars
- NO scrolling marquee text
- NO auto-rotating carousels
- NO hover-reveal panels
- NO parallax scrolling effects
- NO sticky headers
- NO auto-playing videos
- NO animated GIFs
- NO blinking CTA
- NO pulsing buttons
