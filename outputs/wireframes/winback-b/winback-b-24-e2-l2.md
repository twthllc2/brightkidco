**The core finding hasn't changed, but the evidence has deepened.**

Nicholson et al. (2019) established that autistic children show diminished body awareness, the brain doesn't receive the "I need to go" signal the same way. This has been confirmed by a 2025 systematic review of 49 studies (Barmpagiannis & Baldimtsi), making it one of the most robust findings in autism research.

But here's what's less commonly discussed, and what I want to zero in on today:

**Interoception can be trained.**

Hample, Mahler & Amspacher (2020) showed that an 8-week targeted interoception intervention produced measurable improvements in body awareness. Not overnight. Not an instant change. *Trained.* The brain can learn to recognize signals it's been missing, if it has the right input.

That's exactly what the Body-Signal Learning Layer does. It doesn't assume your child can feel the signal. It *creates* the feedback, a sustained 30-60 second "uh-oh" sensation, that gives the brain the information it needs to start recognizing the pattern.

Think of it like this: your BCBA has built the behavioral framework. Scheduled sits. Prompting protocols. Positive reinforcement. That work is not wasted. But those methods all assume your child can *feel* when they need to go. If the interoception signal isn't arriving, the most perfectly executed protocol can't close that gap.

The Body-Signal Layer fills the missing sensory feedback loop.
